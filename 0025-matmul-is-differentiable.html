riktig #25 — matmul is differentiable

Twenty-fifth post documenting the riktig project. Post #24 proved the general theorem; this one collects the corollary that was the point all along — an actual weight matrix, an actual derivative, constant sharp enough to be worth stating.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Every transformer layer that multiplies an activation by a weight matrix needs its derivative for backprop to work. Theory/BilinearDeriv.v proved that any bilinear map is differentiable, generically. Today’s post plugs mat_mul into that theorem — which needed a norm on matrices first, since none existed.

The missing norm

is_deriv needs a way to measure “how big” its inputs and outputs are — that’s what a NormedSpace is for. Nothing in the tree said how big a matrix is. The obvious classical choice, the Frobenius norm ∥A∥F=∑i,jAij2\|A\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} A_{ij}^2}, needs a square root — and this project doesn’t have one yet. Post #20 already ran into the same wall building QR: Gram-Schmidt’s columns stay un-normalized because normalizing needs ⋅\sqrt{\cdot}, deliberately deferred to a later constructive-reals pass.

So: the entrywise L1 norm instead, needing only absolute value, which every OrderedField already has:

∥A∥:=∑i,j|Aij|\|A\| := \sum_{i,j} |A_{ij}|

Simpler than Frobenius, and — this turns out to matter — better behaved under multiplication, as the next section shows.

The sharp bound

To apply bilinear_is_deriv, matmul needs a boundedness witness: some constant CC with ∥AB∥≤C⋅∥A∥⋅∥B∥\|AB\| \le C \cdot \|A\| \cdot \|B\|. The throwaway approach is to prove some CC works, note the dimensions probably show up somewhere, and move on. That’s not what happened here — the proof reaches the sharp constant, C=1C = 1:

Theorem mat_mul_norm_submultiplicative : forall A B,
  ‖ mat_mul A B ‖ <= ‖ A ‖ * ‖ B ‖.

No dimension-dependent slack, no fudge factor. The argument is the textbook one, just carried through in Coq: each entry of ABAB is ∑kAikBkj\sum_k A_{ik} B_{kj}, so |(AB)ij|≤∑k|Aik||Bkj||{(AB)}_{ij}| \le \sum_k |A_{ik}|\,|B_{kj}| by the triangle inequality; sum that over every (i,j)(i,j), swap the order of summation (a Fubini-style rearrangement — sum over kk on the outside instead), and the double sum factors cleanly into (∑i,k|Aik|)(∑k,j|Bkj|)=∥A∥⋅∥B∥\left(\sum_{i,k}|A_{ik}|\right)\left(\sum_{k,j}|B_{kj}|\right) = \|A\|\cdot\|B\|. Six named steps, one genuinely fiddly ingredient (showing a single row’s partial sum is bounded by the whole matrix’s total, since every dropped term is nonnegative) — arithmetic that’s completely routine once written down, but the kind of thing that’s easy to get an off-by-a-dimension bound on if you’re not careful about which sum absorbs which factor.

The corollary that was the actual point

Theorem mat_mul_is_deriv : forall A0 B0,
  is_deriv (fun AB => mat_mul (fst AB) (snd AB)) (A0, B0)
    (mat_mul_deriv_lm A0 B0).

One line, applying post #24’s bilinear_is_deriv to the BilinearMap witness and the C := 1 boundedness proof above. This is the theorem a transformer layer actually reaches for: this specific matrix multiplication, differentiable, everywhere, with the derivative spelled out. Every “multiply activations by weights” layer in Stage B’s eventual attention mechanism inherits its derivative from this single theorem — no per-layer proof needed, exactly the payoff docs/ML- ROADMAP.md staged this work for.

What Stage B still owes

Softmax, ReLU, and layernorm remain untouched — matmul was the piece that needed a genuinely new mathematical ingredient (the norm, the sharp bound); the other three are smaller, more mechanical proofs once picked up. Attention itself — softmax(QKᵀ/√d)V — is a composition of pieces most of which now exist: matmul’s derivative (today), exp (post #22), and deriv_comp (post #12’s chain rule) to chain them. What’s still missing is softmax’s own derivative and the √d scaling, both comparatively small next steps.

Next: softmax and ReLU, the two remaining Stage B layers standing between here and attention, or a return to the determinant — the sign-homomorphism attempt is still narrowing the last gap.
