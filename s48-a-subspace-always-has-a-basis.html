A subspace always has a basis

A linear map between two vector spaces comes with two natural subsets: its kernel, everything it sends to zero, and its image, everything it actually reaches. Both are subspaces — subsets of a vector space that are themselves closed under addition and scaling, and are therefore vector spaces in their own right, using the same two operations as the space they sit inside. A subspace can be any subset with that closure property, not just a kernel or an image — the whole space is a subspace of itself, the single point 0 is a subspace, and so is everything in between.

That raises a basic question, one level below the vector space itself. A basis is a finite list of vectors from which every vector in a space — and no vector outside it — can be built as a combination, in exactly one way; it is what lets you replace “an abstract vector” with “a tuple of numbers” without losing or duplicating any information. Does a subspace always have one? And if the ambient space itself can be described by a basis of some finite size, does the subspace’s own basis need no more vectors than that? Nothing about the definition of “subspace” forces this to be true — a subspace could, in principle, be some subset that resists being pinned down by finitely many of its own vectors, the way a wiggly curve resists being described by finitely many points. This post proves it can’t happen: every subspace of a finite-dimensional vector space has a basis, no bigger than the ambient space’s, built by induction on the ambient space’s dimension.

Making it precise

The codebase’s Basis n F V record is exactly “a finite list of vectors from which every vector can be built as a combination, in exactly one way,” made checkable: it bundles n chosen vectors, a coordinate map sending a vector to its tuple of n scalars, and proofs that reconstructing a vector from its coordinates and reading off the coordinates of a reconstruction both undo each other. Closure under addition and scaling — the property that makes a subset a subspace — is likewise a small record, SubspacePred F V P: a predicate P on vectors, together with proofs that 0 satisfies it and that it is closed under + and scaling. Sub V P is the actual subspace as a type — the vectors satisfying P, paired with their proof of membership — and it inherits a VectorSpace structure automatically, by nothing more than combining the pieces above.

With those three pieces named, the question from the introduction becomes a precise statement: given Bn : Basis n F V and SP : SubspacePred F V P, does Sub V P have a Basis k F (Sub V P) for some k <= n?

The induction, sketched on a blackboard

Stated as one theorem: if V has a basis of size n and P is a subspace of V, then P has a basis of some size k with k <= n. The proof is induction on n, the dimension of the ambient space.

Base case, n=0n=0. A 00-dimensional space has exactly one vector up to ==, namely 0 (that falls straight out of the basis round trip: reconstructing from the empty tuple of coordinates always gives 0). Every subspace of a one-element space is trivial, so it has the empty basis — dimension 0≤00 \le 0.

Step, n=m+1n = m+1. Fix a basis b0,…,bmb_0,\dots,b_m of VV. Reading off the coordinate of b0b_0 is a linear map c1:V→Fc_1 : V \to F, and its kernel H={v:c1(v)=0}H = \{v : c_1(v) = 0\} is a hyperplane — a subspace with an explicit basis of dimension mm, namely b1,…,bmb_1,\dots,b_m with their first coordinate simply dropped. By the induction hypothesis, P∩HP \cap H — a subspace of HH — already has a basis, of some size k0≤mk_0 \le m. Now split into two cases, classically:

  • Some v0∈Pv_0 \in P has c1(v0)≠0c_1(v_0) \neq 0. Rescale it: v1=c1(v0)−1⋅v0v_1 = c_1(v_0)^{-1}\cdot v_0, still in PP (subspaces are closed under scaling), now with c1(v1)=1c_1(v_1) = 1 exactly. Prepending v1v_1 to the basis of P∩HP \cap H gives a basis of PP itself, of size k0+1≤m+1k_0 + 1 \le m + 1 — the new vector supplies the one dimension the hyperplane basis was missing.
  • No such vector exists, i.e. every v∈Pv \in P has c1(v)=0c_1(v) = 0. Then PP already sits entirely inside HH, so PP and P∩HP \cap H are the very same subset — pointwise equal, just phrased two different ways — and the basis already built for P∩HP \cap H is a basis of PP, unchanged, of size k0≤m≤m+1k_0 \le m \le m+1.

Either way the dimension does not exceed n=m+1n = m+1, which closes the induction.

The hyperplane, in Coq

The kernel-of-the-first-coordinate construction from the sketch is a few lines. eval_lm evaluates a coordinate function at one fixed index and is linear (a short fact proved earlier in the file); composing it with basis_coords Bn gives the linear map that reads off a vector’s first coordinate, and its kernel is the hyperplane:

Section Hyperplane.
  Context {F V : Type} {FF : Field F} {VV : VectorSpace F V}.
  Context {m : nat} (Bn : Basis (S m) F V).

  Definition c1 : LinearMap F V F := lm_comp (eval_lm Fin.F1) (basis_coords Bn).

  Definition H (v : V) : Prop := ker_pred c1 v.

  #[global] Instance H_SubspacePred : SubspacePred F V H := Ker_SubspacePred c1.

H being a subspace needs no separate proof: it is literally a kernel, and every kernel is a subspace, the fact established for kernels and images in general. The file goes on to prove, by the same drop-the-first-coordinate idea as the blackboard sketch, that the tail basis vectors give an actual Basis m of H:

Definition hyperplane_basis : Basis m F (Sub V H) :=
  @Build_Basis m F (Sub V H) FF (Sub_VectorSpace H)
    hp_basis_vec hp_coords hp_recon hp_coords_lincomb.

(hp_basis_vec, hp_coords, and their two round-trip proofs hp_recon/hp_coords_lincomb follow the same delta-collapse argument as building the standard basis vector, applied to a dropped coordinate rather than a coordinate function on the nose. The part of the file worth walking in detail is the induction that uses them, below.)

The mirror move — turning a basis of a kernel plus one vector with phi w1 == 1 into a basis one dimension bigger — is built the same way, generically, for any linear functional phi:

Definition eb_vec (i : Fin.t (S k)) : W :=
  Fin.caseS' i (fun _ => W) w1 (fun i' => proj1_sig (basis_vec BHW i')).

and, after the round-trip proofs for the extended coordinate map (a few hundred lines of the same delta-collapse bookkeeping), the assembled basis:

Definition extend_basis_by_vector : Basis (S k) F W :=
  @Build_Basis (S k) F W FF VW eb_vec eb_coords eb_recon eb_coords_lincomb.

eb_vec is exactly “prepend w1, keep the old basis vectors after it” — position 0 is w1, position i+1 is the i-th old basis vector. This is the general form of the “rescale and prepend” step from the sketch’s first case.

The main theorem

Here is the whole induction, exactly as it appears in the file, proof included, nothing elided:

Theorem subspace_basis_exists : forall (n : nat) (F V : Type) (FF : Field F)
    (VV : VectorSpace F V) (Bn : Basis n F V) (P : V -> Prop)
    (SP : SubspacePred F V P),
  exists k : nat, Nat.le k n /\ exists Bk : Basis k F (Sub V P), True.
Proof.
  induction n as [| m IH]; intros F V FF VV Bn P SP.
  - exists (0 : nat). split.
    + exact (le_n 0).
    + exists (trivial_basis Bn P). exact I.
  - destruct (IH F (Sub V (H Bn)) FF _ (hyperplane_basis Bn) (Pinner (H Bn) P)
                 (SubspacePred_pullback (H Bn) P))
      as [k0 [Hk0 [Bk0 _]]].
    pose (BPw := basis_and_collapse (H Bn) P k0 Bk0).
    destruct (classic (exists v0 : V, P v0 /\ c1 Bn v0 =/= 0)) as [Hex | Hnex].
    + destruct (constructive_indefinite_description _ Hex) as [v0 [Hv0P Hv0ne]].
      pose (v1 := inv (c1 Bn v0) *: v0).
      pose proof (sp_scale (inv (c1 Bn v0)) v0 Hv0P) as Hv1P.
      assert (Hv1_1 : c1 Bn v1 == 1).
      { unfold v1.
        transitivity (inv (c1 Bn v0) * c1 Bn v0).
        - exact (lm_scale (c1 Bn) (inv (c1 Bn v0)) v0).
        - exact (field_invl (c1 Bn v0) Hv0ne). }
      pose (w1 := exist P v1 Hv1P : Sub V P).
      pose (phi := lm_comp (c1 Bn) (sub_inject P)).
      pose proof Hv1_1 as Hphiw1.
      pose (BPw' := basis_and_comm (H Bn) P k0 BPw).
      pose (BkPH := basis_and_expand P (H Bn) k0 BPw').
      assert (Hchar : forall w : Sub V P, Pinner P (H Bn) w <-> phi w == 0)
        by (intros w; unfold Pinner, H, ker_pred, phi, lm_comp; simpl; reflexivity).
      exists (S k0). split.
      * exact (le_n_S k0 m Hk0).
      * exists (extend_basis_by_vector phi w1 Hphiw1 (Pinner P (H Bn)) Hchar BkPH). exact I.
    + assert (Hiff : forall v : V, Pand (H Bn) P v <-> P v).
      { intros v. unfold Pand, H, ker_pred. split.
        - intros [_ HPv]. exact HPv.
        - intros HPv.
          split.
          + destruct (classic (c1 Bn v == 0)) as [Hz | Hnz].
            * exact Hz.
            * exfalso. apply Hnex. exists v. exact (conj HPv Hnz).
          + exact HPv.
      }
      exists k0. split.
      * exact (le_S k0 m Hk0).
      * exists (basis_pred_iff (Pand (H Bn) P) P Hiff k0 BPw). exact I.
Qed.

Walking it against the sketch:

  • induction n as [| m IH] is the induction on the ambient dimension; IH is the theorem one dimension down.
  • The base case supplies 0 and trivial_basis Bn P — the empty basis of a subspace of a one-element space — with le_n 0 for 0 <= 0.
  • In the step, destruct (IH ...) is the induction hypothesis applied not to V but to H Bn (the hyperplane), using hyperplane_basis as its size-m basis, and to Pinner (H Bn) P — “P, viewed as a predicate on H” — as the subspace. This is P∩HP \cap H from the sketch, packaged as a subspace of H rather than of V, and it hands back Bk0, a basis of size k0 <= m.
  • basis_and_collapse repackages Bk0 from “a basis of a subspace of a subspace” into BPw, a basis of Pand (H Bn) P — literally H /\ P — read directly as a subspace of V. This is the bookkeeping needed to compare vectors of P ∩ H against vectors of V on equal footing; it changes no dimension, only the packaging.
  • destruct (classic (exists v0, ...)) is the classical case split from the sketch, Hex/Hnex.
  • Hex branch. constructive_indefinite_description extracts an actual v0 (not just the knowledge that one exists). v1 is the rescaled vector c1(v0)−1⋅v0c_1(v_0)^{-1}\cdot v_0; Hv1_1 proves c1(v1)=1c_1(v_1) = 1, by lm_scale (pulling the scalar through the linear map c1) followed by field_invl (the field law a−1⋅a=1a^{-1}\cdot a = 1 for a≠0a \neq 0). w1 packages v1 together with its proof of membership in P, and phi is c1 restricted to Sub V P. basis_and_comm and basis_and_expand are more repackaging — turning BPw (a basis of P ∩ H inside V) into BkPH, a basis of “the subset of P where phi vanishes”, which Hchar confirms is the same predicate as Pinner (H Bn) P, just seen from inside Sub V P. extend_basis_by_vector then prepends w1 to BkPH, exactly the “rescale and prepend” move, giving a basis of size S k0, bounded by le_n_S : k0 <= m -> S k0 <= S m.
  • Hnex branch. Hiff proves P and Pand (H Bn) P agree pointwise: any v in P either already has c1 v == 0 (so it is in H too), or — since Hnex rules out any counterexample — it must have c1 v == 0, again by the classical case split, this time on a single equation rather than an existential. basis_pred_iff transports BPw across that pointwise agreement, unchanged in size, giving a basis of P directly of size k0, bounded by le_S : k0 <= m -> k0 <= S m.

Why two axioms, and why they are unavoidable here

subspace_basis_exists compiles against two axioms beyond Coq’s core logic — checking Print Assumptions on it shows exactly these two and nothing else:

Axioms:
IndefiniteDescription.constructive_indefinite_description :
  forall (A : Type) (P : A -> Prop), (exists x : A, P x) -> {x : A | P x}
Classical_Prop.classic : forall P : Prop, P \/ ~ P

Both are needed at the same spot in the proof — the case split on exists v0 : V, P v0 /\ c1 Bn v0 =/= 0 — for two different reasons.

Classical_Prop.classic decides the split itself: is there such a v0, or isn’t there? Coq’s logic is constructive by default, so P \/ ~P is not available for an arbitrary proposition without a procedure that actually determines which side holds. Here the proposition is about an abstract field F — the codebase’s Field class assumes none of the algebraic laws come with a decision procedure for equality — so there is no way to compute, for an arbitrary vector v0, whether c1 v0 equals zero or not. Excluded middle sidesteps that: it simply asserts every proposition is true or false, letting the proof branch without ever needing to compute which branch it is in.

IndefiniteDescription.constructive_indefinite_description is needed for a different reason, once Hex — the existence of such a v0 — is in hand. Knowing a v0 exists is not the same as having one: Hex lives in Prop, and Coq will not let a bare destruct of a Prop-sorted existential produce data used to build something outside Prop — which is exactly what happens next, since v0 becomes v1, which becomes w1, which becomes an actual entry in the basis-vector list of the Basis record being constructed. The witness has to be extracted as real data, not just used to derive a further fact, and that extraction is what this axiom supplies.

Both are standard and consistent with Coq’s logic — neither is full unrestricted choice, and nothing here relies on proof irrelevance.

Using it: what it closes, and what it doesn’t

Theory/KerIm.v — the file that defines kernel and image as subspaces — names the theorem this post proves before proving it, under the name subspace_has_basis_statement:

Definition subspace_has_basis_statement : Prop :=
  forall (F V : Type) (FF : Field F) (VV : VectorSpace F V) (n : nat)
    (Bn : Basis n F V) (P : V -> Prop) (SP : SubspacePred F V P),
    exists k : nat, k <= n /\ exists Bk : Basis k F (Sub V P), True.

subspace_basis_exists discharges it in one line, by direct application:

Theorem subspace_has_basis : subspace_has_basis_statement.
Proof.
  unfold subspace_has_basis_statement.
  intros F V FF VV n Bn P SP.
  exact (subspace_basis_exists n F V FF VV Bn P SP).
Qed.

That name is chosen to match the gap Theory/KerIm.v left open: a linear map’s domain dimension splits between what it forgets and what it reaches, dim U == dim (ker f) + dim (im f), but that statement cannot even be written down against a chosen basis of Ker f and Im f until you know those subspaces have bases at all. Theory/ KerIm.v states the target one existential level up, so it type-checks and names the goal precisely:

Definition rank_nullity_statement : Prop :=
  forall (F U V : Type) (FF : Field F)
    (VU : VectorSpace F U) (VV : VectorSpace F V)
    (f : LinearMap F U V) (n : nat) (Bn : Basis n F U)
    (k : nat) (Bk : Basis k F (Ker f)) (r : nat) (Br : Basis r F (Im f)),
    n = Nat.add k r.

This is still not proved, and subspace_has_basis does not close the gap by itself. It only says some basis of Ker f exists, of some size k <= n, and likewise for Im f; it says nothing about every basis having the same size. rank_nullity_statement is stated for arbitrary given bases Bk and Br, so proving it needs one more fact this codebase does not yet have: that any two bases of the same vector space have equal size. That is a different half of the exchange argument — showing two bases match in size, rather than extending a smaller basis to a bigger space — and it remains unproved.

One more thing worth being precise about: the proof of subspace_basis_exists runs through two classical axioms, so it is not computational the way earlier posts’ proofs were. There is no numeric example to run and watch reduce to a value — a Compute on a basis produced by subspace_basis_exists would simply get stuck at classic and constructive_indefinite_description, which carry no computational content, only the guarantee that assuming them stays consistent with Coq’s logic. What the theorem supplies is existence, checked by the type checker, not a program that computes a witness. What it does buy directly is exactly the one-line proof above: subspace_has_basis, applicable to any field, any finite-dimensional vector space, and any subspace of it, with no further work at each use site.
