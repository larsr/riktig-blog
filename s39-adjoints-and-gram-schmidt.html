Adjoints, Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, and isometries

An inner product on a vector space over a field FF pairs two vectors and returns a scalar, written ⟨u,v⟩\langle u , v\rangle. It is symmetric (⟨u,v⟩=⟨v,u⟩\langle u,v\rangle = \langle v,u\rangle), additive in each slot (⟨u+u′,v⟩=⟨u,v⟩+⟨u′,v⟩\langle u+u',v\rangle = \langle u,v\rangle + \langle u',v\rangle), and pulls scalars out of each slot (⟨a⋅u,v⟩=a⟨u,v⟩\langle a \cdot u,v\rangle = a\,\langle u,v\rangle). As before, == is a setoid equality: a chosen equivalence relation every operation must respect, not Coq’s equality of raw terms. A linear map ff between vector spaces respects the same two operations — f(u+u′)=f(u)+f(u′)f(u+u') = f(u) + f(u') and f(a⋅u)=a⋅f(u)f(a \cdot u) = a \cdot f(u).

This post is about two things you can do once an inner product and linear maps are both in hand: move a linear map to the other side of the pairing (its adjoint), and turn any list of vectors into an orthogonal one (Gram-Schmidt).

The adjoint moves a map across the pairing

Given a linear map f:V→Vf : V \to V, ask: is there another map gg that, whenever you pair fuf\,u against ww, gives the same scalar as pairing uu against gwg\,w? That is, ⟨fu,w⟩=⟨u,gw⟩\langle f\,u , w\rangle = \langle u , g\,w \rangle for all uu and ww. Such a gg is an adjoint of ff — it lets you slide ff off the left slot and onto the right. The definition names exactly this relation:

  Definition is_adjoint (f g : LinearMap F V V) : Prop :=
    forall u w : V, ⟨ f u , w ⟩ == ⟨ u , g w ⟩.

Nothing here says such a gg exists. Over an infinite-dimensional space it may not, and even in finite dimension building one goes through a basis and a matrix transpose. So the file proves nothing about existence. What it does prove is that an adjoint, if one exists, is determined by ff with no freedom left — plus the algebra of how adjoints interact with the identity map and with composition.

Uniqueness: two adjoints agree everywhere

The claim, in one sentence: if g1g_1 and g2g_2 are both adjoints of the same ff, then g1u=g2ug_1\,u = g_2\,u for every uu.

The blackboard argument runs through the difference. To show two vectors are equal it is enough to show their difference is zero, and to show a vector xx is zero it is enough — this is where positivity of the inner product earns its keep — to show ⟨w,x⟩=0\langle w , x\rangle = 0 for every ww (call this nondegeneracy: only the zero vector is orthogonal to everything). Applied to x=g1u−g2ux = g_1\,u - g_2\,u:

⟨w,g1u−g2u⟩=⟨w,g1u⟩−⟨w,g2u⟩=⟨g1u,w⟩−⟨g2u,w⟩=⟨u,fw⟩−⟨u,fw⟩=0.\langle w , g_1 u - g_2 u\rangle = \langle w , g_1 u\rangle - \langle w , g_2 u\rangle = \langle g_1 u , w\rangle - \langle g_2 u , w\rangle = \langle u , f w\rangle - \langle u , f w\rangle = 0.

The two middle scalars are equal because both g1g_1 and g2g_2 are adjoints of ff — each rewrites ⟨giu,w⟩\langle g_i\,u , w\rangle to the same ⟨u,fw⟩\langle u , f\,w\rangle. In the file, the statement over is_adjoint is a one-line restatement:

  Theorem is_adjoint_uniqueness : forall f g1 g2 : LinearMap F V V,
    is_adjoint g1 f -> is_adjoint g2 f -> forall u : V, g1 u == g2 u.
  Proof.
    intros f g1 g2 Hg1 Hg2 u.
    exact (adjoint_unique f g1 g2 Hg1 Hg2 u).
  Qed.

The real work sits in adjoint_unique, which carries the argument above verbatim:

  Theorem adjoint_unique : forall f g1 g2 : LinearMap F V V,
    (forall u w : V, ⟨ lm_fun g1 u , w ⟩ == ⟨ u , lm_fun f w ⟩) ->
    (forall u w : V, ⟨ lm_fun g2 u , w ⟩ == ⟨ u , lm_fun f w ⟩) ->
    forall u : V, lm_fun g1 u == lm_fun g2 u.
  Proof.
    intros f g1 g2 Hg1 Hg2 u.
    apply (sub_eq_zero (lm_fun g1 u) (lm_fun g2 u)).
    apply inner_nondegenerate.
    intros w.
    rewrite (ip_add_r w (lm_fun g1 u) (- lm_fun g2 u)),
      (ip_neg_r w (lm_fun g2 u)), (ip_sym w (lm_fun g1 u)),
      (ip_sym w (lm_fun g2 u)), (Hg1 u w), (Hg2 u w).
    apply invr.
  Qed.

Reading the proof:

  • sub_eq_zero (g1 u) (g2 u) turns the goal g1u=g2ug_1 u = g_2 u into g1u+−g2u=0g_1 u + {-}\,g_2 u = 0 — the “show the difference is zero” step.
  • inner_nondegenerate reduces that to: for every ww, ⟨w,g1u+−g2u⟩=0\langle w , g_1 u + {-}\,g_2 u\rangle = 0. This is the one step that uses positivity; it holds because a positive-definite pairing has no nonzero vector orthogonal to all of VV.
  • ip_add_r splits the right slot: ⟨w,g1u⟩+⟨w,−g2u⟩\langle w , g_1 u\rangle + \langle w , {-}\,g_2 u\rangle. ip_neg_r turns the second pairing into −⟨w,g2u⟩-\langle w , g_2 u\rangle.
  • The two ip_sym rewrites flip each pairing to put giug_i\,u on the left, so the adjoint hypotheses can fire.
  • Hg1 u w and Hg2 u w are those hypotheses: each rewrites ⟨giu,w⟩\langle g_i\,u , w\rangle to the common ⟨u,fw⟩\langle u , f\,w\rangle. The goal is now ⟨u,fw⟩+−⟨u,fw⟩=0\langle u , f w\rangle + {-}\langle u , f w\rangle = 0, which is invr — a scalar plus its negation is zero.

Composition flips the order: (f2∘f1)*=f1*∘f2*(f_2 \circ f_1)^* = f_1^{*} \circ f_2^{*}

The next fact is the one every reader who has met matrix transposes will expect: the adjoint of a composite is the composite of the adjoints, in the reversed order. Write g1g_1 for an adjoint of f1f_1 and g2g_2 for an adjoint of f2f_2. Then g1∘g2g_1 \circ g_2 is an adjoint of f2∘f1f_2 \circ f_1. Note the flip: f2f_2 acts last in the composite but its adjoint g2g_2 acts first in the adjoint composite.

The blackboard argument slides one map across at a time. Writing the composite f2∘f1f_2 \circ f_1 as “apply f1f_1, then f2f_2”:

⟨f2(f1u),w⟩=⟨f1u,g2w⟩=⟨u,g1(g2w)⟩.\langle f_2(f_1 u) , w\rangle = \langle f_1 u , g_2 w\rangle = \langle u , g_1(g_2 w)\rangle.

The first equality is g2g_2 being an adjoint of f2f_2 (applied at the vector f1uf_1 u); the second is g1g_1 being an adjoint of f1f_1 (applied against g2wg_2 w). The left end is ⟨(f2∘f1)u,w⟩\langle (f_2\circ f_1)\,u , w\rangle and the right end is ⟨u,(g1∘g2)w⟩\langle u , (g_1\circ g_2)\,w\rangle, which is the adjoint relation for the two composites. In Coq, lm_comp f2 f1 is the map that applies f1 first and then f2:

  Theorem is_adjoint_comp : forall f1 g1 f2 g2 : LinearMap F V V,
    is_adjoint f1 g1 -> is_adjoint f2 g2 ->
    is_adjoint (lm_comp f2 f1) (lm_comp g1 g2).
  Proof.
    intros f1 g1 f2 g2 Hfg1 Hfg2 u w.
    calc (⟨ f2 (f1 u) , w ⟩)
      == (⟨ f1 u , g2 w ⟩)      { (Hfg2 (f1 u) w) }
      == (⟨ u , g1 (g2 w) ⟩)    { (Hfg1 u (g2 w)) }.
  Qed.

Reading it: after intros, the goal is ⟨f2(f1u),w⟩=⟨u,g1(g2w)⟩\langle f_2(f_1 u) , w\rangle = \langle u , g_1(g_2 w)\rangle — the two lm_comps have unfolded by conversion, since composition of linear maps is function composition. The calc block is the blackboard chain transcribed: the first step is exactly Hfg2 (the adjoint relation for f2f_2) applied at the point f1uf_1 u; the second is Hfg1 applied against g2wg_2 w. Two steps, one per slide.

Gram-Schmidt: orthogonalize by subtracting projections

An inner product gives orthogonality: uu and vv are orthogonal when ⟨u,v⟩=0\langle u , v\rangle = 0. Given a list of vectors, we would like a new list spanning the same space but with every pair orthogonal. The standard recipe processes the input left to right, and for each new vector subtracts off its projection onto every vector already produced, leaving only the part pointing in a genuinely new direction. The projection of vv onto the axis through uu is

Definition proj {F V : Type} `{InnerProduct F V} (v u : V) : V :=
  (⟨ u , v ⟩ * inv (norm2 v)) *: v.

— the scalar ⟨u,v⟩/‖v‖2\langle u,v\rangle / \lVert v\rVert^2 times vv, where ‖v‖2=⟨v,v⟩\lVert v\rVert^2 = \langle v,v\rangle. The inner loop subtracts one projection per already-processed vector:

  Fixpoint gs_aux (done : list V) (v : V) : V :=
    match done with
    | nil => v
    | cons d done' => gs_aux done' (v + - proj d v)
    end.

and the outer loop folds that over the input, growing an accumulator acc of finished vectors, gs_aux-ing each new input against everything produced so far:

  Fixpoint gs_step (acc vs : list V) : list V :=
    match vs with
    | nil => acc
    | cons v vs' => gs_step (acc ++ (gs_aux acc v :: nil)) vs'
    end.

  Definition gs (vs : list V) : list V := gs_step nil vs.

The result, with its hypotheses in plain sight

The claim: any two distinct vectors in the output gs vs are orthogonal —

d1,d2∈gs(vs),d1≠d2⟹⟨d1,d2⟩=0.d_1, d_2 \in \mathrm{gs}(vs),\quad d_1 \neq d_2 \;\implies\; \langle d_1 , d_2 \rangle = 0.

But there is a genuine hypothesis that cannot be swept aside. The projection of uu onto vv divides by ‖v‖2\lVert v\rVert^2, which is only meaningful when that squared length is nonzero. So the theorem is conditional: it assumes every vector the construction produces has nonzero squared norm — which is the constructive reading of “the input family was linearly independent.” That hypothesis is stated explicitly, on the final output, not hidden:

  Theorem gs_pairwise_orthogonal : forall vs : list V,
    (forall d : V, In d (gs vs) -> norm2 d =/= 0) ->
    forall d1 d2 : V,
      In d1 (gs vs) -> In d2 (gs vs) -> d1 <> d2 -> orthogonal d1 d2.
  Proof.
    intros vs Hnz d1 d2 Hd1 Hd2 Hneq.
    destruct (gs_step_pairwise vs nil pairwise_orth_nil nonzero_nil Hnz
                d1 d2 Hd1 Hd2) as [Ho | Heq].
    - exact Ho.
    - destruct (Hneq Heq).
  Qed.

Reading it: the whole force is in gs_step_pairwise, the loop invariant, run from the empty seed. That invariant is carried in a slightly different shape from the advertised one — it produces, for any two output vectors, orthogonal d1 d2 \/ d1 = d2 (orthogonal or literally equal) rather than the “distinct implies orthogonal” phrasing. The two are classically equivalent, but converting between them needs a decision on whether two vectors are equal, which the disjunctive form sidesteps; it is the form the induction naturally produces and consumes. So here, at the very end, that disjunction is unpacked: gs_step_pairwise hands back Ho : orthogonal d1 d2 or Heq : d1 = d2. The first case is the goal. The second contradicts the hypothesis Hneq : d1 <> d2, so Hneq Heq produces a proof of False, and destruct on it closes the goal. The invariant’s three seed obligations — the empty list is pairwise orthogonal (pairwise_orth_nil), the empty list has no nonzero-norm obligation to discharge (nonzero_nil), and the output stays nonzero (Hnz, the reader’s hypothesis) — are exactly what gs_step_pairwise needs, and no more.

The engine underneath is a single geometric fact, proved separately in the file: subtracting the projection of vv onto dd leaves a residual orthogonal to dd, and — crucially for a loop that revisits earlier axes — projecting a vector that is already orthogonal to an axis onto that axis gives zero, so a repeated subtraction is a harmless no-op. That is what lets each new vector be made orthogonal to every earlier one without disturbing the orthogonalities already established.

An isometry preserves the whole inner product, not merely its own norm

Picture a transformation of a vector space that leaves every vector’s length exactly as it was — nothing stretches, nothing shrinks. Does that also pin down how any two different vectors stand with respect to each other afterward — the angle between them, how far one leans onto another along a shared direction — or could such a transformation, while leaving every individual length untouched, still scramble those relationships? Length is a fact about one vector at a time. The relationship between two vectors is a fact about a pair, and fixing every one-vector fact does not, on its face, force every two-vector fact to come along with it.

A transformation with the length-preserving property just described is called an isometry, and the codebase states it directly:

  Definition is_isometry (f : LinearMap F V V) : Prop :=
    forall u : V, norm2 (f u) == norm2 u.

Whether the two-vector fact, ⟨fu,fv⟩=⟨u,v⟩\langle f\,u , f\,v\rangle = \langle u,v\rangle for u≠vu \ne v, survives anyway is the question the rest of this section answers, and the answer needs its own proof: it is not read off the definition for free.

The claim, in one sentence: if ff is an isometry, then ⟨fu,fv⟩=⟨u,v⟩\langle f\,u , f\,v\rangle = \langle u , v\rangle for every pair u,vu, v, not only when u=vu = v.

The blackboard argument. Expanding the norm-squared of a sum uses one algebraic fact already on hand: ‖u+v‖2=‖u‖2+‖v‖2+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩)\lVert u+v\rVert^2 = \lVert u\rVert^2 + \lVert v\rVert^2 + (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle). Apply it on both sides of ‖fu+fv‖2=‖u+v‖2\lVert f\,u + f\,v\rVert^2 = \lVert u+v\rVert^2 — which holds because ff is linear, so fu+fv=f(u+v)f\,u + f\,v = f(u+v), and ff is an isometry at the point u+vu+v:

(‖fu‖2+‖fv‖2)+(⟨fu,fv⟩+⟨fv,fu⟩)=(‖u‖2+‖v‖2)+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩).(\lVert fu\rVert^2 + \lVert fv\rVert^2) + (\langle fu,fv\rangle + \langle fv,fu\rangle) = (\lVert u\rVert^2 + \lVert v\rVert^2) + (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle).

ff is an isometry at uu and at vv individually too, so ‖fu‖2=‖u‖2\lVert fu\rVert^2 = \lVert u\rVert^2 and ‖fv‖2=‖v‖2\lVert fv\rVert^2 = \lVert v\rVert^2 — the first parenthesised sum is the same quantity on each side, and cancels:

⟨fu,fv⟩+⟨fv,fu⟩=⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩.\langle fu,fv\rangle + \langle fv,fu\rangle = \langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle.

The inner product is symmetric, so each side is really a value added to itself:

⟨fu,fv⟩+⟨fu,fv⟩=⟨u,v⟩+⟨u,v⟩,\langle fu,fv\rangle + \langle fu,fv\rangle = \langle u,v\rangle + \langle u,v\rangle,

and halving both sides — the one step that needs an invertible 22, hence an ordered field’s positivity, not merely symmetry of the inner product — gives exactly ⟨fu,fv⟩=⟨u,v⟩\langle fu,fv\rangle = \langle u,v\rangle.

The halving step is its own small lemma:

  Lemma double_inj : forall a b : F, a + a == b + b -> a == b.
  Proof.
    intros a b Hab.
    apply (mul_cancel_l two a b two_neq_0).
    rewrite (field_mul_comm two a), (field_mul_comm two b),
      (mul_two a), (mul_two b).
    exact Hab.
  Qed.

and the full theorem strings the two together:

  Theorem isometry_preserves_inner : forall f : LinearMap F V V,
    is_isometry f -> forall u v : V, ⟨ f u , f v ⟩ == ⟨ u , v ⟩.
  Proof.
    intros f Hiso u v.
    assert (Hfuv : norm2 (f u + f v) == norm2 (u + v)).
    { rewrite <- (lm_add f u v). exact (Hiso (u + v)). }
    rewrite (norm2_add (f u) (f v)), (norm2_add u v),
      (Hiso u), (Hiso v) in Hfuv.
    apply (cancel_l (norm2 u + norm2 v)) in Hfuv.
    rewrite (ip_sym (f v) (f u)), (ip_sym v u) in Hfuv.
    exact (double_inj (⟨ f u , f v ⟩) (⟨ u , v ⟩) Hfuv).
  Qed.

Reading it:

  • rewrite <- (lm_add f u v) turns the wanted fact ‖fu+fv‖2=‖u+v‖2\lVert f u + f v\rVert^2 = \lVert u+v\rVert^2 into ‖f(u+v)‖2=‖u+v‖2\lVert f(u+v)\rVert^2 = \lVert u+v\rVert^2 by linearity, which Hiso (u + v) — the isometry hypothesis at the point u+vu+v — proves directly.
  • The two norm2_add rewrites expand both sides of Hfuv into the four-term sums from the blackboard chain; the two Hiso rewrites replace ‖fu‖2\lVert fu\rVert^2 and ‖fv‖2\lVert fv\rVert^2 by ‖u‖2\lVert u\rVert^2 and ‖v‖2\lVert v\rVert^2.
  • apply (cancel_l (norm2 u + norm2 v)) strips the now-shared leading summand from both sides of Hfuv, leaving exactly the cross-term equation ⟨fu,fv⟩+⟨fv,fu⟩==⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩\langle fu,fv\rangle + \langle fv,fu\rangle == \langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle.
  • The two ip_sym rewrites turn ⟨fv,fu⟩\langle fv,fu\rangle into ⟨fu,fv⟩\langle fu,fv\rangle and ⟨v,u⟩\langle v,u\rangle into ⟨u,v⟩\langle u,v\rangle, landing on the doubled form double_inj expects.
  • double_inj is the halving step: it cancels the shared factor of 22 (via mul_cancel_l and two_neq_0, the one place positivity is used) to strip the doubling and deliver the theorem exactly.

No rotation or reflection is instantiated as a concrete LinearMap in the codebase yet, so there is no ready-made Example to run this theorem on at a genuinely pair-scrambling candidate. The one witness on hand is the identity map: lm_id u unfolds to u, so is_isometry lm_id holds by reflexivity, and applying isometry_preserves_inner to it says that the identity preserves every inner product — true, if unsurprising, since the identity is the transformation that moves nothing to begin with.
