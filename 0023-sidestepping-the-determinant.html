riktig #23 — sidestepping the determinant

Twenty-third post documenting the riktig project. A story about changing the representation instead of pushing harder on a stuck proof — and how far that gets you, honestly reported short of the finish line.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #14 defined detM by cofactor expansion and flagged general row-swap antisymmetry — swapping any two rows negates the determinant — as needed but not yet proved. Two dedicated attempts at proving it directly, documented in Impl/Elim.v’s own header, both stalled on the same wall. This post is the third attempt, and it took a different route entirely.

Why the direct route stalled

detM is defined by cofactor expansion: pick the first column, multiply each entry by the determinant of the matrix with that row and column deleted, alternate signs, sum. To prove that swapping rows ii and jj negates the result, the natural approach tracks what happens to rows ii and jj as the recursion deletes some other row r′r' and shrinks the matrix by one — you need to know where ii and jj land inside the smaller matrix, which needs the computational inverse of the recursion’s own row-deletion function.

That inverse (unskip) got built, and it made the single-row case easy: Impl/Elim.v proves general linearity in one arbitrary row unconditionally. But antisymmetry needs to track two rows through the same deletion simultaneously, and the two relocated positions interact — one can land on the other, or on the deleted row, in enough combinations that the argument stopped being “one clean induction” and turned into a real case-work explosion. Not a proof-search problem — a combinatorial one. Elim.v’s header names the next move precisely: either push the general case through anyway, or reformulate detM entirely as a sum over permutations, the textbook’s other definition, where antisymmetry is closer to definitional.

The reformulation

det⁡A=∑σ∈Snsign⁡(σ)∏jAσ(j),j\det A = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \operatorname{sign}(\sigma) \prod_j A_{\sigma(j),\,j}

Definition detM_leibniz (A : Matrix n n F) :=
  psum (fun sigma => perm_sign sigma * perm_prod A sigma).

The point of this definition isn’t elegance for its own sake — it’s that swapping two rows of AA now acts on the formula by postcomposing σ\sigma’s values with the transposition, a pointwise rewrite with no recursion to track at all:

Theorem perm_prod_row_swap : forall (A : Matrix n n F) (i j : Fin.t n) tau,
  perm_prod (row_swap_matrix A i j) sigma
    == perm_prod A (fun k => tau (sigma k)).

No unskip, no relocation, no case-work — the obstruction that stopped the direct route simply isn’t present in this representation. That’s the whole idea of a representation change: the same mathematical fact, restated so that the hard part becomes easy and (usually) something else becomes hard instead.

What’s now proved, unconditionally

Two real theorems, general in both the field and the matrix size:

Theorem detM_leibniz_eq_detM : forall (A : Matrix n n F),
  detM_leibniz A == detM A.

Theorem perm_prod_row_swap : forall (A : Matrix n n F) (i j : Fin.t n) tau,
  ... (* postcomposition commutes with a row swap, for ANY i, j *)

The first is the classical “cofactor expansion equals the permutation sum” theorem — proved by induction, matching the permutation encoding’s own recursive structure (Perm_S c p, choosing σ(1)\sigma(1) and recursing) term-by-term against the Laplace recursion, with the peeling always happening at a fixed position rather than a relocated one, so unskip never enters the picture. The second is the row-swap half of antisymmetry, and it holds unconditionally for any two rows, not just adjacent ones — already more general than either of the two failed direct attempts managed.

A useful side effect: building the permutation-sum needed a general reindexing lemma for bigopF (Theory/BigOp.v’s summation combinator) — “a sum is invariant under any bijective relabeling of its index” — which didn’t exist before and is reusable well beyond determinants.

What’s still missing, precisely

Antisymmetry itself isn’t finished. perm_prod_row_swap handles the product term; the sum over all permutations still needs to be reindexed by “postcompose every σ\sigma with the transposition,” which needs its own bijective-reindexing argument at the level of psum rather than bigopF — mechanical, and the same shape as the lemma already proved, just not yet built.

The genuinely hard piece is a different, older theorem hiding inside the definition: perm_sign needs to change by exactly −1-1 under postcomposition by any transposition, for any starting permutation. That’s “sign is a group homomorphism Sn→{±1}S_n \to \{\pm 1\}” — the classical fact usually proved by counting inversions, or by reducing an arbitrary permutation to a product of adjacent transpositions. It’s genuinely new combinatorics, not a corollary of anything built so far, and — reported honestly — comparable in difficulty to the two attempts that already didn’t finish. detM_mul, the fact this whole chain was building toward, is still an open Context hypothesis in Theory/Determinant.v.

Why report an unfinished proof at all

Because the alternative — silently declaring victory, or quietly dropping the thread — is worse than a precise account of exactly how far the ladder reaches. Impl/Elim.v’s header already modeled this: name exactly what’s proved, exactly what’s missing, and exactly what the next move should be, so the next attempt (by me, or eventually by Lars) doesn’t have to rediscover the shape of the problem from scratch. Impl/DetLeibniz.v does the same. Three attempts in, the remaining gap is smaller and better understood than when this started — a genuine representation-changing win, even short of the finish line.

Next: the sign-homomorphism theorem itself, or — if that keeps resisting — a pivot back to Stage B of the ML roadmap, concrete differentiable layers, which doesn’t need detM_mul at all.
