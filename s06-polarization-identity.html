Inner products and geometric algebra: the polarization identity

A vector space over a field FF is a set of vectors that add — forming an abelian group — together with a scaling action a*:va *: v of scalars on vectors: scaling distributes over both kinds of sum, two scalings compose by multiplying the scalars, and the scalar 11 scales trivially. No coordinates appear anywhere. As before, == is a setoid equality: a chosen equivalence relation every operation must respect, not Coq’s equality of raw terms.

A bare vector space has no notion of length or angle. This post adds one, and then shows a single clean identity relating it to multiplication.

An inner product is a symmetric bilinear pairing

An inner product pairs two vectors and returns a scalar, written ⟨u,v⟩\langle u , v\rangle. It is symmetric (⟨u,v⟩=⟨v,u⟩\langle u,v\rangle = \langle v,u\rangle), additive in its left slot, and pulls scalars out of its left slot. Because it is symmetric, the same two laws hold on the right. The class states exactly these:

Class InnerProduct (F V : Type) `{VectorSpace F V} := {
  #[global] ip_inner   :: HasInner F V;
  #[global] ip_proper  :: Proper (equ ==> equ ==> equ) (@inner F V _);
  ip_sym     : forall u v : V, ⟨ u , v ⟩ == ⟨ v , u ⟩;
  ip_add_l   : forall u v w : V, ⟨ u + v , w ⟩ == ⟨ u , w ⟩ + ⟨ v , w ⟩;
  ip_scale_l : forall (a : F) (u v : V), ⟨ a *: u , v ⟩ == a * ⟨ u , v ⟩
}.

Definition norm2 {F V : Type} `{InnerProduct F V} (u : V) : F := ⟨ u , u ⟩.

The last definition, norm2 u = ⟨ u , u ⟩, is the squared length of uu; write it ∥u∥2\|u\|^2. There is deliberately no square root and no claim that it is positive — positivity needs an ordered field, and we do not assume one here. norm2 is simply the quadratic form the pairing induces, and it is all the following needs.

Expanding a square: norm2_add

The one fact we will carry forward is what happens to ∥u+v∥2\|u+v\|^2. In an ordinary real inner product this is the law of cosines; here it holds formally, from the bilinearity axioms alone:

∥u+v∥2=(∥u∥2+∥v∥2)+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩).\|u+v\|^2 \;=\; \big(\|u\|^2 + \|v\|^2\big) + \big(\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle\big).

The heart of it is expanding ⟨u+v,u+v⟩\langle u+v, u+v\rangle by additivity in each slot in turn:

⟨u+v,u+v⟩=⟨u,u+v⟩+⟨v,u+v⟩{additivity in the left slot}=(⟨u,u⟩+⟨u,v⟩)+(⟨v,u⟩+⟨v,v⟩){additivity in the right slot, twice}\begin{aligned} \langle u+v,u+v\rangle &= \langle u,u+v\rangle + \langle v,u+v\rangle &&\{\text{additivity in the left slot}\}\\ &= (\langle u,u\rangle+\langle u,v\rangle) + (\langle v,u\rangle+\langle v,v\rangle) &&\{\text{additivity in the right slot, twice}\} \end{aligned}

then regrouping the four terms into the two diagonal ones and the two cross ones. That is inner_expand:

Theorem inner_expand : forall u v : V,
  ⟨ u + v , u + v ⟩ == (⟨ u , u ⟩ + ⟨ v , v ⟩) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩).
Proof.
  intros u v.
  calc (⟨ u + v , u + v ⟩)
    == (⟨ u , u + v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u + v ⟩)
      { (ip_add_l u v (u + v)) }
    == ((⟨ u , u ⟩ + ⟨ u , v ⟩) + ⟨ v , u + v ⟩)
      { (ip_add_r u u v) }
    == ((⟨ u , u ⟩ + ⟨ u , v ⟩) + (⟨ v , u ⟩ + ⟨ v , v ⟩))
      { (ip_add_r v u v) }
    == ((⟨ u , u ⟩ + ⟨ v , v ⟩) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩))
      { (add_rotate ⟨ u , u ⟩ ⟨ u , v ⟩ ⟨ v , u ⟩ ⟨ v , v ⟩) }.
Qed.

Reading the chain:

  • ip_add_l u v (u + v) splits the left slot: ⟨u+v,w⟩=⟨u,w⟩+⟨v,w⟩\langle u+v, w\rangle = \langle u,w\rangle + \langle v,w\rangle, with w=u+vw = u+v.
  • ip_add_r u u v splits the right slot of the first term. This right-additivity is not an axiom — it was derived earlier in the file from symmetry plus left-additivity — but it is available now as an ordinary lemma.
  • ip_add_r v u v splits the right slot of the second term.
  • add_rotate is a pure abelian-group rearrangement, (a+b)+(c+d)=(a+d)+(b+c)(a+b)+(c+d) = (a+d)+(b+c), proved back in the abelian-groups post. It gathers the two diagonal scalars ⟨u,u⟩,⟨v,v⟩\langle u,u\rangle, \langle v,v\rangle into one pair and the two cross scalars ⟨u,v⟩,⟨v,u⟩\langle u,v\rangle, \langle v,u\rangle into the other.

Since ∥w∥2\|w\|^2 is defined as ⟨w,w⟩\langle w,w\rangle, renaming the three diagonal pairings gives the statement we wanted:

Theorem norm2_add : forall u v : V,
  norm2 (u + v) == (norm2 u + norm2 v) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩).

Notice the cross term ⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle. It is the whole subject of the rest of this post.

Geometric algebra: a vector squared is its length

Now embed the vectors into a ring. A ring AA has an addition and a multiplication with a unit 11, the multiplication distributing over the addition on both sides — and, crucially, it need not be commutative (the endomorphism ring of the previous post was the first example). A geometric algebra over (V,⟨⋅,⋅⟩)(V, \langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle) is such a ring AA, with VV mapped into it linearly by a map ga_vec, subject to one governing axiom — writing eue_u for the image of the vector uu in the ring:

eueu=∥u∥2*:1.e_u\, e_u \;=\; \|u\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1.

In words: a vector, multiplied by itself in the ring, is its own squared length — as a scalar multiple of the ring’s unit. That axiom, ga_contract, is the seed of the Clifford algebras, the setting in which rotations and reflections turn into ordinary multiplication. The class collects the ring, the module structure, the embedding, and the contraction axiom:

Class GeometricAlgebra (F V A : Type) `{InnerProduct F V} := {
  #[global] ga_ring        :: Ring A;
  #[global] ga_scale       :: HasScale F A;
  #[global] ga_scale_laws  :: ScaleLaws F A;
  ga_scale_mul_l : forall (a : F) (x y : A), (a *: x) * y == a *: (x * y);
  ga_scale_mul_r : forall (a : F) (x y : A), x * (a *: y) == a *: (x * y);
  ga_vec         : V -> A;
  #[global] ga_vec_proper  :: Proper (equ ==> equ) ga_vec;
  ga_vec_add     : forall u v : V, ga_vec (u + v) == ga_vec u + ga_vec v;
  ga_vec_scale   : forall (a : F) (u : V), ga_vec (a *: u) == a *: ga_vec u;
  ga_contract    : forall u : V, ga_vec u * ga_vec u == norm2 u *: 1
}.

The contraction axiom only ever mentions a vector squared — a vector times itself. What does it say about the product of two different vectors? That is what the polarization identity extracts.

The polarization identity

The claim, in one sentence: the symmetric part of the geometric product of two vectors — uv+vuu v + v u — is their inner product, taken in both orders:

euev+eveu=(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩)*:1.e_u\, e_v + e_v\, e_u \;=\; \big(\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle\big) \mathbin{*:} 1.

The blackboard argument is to square u+vu+v in two ways and equate them. Expanding the product in the ring, then contracting each squared term:

(eu+ev)(eu+ev)=eueu+eveu+euev+evev{distributivity}=∥u∥2*:1+∥v∥2*:1+(euev+eveu){contraction on each square}\begin{aligned} (e_u + e_v)(e_u + e_v) &= e_u e_u + e_v e_u + e_u e_v + e_v e_v &&\{\text{distributivity}\}\\ &= \|u\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1 + \|v\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1 + (e_u e_v + e_v e_u) &&\{\text{contraction on each square}\} \end{aligned}

But eu+ev=eu+ve_u + e_v = e_{u+v} because the embedding is linear, so the same quantity contracts directly:

(eu+ev)(eu+ev)=eu+veu+v{linearity of the embedding}=∥u+v∥2*:1{contraction}(e_u + e_v)(e_u + e_v) = e_{u+v}\, e_{u+v} \quad\{\text{linearity of the embedding}\}\quad = \|u+v\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1 \quad\{\text{contraction}\}

Equate the two, and substitute the inner-product expansion of ∥u+v∥2\|u+v\|^2 from earlier in this post:

∥u∥2*:1+∥v∥2*:1+(euev+eveu)=(∥u∥2+∥v∥2+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩))*:1.\|u\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1 + \|v\|^2 \mathbin{*:} 1 + (e_u e_v + e_v e_u) = \big(\|u\|^2 + \|v\|^2 + (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle)\big) \mathbin{*:} 1.

The two squared-length terms are identical on both sides. Cancel them, and what is left is precisely the identity:

euev+eveu=(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩)*:1.e_u e_v + e_v e_u = (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle) \mathbin{*:} 1.

In Coq this is two lemmas. The first, contract_add, is the “square in two ways and equate” step: it drives the squared-length terms plus the cross terms all the way to ∥u+v∥2*:1\|u+v\|^2 *: 1.

Lemma contract_add : forall u v : V,
  (norm2 u *: (1 : A) + norm2 v *: 1)
    + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)
  == norm2 (u + v) *: 1.
Proof.
  intros u v.
  calc (((norm2 u *: (1 : A) + norm2 v *: (1 : A))
          + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)) : A)
    == (((ga_vec u * ga_vec u + norm2 v *: (1 : A))
          + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)) : A)
      { (ga_contract u) }
    == (((ga_vec u * ga_vec u + ga_vec v * ga_vec v)
          + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)) : A)
      { (ga_contract v) }
    == (((ga_vec u * ga_vec u + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)
          + (ga_vec v * ga_vec v + ga_vec u * ga_vec v)) : A)
      { (add_rotate (ga_vec u * ga_vec u) (ga_vec v * ga_vec v)
           (ga_vec u * ga_vec v) (ga_vec v * ga_vec u)) }
    == (((ga_vec u * ga_vec u + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)
          + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec v)) : A)
      { (comm (ga_vec v * ga_vec v) (ga_vec u * ga_vec v)) }
    == (((ga_vec u + ga_vec v) * (ga_vec u + ga_vec v)) : A)
      { (mul_add_expand (ga_vec u) (ga_vec v) (ga_vec u) (ga_vec v)) }
    == ((ga_vec (u + v) * ga_vec (u + v)) : A)
      { (ga_vec_add u v) }
    == ((norm2 (u + v) *: (1 : A)) : A)
      { (ga_contract (u + v)) }.
Qed.
  • ga_contract u, used right-to-left, turns ∥u∥2*:1\|u\|^2 *: 1 back into eueue_u e_u; ga_contract v does the same for ∥v∥2*:1\|v\|^2 *: 1. Now every term is a product of embedded vectors.
  • add_rotate, the abelian-group rearrangement from the abelian-groups post, and comm, commutativity of +, shuffle the four products into the pattern (eueu+eveu)+(euev+evev)(e_u e_u + e_v e_u) + (e_u e_v + e_v e_v).
  • mul_add_expand is the ring’s own expansion (a+b)(c+d)=(ac+bc)+(ad+bd)(a+b)(c+d) = (ac+bc) + (ad+bd), used right-to-left to fold those four products back into the single square (eu+ev)(eu+ev)(e_u + e_v)(e_u + e_v).
  • ga_vec_add u v replaces eu+eve_u + e_v by eu+ve_{u+v} (the embedding is linear), and ga_contract (u+v) contracts that square to ∥u+v∥2*:1\|u+v\|^2 *: 1.

The second lemma, polarization, performs the cancellation:

Theorem polarization : forall u v : V,
  ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u
  == (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩) *: (1 : A).
Proof.
  intros u v.
  apply (cancel_l (norm2 u *: (1 : A) + norm2 v *: 1)).
  calc (((norm2 u *: (1 : A) + norm2 v *: (1 : A))
          + (ga_vec u * ga_vec v + ga_vec v * ga_vec u)) : A)
    == ((norm2 (u + v) *: (1 : A)) : A)
      { (contract_add u v) }
    == ((((norm2 u + norm2 v) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩)) *: (1 : A)) : A)
      { (norm2_add u v) }
    == (((norm2 u + norm2 v) *: (1 : A) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩) *: (1 : A)) : A)
      { (sc_add_scale (norm2 u + norm2 v) (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩) (1 : A)) }
    == (((norm2 u *: (1 : A) + norm2 v *: (1 : A)) + (⟨ u , v ⟩ + ⟨ v , u ⟩) *: (1 : A)) : A)
      { (sc_add_scale (norm2 u) (norm2 v) (1 : A)) }.
Qed.
  • apply (cancel_l (norm2 u *: 1 + norm2 v *: 1)) is the cancellation step made precise. cancel_l — “if a+b=a+ca + b = a + c then b=cb = c” — was proved back in the groups post; applying it here adds the two squared-length terms to both sides of the goal, so it now suffices to prove the augmented equation. This is exactly the blackboard move of cancelling ∥u∥2\|u\|^2 and ∥v∥2\|v\|^2 from both sides.
  • contract_add u v rewrites the whole left side — the squared lengths plus the cross terms — to ∥u+v∥2*:1\|u+v\|^2 *: 1, the lemma just proved.
  • norm2_add u v expands ∥u+v∥2\|u+v\|^2 using the inner-product identity from the first half of this post: the reappearance of ⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle is where the inner product enters.
  • The two sc_add_scale steps are a vector-space axiom from the previous post — scaling distributes over a sum of scalars — used to split ((∥u∥2+∥v∥2)+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩))*:1\big((\|u\|^2 + \|v\|^2) + (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle)\big) *: 1 into (∥u∥2*:1+∥v∥2*:1)+(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩)*:1\big(\|u\|^2 *: 1 + \|v\|^2 *: 1\big) + (\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle) *: 1, whose left half is exactly the term cancel_l placed on the right. The chain closes, and the identity is proved.

So the symmetric part of the geometric product recovers the inner product from nothing but “a vector squared is its length.” The proof draws on three earlier posts at once — cancel_l from groups, add_rotate from abelian groups, sc_add_scale from vector spaces — and on norm2_add from its own first half, each used exactly where the blackboard argument reaches for it.

An algebra that satisfies the axioms

The axioms are not vacuous: Impl/CliffordFin.v constructs instances. The simplest interesting one takes the vector space to be the field itself — a one-dimensional line — paired by ⟨u,v⟩=−(uv)\langle u,v\rangle = -(uv), and takes the ring AA to be the complex numbers over FF (pairs with the usual complex multiplication, written C F), embedding each vector uu on the imaginary axis. A vector squared is then (ui)(ui)=−u2(ui)(ui) = -u^2, which is exactly ∥u∥2\|u\|^2 under the chosen pairing — the contraction axiom here is the familiar i2=−1i^2 = -1. The instance is assembled field by field, each obligation discharged by a lemma proved in the same file:

  Definition GA_Cliff1 : GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) :=
    @Build_GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) _ _ Cliff1_NegInner C_Ring Cliff1_Scale
      Cliff1_ScaleLaws cliff1_ga_scale_mul_l cliff1_ga_scale_mul_r
      cliff1_ga_vec cliff1_ga_vec_proper cliff1_ga_vec_add
      cliff1_ga_vec_scale cliff1_ga_contract.

and checked, for an arbitrary field, by a one-line example:

Example ga_cliff1_check (F : Type) (FF : Field F) :
  GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) := GA_Cliff1.

With the instance in scope, polarization — proved above once, for every geometric algebra — is a theorem about complex numbers with no further work.
