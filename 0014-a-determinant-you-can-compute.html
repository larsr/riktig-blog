riktig #14 — A determinant you can compute, and a trace that doesn’t care about your basis

Fourteenth post documenting the riktig project; the linear-algebra course continues. Proofs shown blackboard-style and as they live in the repository.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #10 proved a matrix is a representation of a linear map, chosen by a basis — never the definition of the map itself. Today two of the classical invariants of a matrix, trace and determinant, get built on exactly that foundation: computed via some basis, then proved not to depend on which one. That independence is the entire content of calling them “invariants” — usually assumed, here proved.

The determinant, by the formula you learned

Every course teaches determinant by expansion: pick a column, for each entry multiply by its cofactor (the determinant of the matrix with that row and column struck out), alternate signs, sum. In Impl/Det.v that recipe is executable code, expanding along the first column:

Fixpoint detM {n} (A : Matrix n n F) : F :=
  match n with
  | 0   => 1
  | S _ => bigopF (fun i => sign_at i * (A i F1 * detM (minor A i)))
  end.

minor A i deletes row i and column F1; sign_at i alternates 1, -1, 1, … down the column. The bookkeeping to make this precise in Coq — an injection skip that “increases past” a deleted row, so minor’s type is honest about being one row and one column smaller — is real work, but it’s engineering, not mathematics: once it’s right, the two theorems that matter fall out cleanly:

Theorem det_id : detM mat_id == 1.
Theorem det_col1_add : detM (upd_col1 A (c1 + c2)) ==
  detM (upd_col1 A c1) + detM (upd_col1 A c2).

The identity matrix has determinant 1, and the determinant is additive in its first column — because that column’s contribution to every term of the sum is literally the entry A i F1, and the minors (which don’t touch column F1 at all) don’t change. This is exactly what you’d expect a Laplace expansion to make easy, and it does.

What it does not make easy — deliberately left for later — is multiplicativity, det⁡(AB)=det⁡(A)det⁡(B)\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B). Proving that from this recursive formula wants a genuinely different technique (elementary row operations, coming in a future wave); the file’s own header says so, in plain language, rather than hiding the gap.

Trace: the sum you’d compute either way

The trace of a square matrix — sum the diagonal — looks even simpler, and the classical fact about it is almost never proved in an undergraduate course, just asserted: trace doesn’t care which basis you wrote the matrix in. In Theory/Trace.v that assertion becomes a genuine theorem, and it’s worth watching how it’s built, because the route is the whole point of this project.

Step one, a fact about sums. For any two matrices,

tr(AB)=tr(BA)\mathrm{tr}(AB) = \mathrm{tr}(BA)

— provable directly, by expanding both sides into a double sum over i and j and noting they’re the same double sum, summed in the other order (bigopF_swap, a genuine Fubini theorem for finite sums, proved once in Theory/BigOp.v and reused everywhere a double sum needs reordering).

Step two, change of basis is conjugation. Post #10 already proved that switching bases transports a linear map’s matrix by conjugation: [[f]]B′=P−1[[f]]BP[\![f]\!]_{B'} = P^{-1} [\![f]\!]_B P for the change-of-basis matrix PP. Combine the two facts:

Theorem tr_basis_indep : forall B B' f,
  trM (mat_of B' B' f) == trM (mat_of B B f).

Read the proof route in English: tr(P−1AP)=tr(APP−1)=tr(A)\mathrm{tr}(P^{-1}AP) = \mathrm{tr}(A P P^{-1}) = \mathrm{tr}(A) — cycle the product using step one, then cancel PP−1P P^{-1} back to the identity. Every piece of that sentence is a named lemma already sitting in the repository (mat_of_conj for the conjugation, tr_mul_comm for the cycling, cob_inv for the cancellation) — the theorem assembles, it doesn’t need new machinery.

Definition tr (B : Basis n F U) (f : LinearMap F U U) : F :=
  trM (mat_of B B f).

tr_basis_indep says the basis argument to tr is immaterial — you may compute it in any basis and get the same answer, and now you know why: because the two matrices that different bases produce are always similar, and similar matrices always have the same diagonal sum, for the two structural reasons above and no others.
