riktig #26 — any adjacent pair

Twenty-sixth post documenting the riktig project. The determinant thread continues: a fourth attempt narrows the gap further, proving something strictly stronger than what came before it, while the very last piece keeps its precise shape.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #23 reformulated the determinant as a sum over permutations to sidestep a row-relocation obstruction that had stalled two earlier attempts, and proved the row-swap half of general antisymmetry. What was still missing was the harder half: showing the permutation’s sign itself flips under a swap. This post gets that, for adjacent rows — genuinely, not just as a repeat of an old special case.

What “adjacent” already meant, and what changed

Back in the original cofactor-recursion attempt (Impl/Elim.v), det_swap_F1_FS2 proved that swapping the first two rows specifically negates the determinant — useful, but pinned to one fixed pair, because the recursion’s own structure made every other pair much harder to track. Today’s result removes that restriction entirely:

Theorem perm_sign_postcompose_adj : forall (n : nat) (k : Fin.t n) (sigma : Perm (S n)),
  perm_sign (perm_postcompose sigma (swap_fin (fweaken k) (Fin.FS k))
                                    (swap_fin (fweaken k) (Fin.FS k)))
    == - perm_sign sigma.

Theorem detM_swap_adjacent : forall (n : nat) (k : Fin.t n) (A : Matrix (S n) (S n) F),
  detM (row_swap A (fweaken k) (Fin.FS k)) == - detM A.

Reading the notation: both fweaken k and Fin.FS k embed k : Fin.t n into Fin.t (S n) — the first keeping its value, the second shifting it by one — so together they denote positions kk and k+1k+1, and the pair ranges over every adjacent pair as k does. And perm_postcompose sigma tau tau' builds “apply sigma, then tau” as a permutation value, taking tau together with its inverse tau'; a transposition is its own inverse, which is why the same swap_fin appears twice.

Any adjacent pair, anywhere in the matrix — not just the top two. The smoke test checks this on a concrete 3×33\times 3 matrix by swapping the middle two rows, deliberately not the pair every earlier version of this fact was limited to.

Why the permutation-sum representation makes this tractable

Post #23 explained why perm_prod, the flat permutation-indexed product, lets a row swap act by pure postcomposition — no relocation surgery. Sign is the harder half because perm_sign isn’t read off the matrix at all; it’s a property of the permutation itself, computed by its own recursive structure (choose σ(1)\sigma(1), recurse on the rest). Concretely, a Perm n is a Lehmer-style encoding — Perm_S c p records the choice σ(1)=c\sigma(1) = c and recurses with p on the remaining positions — and perm_sign (Perm_S c p) == sign_at c * perm_sign p, where sign_at c alternates 1,−1,1,…1, -1, 1, \dots with the position chosen; multiplied out over all levels, that is (−1)(-1) raised to the sum of the chosen indices, the permutation’s inversion count. To show sign flips under postcomposition by a transposition, you first need to construct “σ\sigma, then apply τ\tau” as an actual permutation value your recursion can compute on — not just as an equation about where things end up.

That construction, perm_postcompose, is the piece that had to exist before sign’s behavior could even be asked about. Once it does, sign’s recursive definition gives a clean case split: postcompose by a transposition, peel one level of the recursion, and ask what happens to the remaining transposition at the smaller instance. For an adjacent pair, the answer is either “it vanishes” (if the peeled position was one of the two swapped) or “it survives, relocated” (otherwise) — both clean, both closing by structural induction.

Where it stops, precisely

For a non-adjacent pair, the same peeling produces something else entirely: a genuine 3-cycle on the remaining positions, not a transposition. The induction that closes the adjacent case simply doesn’t apply to a 3-cycle — it’s the wrong shape of object. This isn’t a proof-search failure; it’s confirmed by direct hand computation and recorded as such.

The classical fix is well known and doesn’t need new ideas, just more of the same kind of work: any transposition of two positions i,ji, j factors as an odd-length chain of adjacent transpositions (the textbook “bubble” identity, recursed on the distance |i−j||i-j|), and sign’s flip under an adjacent transposition — now proved — pushes through that chain by induction. What’s needed to make the push-through work is one more composition lemma, showing sign behaves correctly when two postcompositions are chained rather than just one — a precise, identified, one-more-induction task, not a new kind of difficulty.

Four attempts have now gone into general determinant multiplicativity — two direct ones that stalled, post #23’s reformulation, and today’s adjacent-pair sign flip — and each kept its progress; the remaining gap is one composition lemma and one factorization argument, both with their proof route already sketched.

Next: perm_postcompose_compose, the composition lemma that chains adjacent transpositions into arbitrary ones — attempt #5, or a pivot to Stage B’s softmax/ReLU if that keeps for another round.
