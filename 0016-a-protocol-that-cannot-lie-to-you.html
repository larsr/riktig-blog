riktig #16 — A protocol that cannot lie to you

Sixteenth post documenting the riktig project. The channels thread opened in post #15 reaches its first real payoff; the linear-algebra course also closes out inner-product geometry. Proofs shown at the level of “here is the theorem and why it is true,” in the project’s usual style.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #15 modeled a faulty channel as a relation between what was sent and what arrived — no probabilities, just possibility. Today that relation earns its keep: the alternating-bit protocol, the oldest trick in telecom for talking reliably over an unreliable wire, gets a safety proof that holds unconditionally — for any pattern of loss and duplication, any number of retransmissions.

The protocol, in one paragraph

You want to send messages m1,m2,m3,…m_1, m_2, m_3, \dots over a wire that can drop packets and duplicate them. Tag each message with a single bit that alternates: m1m_1 goes out tagged 0, m2m_2 tagged 1, m3m_3 tagged 0 again, and so on — retransmitting the current message (same tag) until you’re satisfied it arrived. The receiver expects a bit, starting at 0; when a packet with the expected bit shows up, it’s delivered and the expectation flips; anything with the wrong bit is silently discarded. That’s the whole protocol. The bit’s only job: let the receiver recognize a stale duplicate of the message it already has, and throw it away.

The theorem

Theorem abp_safety : forall (k : nat) (b0 : bit) (ms : list M) (wire : list Packet),
  lossy_dup (sender_stream b0 ms k) wire ->
  subseq (deliver b0 wire) ms.

Read it in English: whatever the wire delivers — for any channel behavior in the lossy_dup class, any retransmission budget k, any message list ms — the sequence of messages the receiver actually accepts is a subsequence of what was sent. No duplicates get through that were never sent. No reordering. No message the application never wrote gets fabricated. This is the entire safety contract of a reliable-delivery protocol, and it is a theorem, not a hope.

Notice what’s absent: no assumption that packets eventually get through. If the channel drops everything, deliver returns the empty list — still, trivially, a subsequence of ms. That’s honest: safety (nothing bad happens) and liveness (something good eventually happens) are different properties, and this theorem only claims the first. Liveness needs a fairness hypothesis — “the channel doesn’t drop forever” — which is deliberately not assumed here; it’s queued as the next stage (T4) of the roadmap, precisely so today’s theorem stays this strong and this simple.

Why the proof needed a detour through channels themselves

The interesting lemma isn’t about ABP at all — it’s about lossy_dup alone:

Lemma lossy_dup_app_split : forall s1 s2 r,
  lossy_dup (s1 ++ s2) r ->
  exists r1 r2, r = r1 ++ r2 /\ lossy_dup s1 r1 /\ lossy_dup s2 r2.

If the channel acted on a sent list that happens to be a concatenation s1 ++ s2, then the received list splits to match — a lossy_dup-behavior on the first part, followed by one on the second. This sounds obvious and is not: the stutter constructor (duplication) can re-emit the front of what’s already been received without consuming anything more from the sent side, so a run of duplicates near the boundary could, in principle, straddle it in an awkward way. The proof goes by induction on the lossy_dup derivation itself — not on the lists — precisely to track that possibility honestly.

Once that lemma exists, ABP’s own proof is short: split the sent wire trace at each message’s retransmission burst, note that a whole burst (all copies carrying the same bit) contributes at most one delivered message no matter how the channel maims it — duplicates all carry the identical bit and get rejected after the first — and induct over the message list.

This is the project’s recurring shape again: a hard, general fact about the substrate (lossy_dup_app_split, purely about relations on lists) makes the domain-specific theorem (abp_safety) almost fall out. The next protocols in the roadmap — Go-Back-N, selective repeat, the mechanism at the heart of TCP — will reuse this exact lemma; a new retransmission scheme only ever owes its own burst-delivery argument, not a new proof that channels behave sanely.
