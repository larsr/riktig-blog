Curry, uncurry, and what “isomorphism” buys you over “round trip”

Take two vector spaces UU and VV over the same field, and look at the functions between them that respect addition and scalar multiplication — the linear maps. Those maps can themselves be added, negated, and scaled, one input point at a time: to add two linear maps ff and gg, build the map u↦fu+guu \mapsto f\,u + g\,u. Because VV is already a vector space, evaluating this sum at any fixed uu lands back in VV, and every vector-space law for the resulting collection of maps reduces, point by point, to a law that already holds in VV. The upshot is a new vector space, written Hom(U,V)\mathrm{Hom}(U, V), built one level up from UU and VV with no genuinely new algebra of its own.

Now do the two-argument version of the same idea, and ask a question about it. A function of two vector arguments that is additive and scale-respecting in each argument separately — fix either input and the result is linear in the other — looks, on the face of it, like a different kind of object from a function of one argument that returns another function of one argument. But there is an obvious way to convert between them. Given the two-argument version, fix the first argument and read off what remains as a function of the second; doing that for every choice of first argument produces a one-argument function that itself returns one-argument functions. Run that construction in reverse — given the nested version, feed it both arguments one after the other — and the original two-argument function comes back. Do both conversions, one after the other, in either order, and nothing is lost: you arrive back where you started.

Here is the actual question. Suppose two conversions like this always cancel — applying one after the other, in either order, is the identity. Is that fact, on its own, as good as saying the two collections being converted between are “the same object, differently packaged”? Or is there a gap: something a bare pair of inverse functions doesn’t hand you, that a genuine claim of sameness would?

Formalizing the two-argument function, and its round trip

A function of two vector arguments with the “linear in each slot separately” property above is called a bilinear map. In the code, a record BilinearMap F U V W carries the function together with proofs of all four half-linearity laws — two for the left argument, two for the right — from Interface/Bilinear.v:

Record BilinearMap (F U V W : Type) {FF : Field F}
    {VU : VectorSpace F U} {VV : VectorSpace F V}
    {VW : VectorSpace F W} := {
  bm_fun :> U -> V -> W;
  #[global] bm_proper :: Proper (equ ==> equ ==> equ) bm_fun;
  bm_add_l : forall (u u' : U) (v : V),
    bm_fun (u + u') v == bm_fun u v + bm_fun u' v;
  bm_scale_l : forall (a : F) (u : U) (v : V),
    bm_fun (a *: u) v == a *: bm_fun u v;
  bm_add_r : forall (u : U) (v v' : V),
    bm_fun u (v + v') == bm_fun u v + bm_fun u v';
  bm_scale_r : forall (a : F) (u : U) (v : V),
    bm_fun u (a *: v) == a *: bm_fun u v
}.

The two conversions from the opening question are curry and uncurry, both in Theory/Hom.v. curry takes a bilinear map b and returns u ↦ (v ↦ b u v): fixing u first gives a linear map in v (an element of Hom(V,W)\mathrm{Hom}(V, W)), and the whole assignment is itself linear in u, landing curry b in Hom(U,Hom(V,W))\mathrm{Hom}(U, \mathrm{Hom}(V,W)).

Definition curry : LinearMap F U HomVW :=
  @Build_LinearMap F U HomVW FF VU HVW
    (fun u : U => bm_appl b u)
    curry_proper curry_add curry_scale.

uncurry runs this backward: given a linear map g that already returns linear maps, uncurry g is the two-argument function (u, v) ↦ g u v, and the four bilinearity laws for it follow from g’s own linearity and the linearity of each g u.

Definition uncurry : BilinearMap F U V W :=
  @Build_BilinearMap F U V W FF VU VV VW
    (fun (u : U) (v : V) => g u v)
    uncurry_proper uncurry_add_l uncurry_scale_l
    uncurry_add_r uncurry_scale_r.

And here is the round trip from the opening question — converting one way and back, in either order, is the identity:

curry(uncurryg)=ganduncurry(curryb)=b,\mathrm{curry}(\mathrm{uncurry}\,g) = g \qquad\text{and}\qquad \mathrm{uncurry}(\mathrm{curry}\,b) = b,

both because u↦(v↦guv)u \mapsto (v \mapsto g\,u\,v) is gg, applied one argument at a time. In Coq:

Theorem curry_uncurry : forall g : LinearMap F U HomVW,
  curry (uncurry g) == g.
Proof.
  intros g u v.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

Theorem uncurry_curry : forall b : BilinearMap F U V W,
  uncurry (curry b) == b.
Proof.
  intros b u v.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

Both proofs are reflexivity, because currying a two-argument function and then uncurrying it again is the same function written a different way — the composite beta-reduces to the identity, with no algebra to check. But look closely at what these two theorems actually claim: they are equalities of plain functions. Nothing in them says that curry and uncurry respect addition and scalar multiplication as maps between vector spaces — and until that is established, and until both sides being converted between are shown to be vector spaces in the first place, there is no basis for calling BilinearMap F U V W and Hom(U,Hom(V,W))\mathrm{Hom}(U, \mathrm{Hom}(V,W)) “the same vector space.” This is exactly the gap from the opening question.

Closing the gap: bilinear maps as a vector space, and a stronger notion of “same”

The first missing piece is a target for “respects addition and scalar multiplication” to even be asked about: do the bilinear maps between UU, VV, and WW form a vector space themselves? Theory/BilinearVS.v proves yes, by the same recipe as Hom(U,V)\mathrm{Hom}(U,V) — add, negate, and scale bilinear maps pointwise, at each pair of inputs (u,v)(u,v) instead of at each single input, and check the result is again bilinear. The whole structure assembles into one instance:

#[global] Instance Bilin_VectorSpace : VectorSpace F Bilin :=
  @Build_VectorSpace F Bilin FF Bilin_AbGroup Bilin_Scale Bilin_ScaleLaws.

The second missing piece is a record for the stronger claim itself — not “these two functions cancel,” but “these two vector spaces are the same, up to relabeling.” Theory/VSIso.v calls it VSIso:

Record VSIso (F U V : Type) {FF : Field F}
    {VU : VectorSpace F U} {VV : VectorSpace F V} := {
  vsi_to : LinearMap F U V;
  vsi_from : LinearMap F V U;
  vsi_fromto : forall u : U, vsi_from (vsi_to u) == u;
  vsi_tofrom : forall v : V, vsi_to (vsi_from v) == v
}.

The fields vsi_to and vsi_from are not bare functions — they are LinearMaps, each one already carrying its own proof of respecting addition and scaling. A VSIso is a pair of mutually inverse linear maps, plus the two round-trip proofs. That is exactly what curry and uncurry were missing: the round trip (curry_uncurry/uncurry_curry) was already on hand; what was missing was a proof that curry and uncurry are themselves linear, and a place — Bilin_VectorSpace — for that linearity to be linearity of.

The proof: assembling vsiso_curry

Two proofs do the actual work: one that a bilinear map’s pointwise scalar multiple is again bilinear (needed for Bilin_VectorSpace), and one that curry is linear (needed to build the isomorphism proper). In the Coq below, Bilin is local shorthand for BilinearMap F U V W, and HomVW for LinearMap F V W.

Scaling a bilinear map stays bilinear. To register a*:ba *: b (scale the bilinear map bb by aa) as bilinear, one of the four laws needed is that scaling commutes with bb’s own left-slot scaling:

a*:b(c*:u,v)=a*:(c*:b(u,v)){b linear in its left slot}=c*:(a*:b(u,v)){two scalings commute}.a *: b(c *: u, v) \;=\; a *: (c *: b(u,v)) \quad \{b\text{ linear in its left slot}\} \;=\; c *: (a *: b(u,v)) \quad \{\text{two scalings commute}\}.

The first step uses bb’s own linearity in its left argument; the second is the fact that two scalings of the same vector commute — the theorem scale_swap from the opening recap’s Hom construction.

Lemma bilin_smul_scale_l : forall (c : F) (u : U) (v : V),
  a *: b (c *: u) v == c *: (a *: b u v).
Proof.
  intros c u v.
  rewrite (bm_scale_l b c u v).
  apply scale_swap.
Qed.

Walking it: intros c u v fixes the scalar and the two vector arguments; rewrite (bm_scale_l b c u v) performs the first equality, replacing b (c *: u) v with c *: b u v in the goal, using bb’s own left-slot linearity; what remains, a *: (c *: b u v) == c *: (a *: b u v), is exactly scale_swap, reused with no new proof.

Currying a sum of bilinear maps. The cleanest step in showing curry is a linear map is the additivity law:

curry(b+b′)(u)(v)=(b+b′)(u,v)=b(u,v)+b′(u,v)=curry(b)(u)(v)+curry(b′)(u)(v).\mathrm{curry}(b + b')(u)(v) = (b+b')(u,v) = b(u,v) + b'(u,v) = \mathrm{curry}(b)(u)(v) + \mathrm{curry}(b')(u)(v).

Lemma curry_lm_add : forall b b' : Bilin, curry (b + b') == curry b + curry b'.
Proof.
  intros b b' u v.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

Walking it: intros b b' fixes the two bilinear maps being added; the goal is an equality of linear maps under Hom’s pointwise setoid, so intros ... u v also fixes the point (u,v)(u,v) at which to check it; and reflexivity closes the goal outright. That single tactic performs the whole three-step blackboard chain at once, because Bilin’s pointwise addition unfolds to exactly “evaluate both maps at (u,v)(u,v), then add,” and curry’s addition (inherited from Hom(U,−)\mathrm{Hom}(U,-)’s own pointwise addition) unfolds to exactly “evaluate both curried maps at uu, then at vv, then add.” The two sides of the equation are the same term once Coq unfolds the definitions. curry_lm and uncurry_lm package curry and uncurry as full LinearMaps this way:

Definition curry_lm : LinearMap F Bilin (LinearMap F U HomVW) :=
  @Build_LinearMap F Bilin (LinearMap F U HomVW) FF VBilin HomUVW
    curry curry_lm_proper curry_lm_add curry_lm_scale.

and, after the mirror-image lemmas for uncurry,

Definition uncurry_lm : LinearMap F (LinearMap F U HomVW) Bilin :=
  @Build_LinearMap F (LinearMap F U HomVW) Bilin FF HomUVW VBilin
    uncurry uncurry_lm_proper uncurry_lm_add uncurry_lm_scale.

The isomorphism itself is these two linear maps, plus the round-trip theorems already proved earlier — uncurry_curry and curry_uncurry — slotted straight in with no new proof:

Definition vsiso_curry : @VSIso F Bilin (LinearMap F U HomVW) FF VBilin HomUVW :=
  @Build_VSIso F Bilin (LinearMap F U HomVW) FF VBilin HomUVW
    curry_lm uncurry_lm uncurry_curry curry_uncurry.

Matching this against the VSIso record: vsi_to is curry_lm, vsi_from is uncurry_lm, vsi_fromto is uncurry_curry, and vsi_tofrom is curry_uncurry. All the work specific to this post was proving curry and uncurry linear; the fact that they cancel was already proved before this construction existed.

One more short proof closes a second gap: a VSIso is a fact about two raw carriers U and V, but the library’s general notion of isomorphism, ≅, lives one level up, on objects of a category. Theory/Category.v defines Isomorphic x y := inhabited (Iso x y) for any category, together with a proof that ≅ is an equivalence relation. Vector spaces over a fixed field form a category, Vect, whose arrows are linear maps. vsiso_isomorphic connects the two:

Corollary vsiso_isomorphic {U V : Type} {VU : VectorSpace F U} {VV : VectorSpace F V}
    (i : VSIso F U V) :
  Build_VectObj F FF U VU ≅ Build_VectObj F FF V VV.
Proof.
  exact (inhabits (vsiso_to_iso i)).
Qed.

Isomorphic is defined as “there exists a witness Iso,” and inhabits is exactly the constructor that packages a witness into that existence claim, so the proof hands vsiso_to_iso i — a helper, defined a few lines earlier in the same file, that transcribes a VSIso’s four fields directly into an Iso’s four fields, since the two records have the same shape — to that constructor. Nothing about Vect or ≅ needed a new proof for VSIso specifically; the Equivalence instance for VS_Isomorphic is the category’s own Isomorphic_Equivalence, reused without modification:

Definition VS_Isomorphic (X Y : VectObj F) : Prop := X ≅ Y.

#[global] Instance VS_Isomorphic_Equivalence : Equivalence VS_Isomorphic :=
  Isomorphic_Equivalence.

Using vsiso_curry

First, structure resolution: Bilin_VectorSpace and vsiso_curry both assemble for any field and any three component vector spaces, with no per-type work. Two smoke tests in the source confirm this by asking Coq to fill each definition with _ and checking it succeeds:

Example bilin_vs_check (F U V W : Type) (FF : Field F)
  (VU : VectorSpace F U) (VV : VectorSpace F V) (VW : VectorSpace F W) :
  VectorSpace F (BilinearMap F U V W) := _.

Second, and this is precisely what building VSIso gains over stopping at a round trip of plain functions: a VSIso can be handed to other constructions. VSIso has its own symmetry and transitivity operations (vsiso_sym, vsiso_trans), so a chain of isomorphisms — AA isomorphic to BB, BB isomorphic to CC — combines into a single AA-isomorphic-to-CC fact automatically, with a constructed proof, rather than requiring someone to re-derive from scratch that two composed pairs of inverse functions are again mutually inverse. And because vsi_to/vsi_from are linear maps with proved round trips, an equation proved on one side of a VSIso can be rewritten into the corresponding equation on the other side, with the linearity guaranteeing the rewrite is sound — a bare pair of inverse functions, with no linearity attached, gives no such license.

Feeding vsiso_curry through vsiso_isomorphic is a concrete instance of “handing a VSIso to another construction”: it produces a checked ≅ fact between the space of bilinear maps and the nested hom-space, using the general-purpose category machinery rather than any bespoke argument about currying:

Example vsiso_curry_isomorphic (F U V W : Type) (FF : Field F)
    (VU : VectorSpace F U) (VV : VectorSpace F V) (VW : VectorSpace F W) :
  Build_VectObj F FF (BilinearMap F U V W) (@Bilin_VectorSpace F U V W FF VU VV VW)
    ≅ Build_VectObj F FF (LinearMap F U (LinearMap F V W))
        (@Hom_VectorSpace F U (LinearMap F V W) FF VU (@Hom_VectorSpace F V W FF VV VW)) :=
  vsiso_isomorphic (@vsiso_curry F U V W FF VU VV VW).

That closes the question this post opened with. Knowing that curry and uncurry cancel was not enough, by itself, to call the bilinear maps and the nested hom-space “the same vector space” — that needed curry and uncurry shown linear, and the space of bilinear maps shown to be a vector space in the first place. Once both were in hand, the packaged isomorphism composed with the library’s general-purpose tools with no further argument: vsiso_isomorphic alone turned it into a fact in the same ≅ relation every other isomorphism in the library already sits in.
