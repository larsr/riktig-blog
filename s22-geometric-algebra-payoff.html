Multiplying vectors: the contraction axiom, and the complex numbers as a Clifford algebra

A geometric algebra embeds a vector space VV into a ring AA — a place where vectors can be not only added but multiplied, with the multiplication distributing over addition but not required to commute. The one governing law is that a vector times itself collapses to a scalar, its own squared length: v⋅v=‖v‖2⋅1v\cdot v = \lVert v\rVert^2 \cdot 1, the scalar acting on the ring’s unit. From that single axiom the polarization identity falls out — the symmetric part of the product of two vectors recovers their inner product,

uv+vu=(⟨u,v⟩+⟨v,u⟩)⋅1.u\,v + v\,u \;=\; \big(\langle u,v\rangle + \langle v,u\rangle\big)\cdot 1.

That identity is the subject of an earlier post; here it is the fact we build on rather than re-prove. This post is about what the product is for, and about the smallest concrete algebra in which the axiom actually holds.

The picture: reflections and rotations as products

The reason to multiply vectors is that rigid motions become multiplication. Read the polarization identity when uu and vv are orthogonal: their inner product vanishes, so uv+vu=0uv + vu = 0, i.e. uv=−vuuv = -vu — orthogonal vectors anticommute. And a unit vector vv, one with ∥v∥2=1\|v\|^2 = 1, is its own inverse in the ring, since vv=1v\,v = 1.

Put those together. For a unit vector vv, the map

x↦−vxvx \;\longmapsto\; -\,v\,x\,v

reflects xx across the hyperplane orthogonal to vv: the component of xx along vv anticommutes past one factor and gets negated twice into a flip, while the components orthogonal to vv commute through and are left alone. Compose two such reflections and the double sign cancels; what remains, x↦RxR−1x \mapsto R\,x\,R^{-1} for a product R=vwR = vw of two unit vectors, is a rotation — the angle doubled from the angle between the two mirrors. This is the whole reason geometric algebra exists: the awkward, dimension-specific formulas for reflections and rotations become one uniform sandwich product, and RR is a single object, a rotor, that composes by ordinary multiplication.

Two boundaries, stated plainly. First, the reflection and rotation theorems above are the motivating picture for the subject in general; they are not formalized in the files this post draws from, and this post does not claim otherwise. Second, anticommutation only has room to act when there are two independent directions, dimension ≥2\ge 2. What the files do contain is the axiom itself, the polarization identity drawn from it, and the smallest concrete algebra satisfying the axiom — the case where “a vector squared is minus its length” is exactly the equation i2=−1i^2 = -1.

The concrete algebra: Cl(0,1)\mathrm{Cl}(0,1) is the complex numbers

Take a one-dimensional space of vectors — a line, modelled by the field FF itself — and pair it with the negative inner product ⟨u,v⟩=−(uv)\langle u,v \rangle = -(u\,v). Embed the line into the complex numbers ℂF\mathbb{C} F (pairs over FF, a ring for any field) along the imaginary axis: vec(u)=0+ui\mathrm{vec}(u) = 0 + u\,i. Then the governing axiom asks for

vec(u)⋅vec(u)=‖u‖2⋅1,\mathrm{vec}(u)\cdot\mathrm{vec}(u) \;=\; \lVert u\rVert^2 \cdot 1,

and both sides are computable. Componentwise, the complex multiplication rule gives

(0+ui)(0+ui)=(0⋅0−uu)+(0⋅u+u⋅0)i=−u2+0i,(0 + u\,i)(0 + u\,i) \;=\; (0\cdot 0 - u\,u) + (0\cdot u + u\cdot 0)\,i \;=\; -u^2 + 0\,i,

while the right-hand side is ‖u‖2=⟨u,u⟩=−(uu)=−u2\lVert u\rVert^2 = \langle u,u\rangle = -(u\,u) = -u^2, scaled onto the real unit. They match — and that matching is i2=−1i^2 = -1, now read as “the unit vector on the imaginary axis squares to the negative of its length.” The two component equations are isolated as plain field-level facts first.

  Lemma cliff1_contract_re : forall u : F,
    0 * 0 + - (u * u) == (- (u * u)) * 1.
  Proof.
    intros u.
    calc (0 * 0 + - (u * u))
      == (0 + - (u * u))    { mul_0_l }
      == (- (u * u))        { idl }
      == ((- (u * u)) * 1)  { ring_mul_1r }.
  Qed.

  Lemma cliff1_contract_im : forall u : F,
    0 * u + u * 0 == (- (u * u)) * 0.
  Proof.
    intros u.
    calc (0 * u + u * 0)
      == (0 + u * 0)        { mul_0_l }
      == ((0 : F) + 0)      { mul_0_r }
      == (0 : F)            { idl }
      == ((- (u * u)) * 0)  { mul_0_r }.
  Qed.

The real part is the multiplication rule of ℂF\mathbb{C}F applied to (0,u)⋅(0,u)(0,u)\cdot(0,u): real part 0⋅0−u⋅u0\cdot 0 - u\cdot u, which is −(uu)-(u u) after the 0⋅00\cdot 0 term is dropped, and that equals (−(uu))⋅1(-(u u))\cdot 1, the real component of the scalar −(uu)-(uu) sitting on the unit. The imaginary part is 0⋅u+u⋅0=00\cdot u + u\cdot 0 = 0, matching (−(uu))⋅0(-(uu))\cdot 0. Each calc chain is a short walk through the ring laws, one law per link — a zero times anything is zero (mul_0_l, mul_0_r), the additive unit absorbs (idl), one is a multiplicative unit (ring_mul_1r).

The contraction axiom for Cl(0,1)\mathrm{Cl}(0,1) is then those two facts, one per component of the complex number:

  Lemma cliff1_ga_contract : forall u : F,
    cliff1_ga_vec u * cliff1_ga_vec u
    == Cliff1_Scale (@InnerProduct.norm2 F F _ _ Cliff1_NegInner u) 1.
  Proof.
    intros u.
    split.
    - exact (cliff1_contract_re u).
    - exact (cliff1_contract_im u).
  Qed.

Equality of two complex numbers is equality of their real and imaginary parts, which is what split produces; cliff1_contract_re discharges the first, cliff1_contract_im the second. This one lemma is the last field of the record that certifies ℂF\mathbb{C}F, with vectors on the imaginary axis and the negative-definite form, as a genuine geometric algebra:

  Definition GA_Cliff1 : GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) :=
    @Build_GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) _ _ Cliff1_NegInner C_Ring Cliff1_Scale
      Cliff1_ScaleLaws cliff1_ga_scale_mul_l cliff1_ga_scale_mul_r
      cliff1_ga_vec cliff1_ga_vec_proper cliff1_ga_vec_add
      cliff1_ga_vec_scale cliff1_ga_contract.

Because none of this needed positivity of the inner product or any special field, the construction typechecks for an arbitrary field FF — the smoke test at the end of the file states exactly that:

Example ga_cliff1_check (F : Type) (FF : Field F) :
  GeometricAlgebra F F (C F) := GA_Cliff1.

So the abstract axiom “a vector squared is its length” is not vacuous: there is a concrete ring satisfying it, and it is one every reader already knows.
