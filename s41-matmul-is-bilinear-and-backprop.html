Matrix multiplication is a bilinear map, and backprop is associativity

Two elementary facts about matrix multiplication sit underneath much of how neural networks are trained. The first is that multiplying matrices is linear in each of the two matrices separately. The second is that it is associative. This post makes the first precise — matrix multiplication packaged as a bounded bilinear map, with a sharp norm bound and, as a consequence, a derivative — and then shows that the second is the whole content of what is called backpropagation: “forward mode” and “reverse mode” differentiation are two ways of parenthesising one associative product of matrices.

Bilinear, and bounded

A map that takes two arguments is bilinear when it is linear in each argument on its own: fix the second argument and it is an additive, scaling-respecting function of the first; fix the first and the same holds of the second. This is weaker and genuinely different from being linear on the pair — doubling both arguments of a bilinear map multiplies the result by four, not by two. Ordinary multiplication of two numbers is the smallest example; the dot product and matrix multiplication are the two that matter here.

A bilinear map is bounded when there is a single constant CC such that the size of the output is never more than CC times the sizes of the two inputs multiplied together — ‖B(x,y)‖≤C‖x‖‖y‖\lVert B(x, y)\rVert \le C\,\lVert x\rVert\,\lVert y\rVert for all xx and yy, with ‖⋅‖\lVert\cdot\rVert a norm measuring the size of a vector. That one inequality is what makes a bilinear map differentiable: the “error term” you get from perturbing both arguments at once is a product of two perturbations, and boundedness forces that product to shrink faster than either perturbation alone. So every bounded bilinear map is automatically differentiable, with its derivative given by the product rule — that is the result this post stands on, and it is proved once, abstractly, for all bounded bilinear maps at once. What remains is to show matrix multiplication is one of them.

mat_mul is a bilinear map

The five facts a BilinearMap needs — that the product respects equality, and is additive and scaling-respecting in the left matrix and in the right matrix — are already lemmas about mat_mul. Packaging them is the whole construction, from Theory/MatMulBilinear.v:

  Definition mat_mul_bilinear :
      BilinearMap F (Matrix m n F) (Matrix n p F) (Matrix m p F) :=
    @Build_BilinearMap F (Matrix m n F) (Matrix n p F) (Matrix m p F) _ _ _ _
      (@mat_mul F _ m n p)
      (mat_mul_proper m n p)
      (mat_mul_add_l m n p)
      (mat_mul_scale_l m n p)
      (mat_mul_add_r m n p)
      (mat_mul_scale_r m n p).

The four middle fields are the two-slots-of-linearity spelled out: mat_mul_add_l and mat_mul_scale_l are additivity and homogeneity in the left matrix, mat_mul_add_r and mat_mul_scale_r the same in the right, and mat_mul_proper is the fact that replacing either matrix by an equal one gives an equal product.

The sharp bound: the norm of a product is at most the product of the norms

The norm here is the entrywise L1L^1 norm: the size of a matrix is the sum of the absolute values of all its entries. With that measure, matrix multiplication satisfies ‖AB‖≤‖A‖‖B‖\lVert A B\rVert \le \lVert A\rVert\,\lVert B\rVert — the bound with the constant C=1C = 1 outright, no factor that grows with the dimensions. Stated in Theory/MatMulBilinear.v:

  Theorem mat_mul_norm_submultiplicative : forall (A : Matrix m n F)
      (B : Matrix n p F), ‖ mat_mul A B ‖ <= ‖ A ‖ * ‖ B ‖.

On the blackboard the argument is a chain of one inequality and three rearrangements — expand the product, push the absolute value through the inner sum, reorder the sums, and factor:

∥A⋅B∥=∑i,k|∑jAijBjk|≤∑i,k∑j|Aij||Bjk|=∑i∑j|Aij|(∑k|Bjk|)≤∑i∑j|Aij|∥B∥=∥A∥∥B∥. \|A\cdot B\| = \sum_{i,k}\Big|\sum_j A_{ij}B_{jk}\Big| \;\le\; \sum_{i,k}\sum_j |A_{ij}|\,|B_{jk}| \;=\; \sum_i\sum_j |A_{ij}|\Big(\sum_k |B_{jk}|\Big) \;\le\; \sum_i\sum_j |A_{ij}|\,\|B\| \;=\; \|A\|\,\|B\|.

The one inequality is the triangle inequality (the size of a sum is at most the sum of the sizes); the last inequality replaces one row’s absolute-value sum by ‖B‖\lVert B\rVert, the sum over all rows, which is only larger because the dropped terms are absolute values and so never negative. Nothing in the chain needs a padding factor, which is why the constant is exactly 11. And 11 cannot be lowered: for two 1×11 \times 1 matrices [a][a] and [b][b] the bound reads |ab|≤|a||b||ab| \le |a|\,|b|, an equality, so no smaller constant could hold in general. The constant is sharp.

Packaging that as the boundedness the differentiability result wants is four lines, and it is where the constant C = 1 is actually supplied:

  Theorem mat_mul_bm_bounded : bm_bounded mat_mul_bilinear.
  Proof.
    exists 1.
    split.
    - exact zero_le_one.
    - intros A B.
      rewrite ring_mul_1l.
      exact (mat_mul_norm_submultiplicative A B).
  Qed.

Walking it: exists 1 names the constant; split leaves two goals, that the constant is non-negative and that the bound holds with it. exact zero_le_one discharges 0≤10 \le 1. For the bound, intros A B fixes the two matrices, rewrite ring_mul_1l erases the leading factor 1⋅1 \cdot in front of ‖A‖\lVert A\rVert, and mat_mul_norm_submultiplicative is then exactly the goal.

The derivative of matrix multiplication

Now the consequence of “bounded bilinear implies differentiable,” specialised to mat_mul. Its derivative at a point (A0,B0)(A_0, B_0) is the linear map that sends a perturbation (dA,dB)(dA, dB) of both matrices to dA⋅B0+A0⋅dBdA \cdot B_0 + A_0 \cdot dB — the product rule, exactly as for numbers. That map is built abstractly by bilinear_deriv_lm, and the differentiability statement is closed by handing the abstract theorem the boundedness proof above:

  Definition mat_mul_deriv_lm (A0 : Matrix m n F) (B0 : Matrix n p F) :
      LinearMap F (Matrix m n F * Matrix n p F) (Matrix m p F) :=
    bilinear_deriv_lm mat_mul_bilinear A0 B0.

  Theorem mat_mul_is_deriv : forall (A0 : Matrix m n F) (B0 : Matrix n p F),
    is_deriv (fun AB : Matrix m n F * Matrix n p F => mat_mul (fst AB) (snd AB))
      (A0, B0) (mat_mul_deriv_lm A0 B0).
  Proof.
    intros A0 B0.
    exact (bilinear_is_deriv mat_mul_bilinear A0 B0 mat_mul_bm_bounded).
  Qed.

The proof is a single application: bilinear_is_deriv is the general “bounded bilinear maps are differentiable” theorem, and its three arguments are the bilinear map, the point, and — the only matrix-specific ingredient — mat_mul_bm_bounded. Every “multiply the input by a weight matrix” step in a neural-network layer is an instance of this one theorem.

Why backprop is associativity

Here is the second fact, and the reason for the title. Suppose three functions are composed, f3(f2(f1(a)))f_3(f_2(f_1(a))). The chain rule says the derivative of the composite is the composition of the three individual derivatives, and each derivative is a linear map — represented, once you pick coordinates, by a matrix, its Jacobian. So the derivative of the whole chain is a product of three Jacobians, L3⋅L2⋅L1L_3 \cdot L_2 \cdot L_1.

A product of three factors can be bracketed two ways, and matrix multiplication is composition of linear maps, which is associative. That correspondence — product of matrices is composition of the maps they denote — is itself one line, from Theory/MatVec.v, and its proof is just associativity viewed through a single column:

  Theorem mat_vec_mul : forall (m n p : nat) (A : Matrix m n F)
      (B : Matrix n p F) (v : Fin.t p -> F),
    mat_vec (mat_mul A B) v == mat_vec A (mat_vec B v).
  Proof.
    intros m n p A B v i.
    exact (mat_mul_assoc m n p 1 A B
             (fun (k : Fin.t p) (_ : Fin.t 1) => v k) i Fin.F1).
  Qed.

The two bracketings of the three-Jacobian product are the two strategies of automatic differentiation. Reverse mode — backpropagation — computes (L3⋅L2)⋅L1(L_3 \cdot L_2) \cdot L_1, starting from the output and working back toward the input. Forward mode computes L3⋅(L2⋅L1)L_3 \cdot (L_2 \cdot L_1), starting from the input and working toward the output. That they give the same answer is, underneath, nothing but the associativity of the matrix product. At the level of linear maps it is even more elementary — composition is literal function composition, so any two bracketings reduce to the same thing by computation alone, and the proof in Theory/HomVS.v is one word:

  Theorem lm_comp_assoc : forall (g : LinearMap F R S) (h : LinearMap F Q R)
      (k : LinearMap F P Q),
    lm_comp (lm_comp g h) k == lm_comp g (lm_comp h k).
  Proof.
    intros g h k u.
    reflexivity.
  Qed.

Lifted to the Jacobian matrices, the statement that backprop and forward mode compute the same derivative reads almost exactly like the associativity law itself, one level up — and its proof is that law, mat_mul_assoc, applied to the three Jacobians:

  Theorem backprop_eq_forward_mode :
    forall (f1 : U -> V) (f2 : V -> W) (f3 : W -> X) (a : U)
      (L1 : LinearMap F U V) (L2 : LinearMap F V W) (L3 : LinearMap F W X)
      (P2 : Proper (equ ==> equ) f2) (P3 : Proper (equ ==> equ) f3)
      (Hd1 : is_deriv f1 a L1) (Hd2 : is_deriv f2 (f1 a) L2)
      (Hd3 : is_deriv f3 (f2 (f1 a)) L3),
    mat_mul (mat_mul (jacobian p q BW BX Hd3) (jacobian m p BV BW Hd2))
      (jacobian n m BU BV Hd1)
    == mat_mul (jacobian p q BW BX Hd3)
         (mat_mul (jacobian m p BV BW Hd2) (jacobian n m BU BV Hd1)).
  Proof.
    intros f1 f2 f3 a L1 L2 L3 P2 P3 Hd1 Hd2 Hd3.
    exact (mat_mul_assoc q p m n (jacobian p q BW BX Hd3)
             (jacobian m p BV BW Hd2) (jacobian n m BU BV Hd1)).
  Qed.

The long list of hypotheses just says what the setup is: three functions, a point a, their three derivatives L1, L2, L3, and evidence (Hd1, Hd2, Hd3) that each LiL_i really is the derivative of its fif_i. The jacobian terms are the three Jacobian matrices. The left side is backprop’s bracketing, the right side forward mode’s, and the single line of proof reassociates the product. Reverse and forward mode are the same computation, parenthesised differently.

If they compute the same matrix, why prefer one? Because the two bracketings do not cost the same to evaluate — one can form a much smaller intermediate than the other. This file states that the two orders agree but does not put a number on the difference; the next post does, for the matrix product at the heart of an attention layer, where reassociating the very same way turns out to save a factor of sixteen at a realistic size.
