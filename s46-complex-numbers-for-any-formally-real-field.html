Complex numbers over any formally real field

A field is a set with addition and multiplication behaving the way they do in the rationals: both are associative and commutative, multiplication distributes over addition, there is a 0 and a 1, and — the property that makes it a field rather than merely a ring — every element except 0 has a multiplicative inverse. That last clause is what lets you divide: inv x is the thing you multiply x by to get 1, and it exists for every x that is not zero.

Now try to build the complex numbers on top of an arbitrary field F. Represent a complex number as a pair (a, b), meaning a+bia + b\,i. Adding and multiplying such pairs is straightforward algebra. The interesting operation is division, because the inverse of a+bia + b\,i is

a−bia2+b2.\frac{a - b\,i}{a^2 + b^2}.

That formula divides by a2+b2a^2 + b^2. For it to make sense whenever a+bia + b\,i is nonzero, we need a2+b2a^2 + b^2 to be nonzero whenever aa and bb are not both zero. Over the rationals that is obvious — a sum of two squares is a sum of two nonnegative numbers, zero only when both are. But over an arbitrary field it can fail: in a field where −1-1 is itself a square, say −1=j2-1 = j^2, the sum 12+j2=1+(−1)=01^2 + j^2 = 1 + (-1) = 0 vanishes with neither summand zero, and the formula divides by zero. So building complex numbers over F needs a hypothesis, and the hypothesis is exactly the one that rules this out.

Formal reality: the one-line hypothesis

The property is called formal reality. Here is its entire definition, from Interface/FormallyReal.v:

Class FormallyReal (F : Type) `{Field F} :=
  fr_sos : forall a b : F, a * a + b * b == 0 -> a == 0.

One axiom, fr_sos (“sum of squares”): if a*a + b*b == 0, then a == 0. By the symmetry of the statement b must vanish too — that companion fact is fr_sos_r in Theory/Field.v, proved in three lines by commuting the two squares and appealing to fr_sos. Equivalently: −1-1 is not a sum of two squares. The rationals Q and the reals R are formally real, because their order makes every square nonnegative and a sum of nonnegatives zero only when each is zero. The complex numbers are famously not formally real (12+i2=01^2 + i^2 = 0) — which is the whole reason i cannot itself live in a formally real F.

This is precisely the missing fact from the division formula, and nothing more. We are not asking F to be ordered, or complete, or the rationals specifically — only that a sum of two squares cannot vanish unless both squares do.

The construction

Complex numbers over F are pairs with named projections, from Impl/Complex.v:

Record C (F : Type) := mkC { re : F; im : F }.

Two of them are equal when their real and imaginary parts are equal componentwise — this is why every proof below splits a goal about C F into a real-part obligation and an imaginary-part obligation:

#[global] Instance C_EqRel : EqRel (C F) :=
  fun z w => re z == re w /\ im z == im w.

Multiplication is the ordinary rule (a+bi)(c+di)=(ac−bd)+(ad+bc)i(a + b\,i)(c + d\,i) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)\,i, written componentwise:

#[global] Instance C_Mult : HasMult (C F) :=
  fun z w => mkC (re z * re w + - (im z * im w))
                 (re z * im w + im z * re w).

The quantity a2+b2a^2 + b^2 gets a name of its own, norm2, and the inverse is the division formula from the top, spelled out in components — real part a⋅(a2+b2)−1a \cdot (a^2+b^2)^{-1}, imaginary part −b⋅(a2+b2)−1-b \cdot (a^2+b^2)^{-1}:

Definition norm2 (z : C F) : F := re z * re z + im z * im z.

(* inv 0 is garbage (inv of 0 in F); the field law only speaks for
   z =/= 0, where norm2 z is invertible by formal reality. *)
#[global] Instance C_Inv : HasInv (C F) :=
  fun z => mkC (re z * inv (norm2 z)) (- im z * inv (norm2 z)).

For z = 0 this divides by inv (norm2 0) = inv 0, which is garbage — but the field law only ever speaks about inv z for z nonzero, and there norm2 z is invertible. Making that precise is where formal reality does its work.

Where the hypothesis enters

The bridge is a single lemma: away from the origin, norm2 z is nonzero. On the blackboard, writing z=a+biz = a + b\,i: suppose z≠0z \neq 0 and, for contradiction, a2+b2=0a^2 + b^2 = 0. Then

a2+b2=0⇒a=0 and b=0⇒z=0,a^2 + b^2 = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; a = 0 \;\text{ and }\; b = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; z = 0,

where the first implication is formal reality (once for aa, once for bb by symmetry) — contradicting z≠0z \neq 0. The proof of this lemma is the only place in the whole construction that touches fr_sos.

Lemma norm2_nonzero : forall z : C F, z =/= 0 -> norm2 z =/= 0.
Proof.
  intros z Hz Hn.
  apply Hz. split.
  - exact (fr_sos (re z) (im z) Hn).
  - exact (fr_sos_r (re z) (im z) Hn).
Qed.

Read it by contradiction, which is how the =/= (meaning “not equal”) goals unfold. Assume z =/= 0 and, for contradiction, norm2 z == 0 (that is Hn). To derive the contradiction we show z == 0, i.e. both components vanish: fr_sos gives re z == 0 from Hn, and its companion fr_sos_r gives im z == 0. Together they contradict Hz. Without formal reality this lemma is simply false, and everything below collapses.

The inverse law, in full

Now the field law that formal reality was for: inv z * z == 1 for z nonzero. Multiplying (aN−1)−(bN−1)i(a N^{-1}) - (b N^{-1})\,i by a+bia + b\,i, where N=a2+b2N = a^2 + b^2, the real component runs

a2N−1+b2N−1=(a2+b2)N−1{distributivity}=NN−1{N=a2+b2 by definition}=1{inverse law — the step that needs N≠0}\begin{aligned} a^2 N^{-1} + b^2 N^{-1} &= (a^2+b^2)\,N^{-1} && \{\text{distributivity}\}\\ &= N\,N^{-1} && \{N = a^2 + b^2 \text{ by definition}\}\\ &= 1 && \{\text{inverse law — the step that needs } N \neq 0\} \end{aligned}

and the imaginary component cancels by commutativity alone:

abN−1−baN−1=0.ab\,N^{-1} - ba\,N^{-1} = 0.

The real part is where N≠0N \neq 0 is spent — it is the step NN−1=1N\,N^{-1} = 1. The C-level law splits into exactly those two components:

Lemma c_invl : forall z : C F, z =/= 0 -> inv z * z == 1.
Proof.
  intros z Hz. split.
  - apply (cinv_re (re z) (im z) (norm2 z)).
    + exact (norm2_nonzero z Hz).
    + reflexivity.
  - exact (cinv_im (re z) (im z) (norm2 z)).
Qed.

The split produces the real-part goal and the imaginary-part goal. The real part is discharged by a helper cinv_re applied at n := norm2 z, which asks for two things: that n =/= 0 (supplied by norm2_nonzero z Hz — the hypothesis, cashed in) and that a*a + b*b == n (true by reflexivity, since norm2 z is defined as a*a + b*b). The imaginary part is the helper cinv_im, which needs no hypothesis at all — the imaginary component cancels for algebraic reasons alone.

The helper cinv_re is where the blackboard real-part chain actually runs:

Lemma cinv_re : forall a b n : F,
  n =/= 0 -> a * a + b * b == n ->
  (a * inv n) * a + - ((- b * inv n) * b) == 1.
Proof.
  intros a b n Hn Heq.
  rewrite (mul_neg_l b (inv n)), (mul_neg_l (b * inv n) b), neg_involutive.
  rewrite (mul_inv_shuffle a n), (mul_inv_shuffle b n), <- ring_distl, Heq.
  exact (field_invl n Hn).
Qed.

Walking it against the blackboard chain:

  • The first rewrite line clears the double negation. - b * inv n is -(b * inv n), so (- b * inv n) * b is -((b * inv n) * b), and the outer negation in front of it cancels by neg_involutive. The expression is now (a * inv n) * a + (b * inv n) * b.
  • mul_inv_shuffle at a and at b reassociates each term (x⋅n−1)⋅x(x \cdot n^{-1}) \cdot x into n−1⋅(x⋅x)n^{-1} \cdot (x \cdot x), giving n−1(a⋅a)+n−1(b⋅b)n^{-1}(a \cdot a) + n^{-1}(b \cdot b).
  • <- ring_distl factors the shared n−1n^{-1} back out (distributivity, used right-to-left): n−1(a⋅a+b⋅b)n^{-1}(a \cdot a + b \cdot b).
  • Heq replaces a⋅a+b⋅ba \cdot a + b \cdot b with nn, leaving n−1⋅nn^{-1} \cdot n.
  • field_invl n Hn is the field law that inv n * n == 1 — and this is the one step that consumes Hn, the proof that n is nonzero.

That single use of field_invl, reachable only because norm2_nonzero supplied Hn, is the entire dependence of the complex field on formal reality. Every other field law for C F — associativity, distributivity, commutativity, the additive group structure — is proved over Field F alone, with no mention of order or formal reality.

The complex rationals, for free

Assemble all the laws and you get an instance C_Field : Field (C F) that holds whenever F is a formally real field. Nothing in it is specific to any particular F. So to obtain the complex rationals as a genuine field, the only new obligation is the one hypothesis: that Q is formally real. That single instance lives in Impl/Qof.v, proved from Q’s order (a rational square is nonnegative, a sum of two nonnegative rationals is zero only when both are):

#[global] Instance Q_FormallyReal : FormallyReal Q := Qfr_sos.

And then, in the same file, the complex rationals are a field with no further construction — the underscore asks Coq to find the field structure, and its instance resolution assembles it, reaching for Q_FormallyReal at exactly the point the inverse law needs it:

Example cq_field_check : Field (C Q) := _.

The file compiles as it stands, so the _ really is filled by resolution alone. That makes the point of the post concrete: register one fact about the rationals — a sum of two rational squares vanishes only at zero — and the complex rationals, with working addition, multiplication, and division, appear at once, without a single line of code written specifically for them.
