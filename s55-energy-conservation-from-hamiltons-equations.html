Energy conservation, from Hamilton’s equations

A body moves along a line under a force that depends only on where the body is — a weight bobbing on a spring, a ball rolling in a bowl. Nothing about the motion holds still: the position changes at every instant, the speed changes at every instant, the momentum changes at every instant. Yet one number computed from all of them together never changes. Add the kinetic energy, which depends on the momentum, to the potential energy, which depends on the position, and the sum at the end of the motion equals the sum at the start, to as many decimal places as you care to check.

Physics books often present this as a law of nature, confirmed by experiment. It is something better than that: given the equations of motion, it is a theorem. Write q(t)q(t) for the body’s position at time tt, p(t)p(t) for its momentum, mm for its mass, and VV for the potential — the function whose downhill slope at each position is the force there. Hamilton’s equations say how the state (q,p)(q, p) moves:

q̇=pm,ṗ=−V′(q).\dot q = \frac{p}{m}, \qquad \dot p = -V'(q).

The first says momentum is mass times velocity, read as a statement about q̇\dot q; the second says the momentum changes at exactly the rate the force dictates. The conserved number is the total energy

H=p22m+V(q),H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(q),

and the claim is that along any motion obeying the two equations,

ddt(p(t)22m+V(q(t)))=0.\frac{d}{dt}\Bigl(\frac{p(t)^2}{2m} + V(q(t))\Bigr) = 0.

The whole argument fits on two lines:

ddt(p22m+V(q))=pṗm+V′(q)q̇(product rule; chain rule)\frac{d}{dt}\Bigl(\frac{p^2}{2m} + V(q)\Bigr) \;=\; \frac{p\,\dot p}{m} + V'(q)\,\dot q \qquad\text{(product rule; chain rule)}

=p(−V′(q))m+V′(q)pm=0.(Hamilton’s equations; cancellation)\;=\; \frac{p\,\bigl(-V'(q)\bigr)}{m} + V'(q)\,\frac{p}{m} \;=\; 0. \qquad\text{(Hamilton's equations; cancellation)}

Differentiate term by term, substitute the two equations of motion, and the two summands are the same quantity with opposite signs. Nothing else is needed — no integration, no physical reasoning, no appeal to experiment. This post is that two-line computation, machine-checked, and an account of what it takes to make each of its ingredients — “derivative”, “product rule”, “chain rule” — precise enough for a proof checker to accept.

A derivative is a linear map; a scalar derivative is a number

Two earlier posts in this series set the analytic scene, and three sentences recap what this one needs. An ordered field is a field equipped with a linear order compatible with addition and multiplication, and with an absolute value; the rationals are the model to keep in mind, and everything below works over any such field, written FF. A norm assigns each vector a nonnegative “length” in FF, and FF itself is a normed space over itself, with the absolute value as the norm. The derivative of a function between normed spaces at a point is not a number but a bounded linear map — the best linear approximation of the function near that point — and this is the definition everything below builds on, from Theory/Derivative.v:

  Definition is_deriv (f : U -> V) (a : U) (L : LinearMap F U V) : Prop :=
    lm_bounded L /\
    forall eps : F, 0 < eps ->
      exists delta : F, 0 < delta /\
        forall h : U, ‖ h ‖ < delta ->
          ‖ f (a + h) + - (f a + L h) ‖ <= eps * ‖ h ‖.

Read it as: the error made by the linear prediction f(a)+L(h)f(a) + L(h) is below any chosen fraction ε\varepsilon of ‖h‖\lVert h \rVert, once hh is small enough. (lm_bounded L says some constant C≥0C \ge 0 has ‖Lu‖≤C‖u‖\lVert L\,u \rVert \le C \lVert u \rVert for all uu; the ‖ ‖ brackets are the norm; == throughout is each type’s chosen equivalence relation, the setoid equality earlier posts built everything on.) The estimate is multiplied out — ε⋅‖h‖\varepsilon \cdot \lVert h \rVert on the right rather than a quotient on the left — so no division ever happens, and h=0h = 0 needs no special exemption.

For a function F→FF \to F, the classical derivative is a single number cc, and the two views meet in the simplest possible way: the linear maps F→FF \to F are exactly the maps “multiply by cc”. That map is packaged once, in Theory/MLDerivatives.v, parameterized by the scalar c:

  Definition scale_map (h : F) : F := c * h.
  Definition lm_scale_map : LinearMap F F F :=
    @Build_LinearMap F F F _ Field_VectorSpace Field_VectorSpace scale_map
      scale_map_proper scale_map_add scale_map_scale.

So “f′(a)=cf'(a) = c” is rendered is_deriv f a (lm_scale_map c), and “the derivative vanishes” is is_deriv f a lm_const0, where lm_const0 is the everywhere-zero linear map — the derivative every constant function has. Every derivative statement in this post has exactly one of these two shapes.

The setting: a mass, a potential, three paths, two equations

Everything in Theory/Hamiltonian.v lives in one Coq section, and the physics enters as that section’s hypotheses. First the data:

  Context (m : F) (V Vd : F -> F) (q p v : F -> F).
  Context (V_proper : Proper (equ ==> equ) V).
  Context (potential_deriv : forall x : F, is_deriv V x (lm_scale_map (Vd x))).

Here m is the mass, V the potential, and Vd its pointwise derivative — supplied as a function, with potential_deriv asserting that at every point xx the derivative of VV is multiplication by Vd x; in blackboard terms, V′(x)V'(x) exists everywhere and Vd is the function x↦V′(x)x \mapsto V'(x). The three paths are q (position), p (momentum), and v (velocity), each a function of time. The Proper hypothesis says V sends equal inputs (in the == sense) to equal outputs; the chain rule needs it to move V across an equality of arguments, and any concrete potential built from field operations satisfies it automatically.

Then the equations of motion, one hypothesis per equation:

  Context (hamilton_q : forall t : F, is_deriv q t (lm_scale_map (v t))).
  Context (hamilton_p : forall t : F, is_deriv p t (lm_scale_map (- Vd (q t)))).
  Context (momentum : forall t : F, m * v t == p t).

hamilton_q renders q̇(t)=v(t)\dot q(t) = v(t): the derivative of the position path at tt is scaling by the velocity there. momentum renders mv(t)=p(t)m\,v(t) = p(t). Together the two say q̇=p/m\dot q = p/m — Hamilton’s first equation — without ever writing a division. hamilton_p renders ṗ(t)=−V′(q(t))\dot p(t) = -V'(q(t)), Hamilton’s second equation, with Vd standing in for V′V'.

Why avoid the division? In an arbitrary field, p/mp/m only makes sense under the extra hypothesis m≠0m \neq 0, and the same problem infects the energy itself: p2/2mp^2/2m divides by 2m2m. The file’s policy is to multiply the conserved quantity through by 2m2m first:

  Definition energy (t : F) : F := p t * p t + (two * m) * V (q t).

This is 2mH=p2+2mV(q)2m H = p^2 + 2m\,V(q), a polynomial in the data — no inverse anywhere. (two is the field element 1+11 + 1, and 2≠02 \neq 0 is a theorem of every ordered field, two_neq_0 in Theory/Order.v.) Multiplying by a constant does not change whether a quantity is constant in time, so nothing is lost: the main theorem says this energy has derivative zero with no invertibility hypothesis at all — it even holds, degenerately, for m=0m = 0 — and the customary H=p2/2m+V(q)H = p^2/2m + V(q) is recovered as a corollary the moment m≠0m \neq 0 is assumed.

The product rule, derived rather than assumed

The blackboard computation leans on the product rule: if f′(a)=cf'(a) = c and g′(a)=dg'(a) = d, then

(f⋅g)′(a)=c⋅g(a)+f(a)⋅d.(f \cdot g)'(a) = c \cdot g(a) + f(a) \cdot d.

The derivative file proves rules for constants, bounded linear maps, sums, scalings, negations, and composites — but no product rule for two scalar functions. Rather than run a new ε\varepsilon–δ\delta estimate from scratch, Theory/Hamiltonian.v assembles the product rule from a fact an earlier post established about bilinear maps — maps of two arguments, linear in each argument separately, like multiplication itself. A bounded bilinear map BB is automatically differentiable, with derivative at (u0,v0)(u_0, v_0) the linear map

(h,k)↦B(h,v0)+B(u0,k),(h, k) \;\longmapsto\; B(h, v_0) + B(u_0, k),

because the error of that approximation is exactly B(h,k)B(h,k), a quantity quadratically small in the increment. That fact is bilinear_is_deriv in Theory/BilinearDeriv.v; multiplication qualifies because |u⋅v|=|u|⋅|v||u \cdot v| = |u| \cdot |v| makes it bounded with constant 11 (packaged as mul_bm and mul_bm_bounded in the Hamiltonian file).

To apply it to f⋅gf \cdot g, write the product as a composite: first the pairing t↦(f(t),g(t))t \mapsto (f(t), g(t)), then multiplication (u,v)↦u⋅v(u,v) \mapsto u \cdot v. The pairing needs its own derivative lemma. In a section parameterized by two linear maps Lf Lg : LinearMap F F F, with lm_pair the paired map h↦(Lfh,Lgh)h \mapsto (L_f\,h,\; L_g\,h):

  Lemma deriv_pair : forall (f g : F -> F) (a : F),
    is_deriv f a Lf -> is_deriv g a Lg ->
    is_deriv (fun t : F => (f t, g t)) a lm_pair.

Its proof is the same ε/2\varepsilon/2 argument that proved the sum rule: under the product space’s sum norm, the pairing’s approximation error is the two component errors added together, so bounding each by ε2‖h‖\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\lVert h \rVert bounds the pair by ε‖h‖\varepsilon \lVert h \rVert. With the pairing differentiated and multiplication differentiated, the chain rule deriv_comp — the derivative of a composite is the composite of the derivatives — does the rest. On the blackboard: the pairing’s derivative sends hh to (ch,dh)(c\,h,\, d\,h), multiplication’s derivative at (f(a),g(a))(f(a), g(a)) sends (h′,k′)(h', k') to h′g(a)+f(a)k′h' g(a) + f(a) k', and chaining them sends hh to

ch⋅g(a)+f(a)⋅dh=(c⋅g(a)+f(a)⋅d)h,c\,h \cdot g(a) + f(a) \cdot d\,h \;=\; \bigl(c \cdot g(a) + f(a) \cdot d\bigr)\,h,

which is scaling by the product-rule scalar. Here is the Coq version, in full:

  Theorem deriv_mul : forall (f g : F -> F) (a : F) (fd gd : F),
    is_deriv f a (lm_scale_map fd) -> is_deriv g a (lm_scale_map gd) ->
    is_deriv (fun t : F => f t * g t) a
      (lm_scale_map (fd * g a + f a * gd)).
  Proof.
    intros f g a fd gd HDf HDg.
    apply (is_deriv_L_equ (fun t : F => f t * g t) a
      (lm_comp (bilinear_deriv_lm mul_bm (f a) (g a))
               (lm_pair (lm_scale_map fd) (lm_scale_map gd)))).
    - (* the composite linear map is scaling by fd * g a + f a * gd;
         the change is pure conversion (beta plus record projections) *)
      intros h.
      change ((fd * h) * g a + f a * (gd * h)
                == (fd * g a + f a * gd) * h).
      rewrite (mul_assoc_comm_r fd h (g a)),
        <- (ring_mul_assoc (f a) gd h),
        <- (ring_distr (fd * g a) (f a * gd) h).
      reflexivity.
    - exact (deriv_comp (fun t : F => (f t, g t))
        (fun pr : F * F => mul_bm (fst pr) (snd pr)) a
        (lm_pair (lm_scale_map fd) (lm_scale_map gd))
        (bilinear_deriv_lm mul_bm (f a) (g a))
        mul_pair_proper
        (deriv_pair (lm_scale_map fd) (lm_scale_map gd) f g a HDf HDg)
        (bilinear_is_deriv mul_bm (f a) (g a) mul_bm_bounded)).
  Qed.

The walk. is_deriv_L_equ transports a derivative fact across a pointwise equality of linear maps — is_deriv only ever evaluates its linear map, so two maps agreeing at every point are interchangeable in it. The proof therefore splits in two. The second bullet is the chain rule applied to the pairing’s derivative (deriv_pair) and multiplication’s derivative (bilinear_is_deriv) — the composition step of the blackboard argument, with mul_pair_proper supplying the respects-equality side condition deriv_comp asks of its outer function. The first bullet is the displayed algebra: the composite map applied to h computes, by mere unfolding (change), to (fd * h) * g a + f a * (gd * h), and three rewrites — reassociate, reassociate, undistribute — reshape it into (fd * g a + f a * gd) * h, the product-rule scalar times h. No new estimate appears anywhere; the only ε\varepsilon in the whole derivation lives inside deriv_pair.

The conservation theorem

Now the blackboard version of the main computation, division-free, each step labeled:

ddt(p2+2mV(q))=ṗp+pṗ+2mV′(q)q̇(product rule; chain rule)\frac{d}{dt}\bigl(p^2 + 2m\,V(q)\bigr) \;=\; \dot p\,p + p\,\dot p \;+\; 2m\,V'(q)\,\dot q \qquad\text{(product rule; chain rule)}

=(−V′(q))p+p(−V′(q))+2mV′(q)v(Hamilton’s equations)\;=\; \bigl(-V'(q)\bigr)\,p + p\,\bigl(-V'(q)\bigr) \;+\; 2m\,V'(q)\,v \qquad\text{(Hamilton's equations)}

=−2V′(q)p+2V′(q)(mv)=−2V′(q)p+2V′(q)p=0.(mv=p; cancellation)\;=\; -2\,V'(q)\,p + 2\,V'(q)\,(m\,v) \;=\; -2\,V'(q)\,p + 2\,V'(q)\,p \;=\; 0. \qquad\text{(}m v = p\text{; cancellation)}

The Coq proof follows the same three moves, one lemma per move. The kinetic term is the product rule applied to p with itself — and because hamilton_p already names p’s derivative, the equations of motion are substituted in the same breath:

  Lemma kinetic_deriv : forall t : F,
    is_deriv (fun s : F => p s * p s) t
      (lm_scale_map ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t)))).
  Proof.
    intros t.
    exact (deriv_mul p p t (- Vd (q t)) (- Vd (q t))
      (hamilton_p t) (hamilton_p t)).
  Qed.

One application of deriv_mul, nothing else: this is ddtp2=ṗp+pṗ\frac{d}{dt}p^2 = \dot p\,p + p\,\dot p with ṗ=−V′(q)\dot p = -V'(q) already in place. The potential term is the chain rule along the path — ddtV(q(t))=V′(q(t))⋅q̇(t)=V′(q(t))⋅v(t)\frac{d}{dt} V(q(t)) = V'(q(t)) \cdot \dot q(t) = V'(q(t)) \cdot v(t) — stated as:

  Lemma potential_along_path_deriv : forall t : F,
    is_deriv (fun s : F => V (q s)) t (lm_scale_map (Vd (q t) * v t)).

(its proof composes hamilton_q with potential_deriv via deriv_comp, then collapses the composite of two scalings into one), and the constant 2m2m passes through the derivative by one more composition with the scaling map:

  Lemma scaled_potential_deriv : forall t : F,
    is_deriv (fun s : F => (two * m) * V (q s)) t
      (lm_scale_map ((two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t))).

That accounts for the first line of the blackboard chain, with the second line’s substitutions already made. What remains is the third line — pure algebra, no analysis: the two contributions add to zero. Writing A=V′(q(t))A = V'(q(t)) and P=p(t)P = p(t), the claim is

((−A)P+P(−A))+2m(Av)=−(AP+AP)+2A(mv)=−(AP+AP)+(AP+AP)=0,\bigl((-A)\,P + P\,(-A)\bigr) + 2m\,(A\,v) \;=\; -(A P + A P) + 2\,A\,(m v) \;=\; -(A P + A P) + (A P + A P) \;=\; 0,

and its Coq form is:

  Lemma hamilton_cancel : forall t : F,
    ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t))) + (two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t)
      == 0.
  Proof.
    intros t.
    rewrite (mul_neg_l (Vd (q t)) (p t)),
      (mul_neg_r (p t) (Vd (q t))),
      (field_mul_comm (p t) (Vd (q t))),
      <- (neg_add (Vd (q t) * p t) (Vd (q t) * p t)),
      (ring_mul_assoc two m (Vd (q t) * v t)),
      (mul_comm_swap m (Vd (q t)) (v t)),
      (momentum t),
      (field_mul_comm two (Vd (q t) * p t)),
      (mul_two (Vd (q t) * p t)).
    exact (grp_invl (Vd (q t) * p t + Vd (q t) * p t)).
  Qed.

The walk, rewrite by rewrite. The first three rewrites pull the two minus signs out of the kinetic contribution and commute one factor, turning it into the sum of two copies of APA P, each negated; the fourth folds the two negations into one, giving −(AP+AP)-(A P + A P). The fifth and sixth reassociate the potential contribution 2m⋅(Av)2m \cdot (A v) into 2⋅A⋅(mv)2 \cdot A \cdot (m v) — and momentum then replaces mvm v by PP, the single point where a Hamilton equation enters this lemma. The last two rewrites unfold 2⋅(AP)2 \cdot (A P) into AP+APA P + A P. The goal is now −(AP+AP)+(AP+AP)=0-(A P + A P) + (A P + A P) = 0, which is grp_invl — the group law that a sum of an element’s negation with the element is zero.

The headline theorem stacks the three lemmas:

  Theorem energy_conserved : forall t : F, is_deriv energy t lm_const0.
  Proof.
    intros t.
    apply (is_deriv_L_equ energy t
      (lm_scale_map ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t)))
         + lm_scale_map ((two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t)))).
    - intros h.
      calc ((lm_scale_map ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t)))
               + lm_scale_map ((two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t))) h)
        == ((((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t)))
               + (two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t)) * h)
        == ((0 : F) * h)
        == (0 : F).
      + exact (lm_add_scale_scale ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t)))
          ((two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t)) h).
      + apply ring_mul_proper; [ exact (hamilton_cancel t) | reflexivity ].
      + exact (mul_0_l h).
    - exact (deriv_add (fun s : F => p s * p s)
        (fun s : F => (two * m) * V (q s)) t
        (lm_scale_map ((- Vd (q t)) * p t + p t * (- Vd (q t))))
        (lm_scale_map ((two * m) * (Vd (q t) * v t)))
        (kinetic_deriv t) (scaled_potential_deriv t)).
  Qed.

The walk. energy is a sum of two functions, so the sum rule deriv_add (second bullet) gives its derivative as the sum of the two linear maps established by kinetic_deriv and scaled_potential_deriv — the first blackboard line, complete. It remains to identify that sum of maps with the zero map, and is_deriv_L_equ reduces this to a pointwise check, carried out by the first bullet’s calc chain (calc writes a proof as a chain of equalities, one justification per step, exactly as on the blackboard). Step one: a sum of two scaling maps applied to h is the sum of the scalars times h — the lemma lm_add_scale_scale, which is distributivity read backwards. Step two: that sum of scalars is 0, by hamilton_cancel — the third blackboard line. Step three: 0 * h == 0. So the derivative map sends every h to 0, which is lm_const0, and the energy’s rate of change vanishes at every time t.

Newton’s second law, and the divided Hamiltonian

Two corollaries round out the file. First, the two Hamilton equations compose into Newton’s second law, ddt(mv)=−V′(q)\frac{d}{dt}(m v) = -V'(q) — force equals rate of change of momentum — again with no division:

  Theorem newton_second_law : forall t : F,
    is_deriv (fun s : F => m * v s) t (lm_scale_map (- Vd (q t))).
  Proof.
    intros t.
    apply (is_deriv_fun_equ p (fun s : F => m * v s) t
      (lm_scale_map (- Vd (q t)))).
    - intros x. symmetry. exact (momentum x).
    - exact (hamilton_p t).
  Qed.

The function s↦mv(s)s \mapsto m\,v(s) agrees with p at every point — that is exactly momentum — and is_deriv_fun_equ says a derivative fact transfers between everywhere-equal functions (the definition only ever evaluates the function, so agreement at every point is all it can see). So p’s equation of motion is Newton’s law for mvm v.

Second, the customary Hamiltonian with its division restored:

  Definition hamiltonian (t : F) : F := p t * p t * inv (two * m) + V (q t).

Under the single extra hypothesis m =/= 0 (from which 2m≠02m \neq 0 follows, since 2≠02 \neq 0 always holds in an ordered field), the file first checks that HH is the energy divided by 2m2m, pointwise:

    Lemma hamiltonian_from_energy : forall t : F,
      inv (two * m) * energy t == hamiltonian t.

and then concludes, since H=12mEH = \frac{1}{2m} E gives Ḣ=12mĖ=0\dot H = \frac{1}{2m} \dot E = 0:

    Theorem hamiltonian_conserved : forall t : F,
      is_deriv hamiltonian t lm_const0.

The proof is two transports: the chain rule composes energy_conserved with the scaling map for 12m\frac{1}{2m} — a constant multiple of a zero derivative is a zero derivative — and is_deriv_fun_equ moves the result across the pointwise identity above. The division was never needed for the conservation itself; it is cosmetic, and it costs exactly one hypothesis.

What a user supplies

The section hypotheses are the theorem’s interface. Here is the complete block, as it stands in the file:

  (* The data: mass, potential with its pointwise derivative, and the
     three paths.  V's Proper hypothesis is required by deriv_comp
     (see Theory/Derivative.v's header: it cannot be derived from
     is_deriv itself); every concrete potential built from the algebra
     satisfies it. *)
  Context (m : F) (V Vd : F -> F) (q p v : F -> F).
  Context (V_proper : Proper (equ ==> equ) V).
  Context (potential_deriv : forall x : F, is_deriv V x (lm_scale_map (Vd x))).

  (* Hamilton's equations, division-free: q' = v with m·v = p (i.e.
     q' = p/m without dividing), and p' = - V'(q). *)
  Context (hamilton_q : forall t : F, is_deriv q t (lm_scale_map (v t))).
  Context (hamilton_p : forall t : F, is_deriv p t (lm_scale_map (- Vd (q t)))).
  Context (momentum : forall t : F, m * v t == p t).

To apply energy_conserved, instantiate the section: choose an ordered field FF (the rationals qualify; so does anything satisfying the reals specification from earlier in the series), choose a mass, a potential together with its derivative function, and three concrete paths, and prove the five hypotheses for them. Each derivative hypothesis has the same shape the derivative rules produce (an is_deriv fact whose linear map is an lm_scale_map), so the rules seen above — constant, linear, sum, scaling, composite, product — are precisely the tools for discharging them; for a free particle, say, with the potential constantly zero, they reduce to the constant and linear-map rules alone. The file itself contains no worked instance yet, so there is no Example to quote or output to show — the concrete usage is the instantiation recipe just described.

Because every physical and analytic assumption is a section hypothesis rather than an axiom, the theorems close over nothing hidden. The end of the file records the check:

Print Assumptions lists every axiom a proof term depends on; “closed under the global context” is Coq’s phrase for the empty list.
