Symmetry groups live inside geometric algebras

The problem: where does a rotation actually live?

Take a flat plane and turn it around a fixed point by some angle. This is a transformation: a rule sending every point of the plane to another point, leaving distances and the center point alone. Do two such turns one after another and the result is again a turn, by the sum of the two angles. Turning by zero does nothing, and turning by −θ-\theta after θ\theta undoes it. Closure under composition, an identity, and an inverse for every element are exactly the three laws that make “all the turns of a plane” a group — the same three laws that any collection of symmetries obeys, whether the symmetries are turns of a plane, turns of a cube, or rearrangements of a deck of cards.

But “a group” is only a list of laws the elements must obey. It says nothing about what the elements are, as things one could write down and compute with. The standard answer from a first linear-algebra course is: represent each turn by a matrix, and compose two turns by multiplying the matrices. That works, but the representation is borrowed rather than native — matrices were built for linear maps of every kind, and a 2×22\times 2 rotation matrix stores four numbers to record what is really one number, an angle.

Suppose instead that the plane’s own directed line segments — its vectors — could be multiplied, not just added and scaled, by a rule general enough to make sense in any number of dimensions. Would restricting that multiplication to the right subset of the results reproduce the turns of the plane exactly, with two turns composing by nothing more than ordinary multiplication of two such objects? And in three dimensions, where a turn needs an axis as well as an angle, does the same idea produce the turns of space, together with a fact that has no counterpart in the plane at all — that turning something by a full circle and not turning it at all are, at the level of the object doing the turning, two different operations? Both questions get exact, checked answers below.

Multiplying vectors: the geometric product, briefly

A vector space’s vectors add and scale; nothing so far lets two of them multiply. A geometric algebra adds exactly one such multiplication: the vectors are embedded into a ring — addition and multiplication, the latter not required to commute — subject to a single rule, that a vector multiplied by itself gives back its own squared length, evev=∥v∥2e_v e_v = \|v\|^2, sitting as a plain number on the ring’s unit. Splitting the product of two different vectors into its symmetric and antisymmetric halves recovers their ordinary inner product on the symmetric side, and on the antisymmetric side a new quantity built from nothing but the multiplication rule: an oriented plane element, a bivector, spanned by the two vectors. (Here == is the algebra’s own equality — a chosen equivalence relation every operation must respect — not Coq’s equality of raw terms.)

The smallest case with room for an actual rotation takes the vector space to be the ordinary plane, with two orthonormal vectors e1,e2e_1, e_2: e1e1=e2e2=1e_1e_1 = e_2e_2 = 1, and because the two are orthogonal, e1e2=−e2e1e_1e_2 = -e_2e_1. Multiplying pairs of these two vectors, and pairs of the results, closes up a four-dimensional ring — 1, e1e_1, e2e_2, and the bivector e12:=e1e2e_{12} := e_1e_2 — called Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0) (Impl/Cliff2.v). Its carrier, C2 F, is a four-field record whose fields c2_s, c2_1, c2_2, c2_12 are the coefficients of 1,e1,e2,e121, e_1, e_2, e_{12}. Half of those four dimensions — 1 and e12e_{12} alone, the even part, built from an even number of vector factors — is closed under multiplication:

  Definition c2_even (x : C2 F) : Prop := c2_1 x == 0 /\ c2_2 x == 0.

and that even part turns out to match the complex numbers exactly.

The plane’s even part is the complex numbers

The two directions of the correspondence read off, or rebuild, the even part’s two surviving coordinates against a complex number’s real and imaginary parts:

  Definition c2_to_c (x : C2 F) : C F := mkC (c2_s x) (c2_12 x).
  Definition c_to_c2 (z : C F) : C2 F := mkC2 (re z) 0 0 (im z).

They are mutually inverse exactly on the even part. Round-tripping a general element of C2 F through c_to_c2 forgets its vector coordinates, so that direction only holds under c2_even; the other direction needs no hypothesis at all, because c_to_c2’s image is always even:

  Lemma c_to_c2_to_c : forall x : C2 F, c2_even x -> c_to_c2 (c2_to_c x) == x.
  Lemma c2_to_c_to_c2 : forall z : C F, c2_to_c (c_to_c2 z) == z.

A bijection between two four- and two-dimensional carriers is cheap; what makes this a ring isomorphism rather than a relabeling is that multiplication matches too, and multiplication is where the two constructions could have disagreed. C2 F was built entirely from the relations e1e1=e2e2=1e_1e_1=e_2e_2=1, e1e2=−e2e1e_1e_2=-e_2e_1 — nothing in its definition mentions complex numbers. Checking that its multiplication, restricted to even elements, reproduces the complex numbers’ product is a real theorem, verified directly against Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0)’s own multiplication table:

  Lemma c2_to_c_mul_even : forall x y : C2 F,
    c2_even x -> c2_even y -> c2_to_c (x * y) == c2_to_c x * c2_to_c y.

(0, 1, addition, and negation match unconditionally — c2_to_c_zero, c2_to_c_one, c2_to_c_add, c2_to_c_neg — because those operations only ever touch the scalar and bivector coordinates, whether or not the vector coordinates happen to be zero.) Contrast this with a smaller construction elsewhere in the same codebase, Cl(0,1)\mathrm{Cl}(0,1): there, a one-dimensional space is embedded directly into the complex numbers, which are the carrier by definition — the match is built in from the start, a coincidence of carriers. Here C2 F is an independent four-dimensional ring, built from the geometric relations alone, and its agreement with the complex numbers under multiplication had to be checked, and could in principle have failed.

Space’s even part is the quaternions

Space needs one more vector, e3e_3, still squaring to 1 and anticommuting with the other two. Multiplying triples of e1,e2,e3e_1, e_2, e_3 out closes up an eight-dimensional ring, Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) (Impl/Cliff3.v): the scalar, three vectors, three bivectors e12,e13,e23e_{12}, e_{13}, e_{23}, and one trivector e123e_{123}, the pseudoscalar. Its record has one field per blade, in that order:

Record C3 (F : Type) := mkC3 {
  c3_s   : F;
  c3_1   : F;
  c3_2   : F;
  c3_3   : F;
  c3_12  : F;
  c3_13  : F;
  c3_23  : F;
  c3_123 : F
}.

and the seven nonzero basis elements are just this record with a single 1 in the matching slot:

  Definition c3_e1 : C3 F := mkC3 0 1 0 0 0 0 0 0.
  Definition c3_e2 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 1 0 0 0 0 0.
  Definition c3_e3 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 0 1 0 0 0 0.
  Definition c3_e12 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 0 0 1 0 0 0.
  Definition c3_e13 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 0 0 0 1 0 0.
  Definition c3_e23 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 0 0 0 0 1 0.
  Definition c3_I3 : C3 F := mkC3 0 0 0 0 0 0 0 1.

The even part — scalar plus the three bivectors, four dimensions — is

  Definition c3_even (x : C3 F) : Prop :=
    c3_1 x == 0 /\ c3_2 x == 0 /\ c3_3 x == 0 /\ c3_123 x == 0.

Call the pseudoscalar I3:=e123I_3 := e_{123}. In Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0)’s two dimensions, the bivector e12e_{12} anticommutes with e1e_1 and e2e_2: moving it past a single vector costs one transposition, an odd number. In three dimensions the same kind of move costs 3−1=23-1=2 transpositions, an even number, so I3I_3 commutes with every vector instead — a fact about odd dimension, checked here for each of the three generators rather than assumed:

  Lemma c3_e1_I3_comm : c3_e1 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e1.
  Lemma c3_e2_I3_comm : c3_e2 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e2.
  Lemma c3_e3_I3_comm : c3_e3 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e3.

I3I_3 itself squares to −1-1, arrived at from three real, anticommuting generators rather than postulated. And the three bivectors, taken in the cyclic order e12,e23,e13e_{12}, e_{23}, e_{13} — not the differently-signed guess e12,e23,e3e1e_{12}, e_{23}, e_3e_1, which would need an extra sign correction the actual record does not carry — satisfy exactly the defining relations of the quaternions, each cyclic product landing on the next:

  Lemma c3_e12_e23 : c3_e12 * c3_e23 == c3_e13.
  Lemma c3_e23_e13 : c3_e23 * c3_e13 == c3_e12.
  Lemma c3_e13_e12 : c3_e13 * c3_e12 == c3_e23.

  Lemma c3_ijk : c3_e12 * c3_e23 * c3_e13 == - (1 : C3 F).

With i:=e12i := e_{12}, j:=e23j := e_{23}, k:=e13k := e_{13}, these read ij=kij=k, jk=ijk=i, ki=jki=j, and ijk=−1ijk=-1 (together with i2=j2=k2=−1i^2=j^2=k^2=-1, each proved the same way): the even part of Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) is the quaternions, the same way the even part of Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0) is the complex numbers.

Composing rotors is complex multiplication

A rotor is an even element, written directly by its two coordinates, with no angle mentioned:

  Definition rotor (c s : F) : C2 F := mkC2 c 0 0 s.

and reversal — the operation that will let a rotor act correctly on a vector — fixes the scalar coordinate and flips the sign of the bivector one, since reversing the order of the two vectors inside e12=e1e2e_{12}=e_1e_2 gives e2e1=−e12e_2e_1=-e_{12}:

  Definition c2_reverse (x : C2 F) : C2 F :=
    mkC2 (c2_s x) (c2_1 x) (c2_2 x) (- c2_12 x).

Composing two rotors — multiplying them as elements of Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0) — gives back a rotor, at the ordinary angle-addition pair:

rotor(c1,s1)rotor(c2,s2)=rotor(c1c2−s1s2,c1s2+s1c2).\mathrm{rotor}(c_1,s_1)\,\mathrm{rotor}(c_2,s_2) \;=\; \mathrm{rotor}(c_1c_2-s_1s_2,\ c_1s_2+s_1c_2).

This is the cosine/sine addition formula without ever introducing an angle: c,sc, s are just field elements, constrained to c2+s2=1c^2+s^2=1 only once a rotor is required to preserve length. The Coq statement is exactly this equation, checked against the multiplication table:

  Lemma rotor_mul : forall c1 s1 c2 s2 : F,
    rotor c1 s1 * rotor c2 s2 == rotor (c1*c2 + - (s1*s2)) (c1*s2 + s1*c2).
  Proof.
    intros c1 s1 c2 s2.
    unfold rotor.
    repeat split; simpl.
    - rewrite mul_0_l.
      autorewrite with grp.
      reflexivity.
    - repeat rewrite mul_0_l.
      repeat rewrite mul_0_r.
      autorewrite with grp.
      reflexivity.
    - repeat rewrite mul_0_l.
      repeat rewrite mul_0_r.
      autorewrite with grp.
      reflexivity.
    - rewrite mul_0_l.
      autorewrite with grp.
      reflexivity.
  Qed.

Unfolding rotor splits the goal into four field-level equations, one per coordinate of the result; a rotor’s own vector coordinates are always 0, so mul_0_l/mul_0_r erase every term multiplying one of them, and the grouping lemmas gathered as grp close what remains.

That this matches complex multiplication is not a second coincidence — it is one instance of the homomorphism theorem above, since a rotor is always even (rotor_even):

  Lemma rotor_mul_to_c : forall c1 s1 c2 s2 : F,
    c2_to_c (rotor c1 s1 * rotor c2 s2)
    == c2_to_c (rotor c1 s1) * c2_to_c (rotor c2 s2).
  Proof.
    intros c1 s1 c2 s2.
    exact (c2_to_c_mul_even (rotor c1 s1) (rotor c2 s2)
             (rotor_even c1 s1) (rotor_even c2 s2)).
  Qed.

Putting the two together — the image of a product of rotors equals the product of images, and the product of rotors is itself the angle-added rotor — gives the statement that composing rotors literally is multiplying complex numbers:

  Theorem rotor_composition_is_u1 : forall c1 s1 c2 s2 : F,
    c2_to_c (rotor c1 s1) * c2_to_c (rotor c2 s2)
    == c2_to_c (rotor (c1*c2 + - (s1*s2)) (c1*s2 + s1*c2)).
  Proof.
    intros c1 s1 c2 s2.
    rewrite <- (rotor_mul_to_c c1 s1 c2 s2).
    rewrite (rotor_mul c1 s1 c2 s2).
    reflexivity.
  Qed.

The first rewrite turns the left side, a product of two complex images, back into the image of a single product of rotors (rotor_mul_to_c, read right to left); the second replaces that product of rotors with the already-computed angle-added rotor (rotor_mul); the two sides are now the same expression, and reflexivity closes the goal. The unit-length elements of the complex numbers form a group under multiplication by definition — that group is what “U(1)” names — and this theorem says that group’s operation is exactly what composing two rotors already does.

A rotor and its negation: the double cover

Space’s version of a rotor sandwiches instead of merely multiplying. Reversal at this level fixes the scalar and the three vector coordinates and flips the sign of the three bivector coordinates and the trivector one — reversing the order of the vectors inside a blade costs k(k−1)/2k(k-1)/2 transpositions, odd exactly when the blade has grade 2 or 3:

  Definition c3_reverse (x : C3 F) : C3 F :=
    mkC3 (c3_s x) (c3_1 x) (c3_2 x) (c3_3 x)
      (- c3_12 x) (- c3_13 x) (- c3_23 x) (- c3_123 x).

For any element rr of Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) at all — not yet required to be even, or of unit length — define

  Definition c3_rotate (r x : C3 F) : C3 F := r * x * c3_reverse r.

so ρr(x)=rxr̃\rho_r(x) = r\,x\,\tilde r with r̃\tilde r the reversal. When rr is even, a separate theorem (c3_rotate_vector) shows ρr\rho_r sends every vector to another vector, with no unit hypothesis needed for that part. The question this section is built around is different: what happens to ρr\rho_r when rr is replaced by −r-r? In one sentence: nothing.

ρ−r(x)=ρr(x)for every r,x.\rho_{-r}(x) \;=\; \rho_r(x) \qquad\text{for every } r, x.

A rotor and its negation act identically, on every element, not only on vectors. This is the algebraic fact behind the physical claim that something built the way a rotor is built must be turned by 720°720°, not 360°360°, to return to where it started: a continuous family of rotors tracing out a full circle of actual rotations brings the rotations themselves back to “no rotation,” but need not bring the rotor itself back to where it began — it may instead land on the negation, and the theorem below says that negation produces exactly the same rotations, indistinguishable by anything built out of vectors alone.

  Theorem c3_rotate_neg : forall r x : C3 F, c3_rotate (- r) x == c3_rotate r x.
  Proof.
    intros r x. unfold c3_rotate.
    assert (Hrev : c3_reverse (- r) == - c3_reverse r).
    { apply c3_eq_intro. intros A.
      rewrite (c3_proj_neg (c3_reverse r) A), (c3_proj_reverse (- r) A),
        (c3_proj_reverse r A), (c3_proj_neg r A).
      exact (mul_neg_r (c3_rsign A) (c3_proj r A)). }
    rewrite Hrev.
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_l r x).
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_r (- (r * x)) (c3_reverse r)).
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_l (r * x) (c3_reverse r)).
    exact (c3_neg_involutive (r * x * c3_reverse r)).
  Qed.

Walk it against the sentence above. Reversal multiplies every coordinate by a fixed sign (c3_rsign, the same sign regardless of whether the input was negated), so it is linear, and Hrev proves −r̃=−r̃\widetilde{-r} = -\tilde r coordinate by coordinate. Rewriting by Hrev turns the goal into (−r)x(−r̃)==rxr̃(-r)\,x\,(-\tilde r) == r\,x\,\tilde r. Three applications of (−a)b=−(ab)(-a)b = -(ab) and a(−b)=−(ab)a(-b) = -(ab) (c3_neg_mul_l, c3_neg_mul_r) then pull both minus signs out to the front, one at a time, leaving −(−(rxr̃))-(-(r\,x\,\tilde r)); double negation (c3_neg_involutive) closes it. The sign introduced by negating rr on the left and the sign Hrev introduces on the right are two separate minus signs that cancel exactly against each other, with nothing left over.

Getting the full Standard Model group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1)SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1) out of one Clifford algebra is not settled the way the results above are settled: it is a small cluster of competing, mutually citing single-paper proposals — Trayling & Baylis (J. Phys. A 34 (2001) 3309) and Furey (Eur. Phys. J. C 78 (2018) 375) among them — none endorsed by mainstream particle physics, each reproducing known particle charges by construction rather than predicting anything new; the one genuinely uncontested piece of adjacent mathematics is that the automorphism group of the octonions is the exceptional Lie group G2G_2, and the subgroup of G2G_2 fixing one chosen unit imaginary octonion is exactly SU(3)SU(3) (Günaydin & Gürsey, J. Math. Phys. 14 (1973) 1651; canonical write-up: Baez, Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. 39 (2002) 145) — real, checkable, and a separate piece of mathematics from the two theorems proved above, not a third instance of the same argument.

Using it: a rotation matrix, with no trigonometry

Back in the plane, wrap a rotor around a vector the way c3_rotate above wrapped one around any element of Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0):

  Definition c2_sandwich (R x : C2 F) : C2 F := R * x * c2_reverse R.

Feed it a pure vector — an element with only its e1,e2e_1, e_2 coordinates set, c2_vec (row2 v1 v2) where row2 a b is just the pair (a,b)(a,b) read as a function on two indices. Computed directly from the multiplication table, with no hypothesis on c,sc,s anywhere:

  Lemma c2_sandwich_rotor_vec : forall c s v1 v2 : F,
    c2_sandwich (rotor c s) (c2_vec (row2 v1 v2))
    == c2_vec (row2
         ((c*c + - (s*s)) * v1 + (c*s + c*s) * v2)
         (- ((c*s + c*s) * v1) + (c*c + - (s*s)) * v2)).

Wrapping rotor(c,s)\mathrm{rotor}(c,s) around (v1,v2)(v_1,v_2) returns (w1,w2)(w_1,w_2) with

w1=(c2−s2)v1+2csv2,w2=−2csv1+(c2−s2)v2,w_1 = (c^2-s^2)v_1 + 2cs\,v_2, \qquad w_2 = -2cs\,v_1 + (c^2-s^2)v_2,

the matrix

(c2−s22cs−2csc2−s2).\begin{pmatrix} c^2-s^2 & 2cs \\ -2cs & c^2-s^2\end{pmatrix}.

Once c2+s2=1c^2+s^2=1, these are exactly the double-angle formulas cos⁡2θ=c2−s2\cos 2\theta = c^2-s^2 and sin⁡2θ=2cs\sin 2\theta = 2cs for c=cos⁡θ,s=sin⁡θc=\cos\theta, s=\sin\theta — but nothing above ever invoked cos\cos, sin\sin, or a trigonometric identity; the whole computation is multiplying out the sandwich against the multiplication table and regrouping terms. (The matrix is rotation by −2θ-2\theta in the usual counterclockwise-positive convention rather than +2θ+2\theta — a real consequence of writing the bivector as e1e2e_1e_2 rather than e2e1e_2e_1, not an error.) A further theorem, proved the same way from c2+s2=1c^2+s^2=1 alone (c2_sandwich_rotor_preserves_dot), checks that the squared length v12+v22v_1^2+v_2^2 is exactly preserved: the sandwich is a genuine rotation, not merely some linear map that happens to look like one.
