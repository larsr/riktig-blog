The action: mass times velocity times distance

Mechanics has a quantity called the action. Its usual introduction is the variational principle: among all conceivable motions between two configurations, the actual motion is the one that makes the action stationary, and working out what stationarity demands yields the equations of motion. That principle is not this post’s subject — one sentence of context is all it gets. This post is about something that comes before any variational calculation: the action’s dimensions, and a theorem that those dimensions all but dictate.

Start with nothing but a moving body. It has a mass mm, it has a speed vv, and over some stretch of its motion it covers a distance. Any quantity built multiplicatively from the motion itself should scale linearly in each of these: run the same motion with twice the mass and the quantity doubles; traverse two consecutive stretches and the quantities for the stretches add. So whatever the action is, its units must be

mass×velocity×length.\text{mass} \times \text{velocity} \times \text{length}.

Now look for integrals with exactly those dimensions. There are two natural candidates, and they sum over different things. The first sums over where the body went: momentum p=mvp = m\,v integrated along the path through space,

∫pdq,units: (mass×velocity)×length,\int p \, dq, \qquad \text{units: } (\text{mass}\times\text{velocity}) \times \text{length},

classically called the abbreviated action. The second sums over when: twice the kinetic energy — the quantity mv2m\,v^2 — integrated over time,

∫mv2dt,units: mass×velocity×(velocity×time),\int m\,v^2 \, dt, \qquad \text{units: } \text{mass}\times\text{velocity} \times (\text{velocity}\times\text{time}),

and velocity times time is again a length. Same dimensions, two different integration variables. The theorem of this post is that they are the same number: if the body follows a path qq with velocity v=q̇v = \dot q from time aa to time bb, then

∫q(a)q(b)pdq=∫abmv(t)2dt.\int_{q(a)}^{q(b)} p \, dq \;=\; \int_a^b m\,v(t)^2 \, dt.

The bridge between “an integral over position” and “an integral over time” is the change-of-variables rule — substitution — and substitution is itself nothing more than the chain rule read through the fundamental theorem of calculus, twice. That is the whole proof, and the rest of this post carries it out formally.

The two scaling facts from the dimensional story become theorems too, and they are even cheaper: linearity in the mass,

∫ab(cm)v(t)2dt=c∫abmv(t)2dt,\int_a^b (c\,m)\,v(t)^2 \, dt \;=\; c \int_a^b m\,v(t)^2 \, dt,

and additivity over consecutive stretches of the motion,

∫abmv(t)2dt+∫bcmv(t)2dt=∫acmv(t)2dt.\int_a^b m\,v(t)^2\,dt \;+\; \int_b^c m\,v(t)^2\,dt \;=\; \int_a^c m\,v(t)^2\,dt.

Neither needs the fundamental theorem of calculus at all — they are integral laws wearing the action’s clothes.

The integral is a specification, and FTC is not one of its laws

Two recaps, in a few sentences each, of material this post builds on.

First, the derivative. In this series a derivative is not a number but a bounded linear map: is_deriv f a L says that the linear map L approximates the increments of f near the point a to first order, with an error that vanishes faster than the increment. For a scalar function on a field FF, every linear map F→FF \to F is multiplication by a fixed number, and lm_scale_map c is exactly that map, h↦chh \mapsto c\,h — so “the derivative of GG at tt is the number g(t)g(t)” is written is_deriv G t (lm_scale_map (g t)). The chain rule, proved once for linear-map derivatives, says the derivative of a composite is the composite of the derivatives.

Second, the integral. The integral here is a specification: a class that lists the laws any integral must satisfy — it is linear in the integrand, additive over endpoints (int f a b + int f b c == int f a c), it gives constants their obvious value, and it obeys a norm bound — with no construction behind it. No Riemann sums, no limits of partitions; just the laws. Everything proved against the class holds for every future model of it. (Throughout, == is the equivalence relation each carrier brings with it, and subtraction is written + -, addition of a negation.)

Conspicuously absent from that list of laws: the fundamental theorem of calculus. The interface file says exactly why, in a comment worth quoting in full:

(* FTC — the intended top law, stated here as SPEC only.

     If G' = g on [a, b]   (in the sense of Theory/Derivative.v's
                            is_deriv, for G g : F -> V)
     then   int g a b == G b + - G a.

   It is NOT a field of the class and NOT a theorem here, because it
   cannot be either yet:
   - as a field it would need is_deriv, and Theory/Derivative.v is being
     built in parallel — importing it here would couple the spec to the
     derivative file's exact formulation before it has settled;
   - as a theorem it does not follow from the laws above: the laws pin
     down the integral on step-function-like data (const, linearity,
     additivity, the bound), but connecting it to derivatives needs
     either a mean-value-style argument (which consumes completeness,
     Interface/Reals.v) or the concrete Riemann-sum construction.
   It will land as a theorem in the Theory file that has both the
   derivative and a model — or as a law of a stronger class
   (IntegralFTC extending this one) if programs need it before the
   construction exists. *)

The mathematical point is the second bullet: linearity, additivity, the constant rule and the bound pin the integral down on step-function-like data, but nothing in them connects integrals to derivatives. Making that connection requires either a mean-value argument — which consumes completeness, an assumption the specification deliberately does not make — or an actual construction like Riemann sums. Since this post works against the bare specification, the fundamental theorem of calculus enters as an explicitly disclosed hypothesis of the development, visible in every statement that uses it. On the blackboard:

if G′(t)=g(t) for every t,then∫abg(t)dt=G(b)−G(a).\text{if } G'(t) = g(t) \text{ for every } t, \quad \text{then} \quad \int_a^b g(t)\,dt \;=\; G(b) - G(a).

And as the section hypothesis, verbatim:

  Context (ftc : forall (G g : F -> F) (a b : F),
    (forall t : F, is_deriv G t (lm_scale_map (g t))) ->
    int g a b == G b + - G a).

A model of the integral built from Riemann sums over a complete field is expected to discharge this hypothesis; the bare class does not supply it, and nothing below pretends otherwise. The section sits over an ordered field F with an integral Int : Integral F F — scalar integrands, scalar variable, the field acting on itself as a one-dimensional vector space, so the scaling c *: x appearing below is ordinary multiplication.

The cast: a path, its speed, and momentum as a function of position

The theorem needs a small cast of characters, declared as section hypotheses:

    Context (m : F) (q v phi Phi : F -> F).
    Context (Phi_proper : Proper (equ ==> equ) Phi).
    Context (Phi_antideriv : forall x : F, is_deriv Phi x (lm_scale_map (phi x))).
    Context (q_deriv : forall t : F, is_deriv q t (lm_scale_map (v t))).
    Context (speed_of_position : forall t : F, v t == phi (q t)).

In order: m is the mass. q is the path — position as a function of time — and q_deriv says its derivative at every time t is the number v t, so v is the velocity. phi is speed as a function of position: speed_of_position says that at every time, the speed equals phi evaluated at the current position. Momentum as a function of position is then mφ(x)m\,\varphi(x), which is the integrand of the position integral. Phi is an antiderivative of phi (Phi_antideriv), needed because substitution will invoke the FTC hypothesis, and FTC consumes an antiderivative. Phi_proper says Phi maps equivalent inputs to equivalent outputs — the chain rule (deriv_comp) demands this of the outer function in a composite, it cannot be derived from is_deriv alone, and any function actually built from the field operations satisfies it automatically.

The hypothesis v t == phi (q t) deserves a plain statement of what it assumes geometrically: that along this stretch of trajectory, position determines speed. That restricts attention to trajectory segments on which the body never revisits a position — if the body passed the same point twice at different speeds, no function of position could return both. Such segments are not exotic. An earlier post proved energy conservation for one-dimensional motion: with a potential VV, the total energy 12mv2+V(q)\tfrac{1}{2}m\,v^2 + V(q) is constant along any motion obeying Hamilton’s equations. Solving that constant for the speed gives v=±2(E−V(q))/mv = \pm\sqrt{2(E - V(q))/m} — position determines speed up to sign, so on any segment where the body does not turn around, it determines speed outright, and phi exists. No claim is made beyond such segments.

Change of variables, from the chain rule and FTC

Theorem. If WW is an antiderivative of ff everywhere and uu has derivative u′u' everywhere, then integrating f(u(t))u′(t)f(u(t))\,u'(t) over tt from aa to bb is the same as integrating ff from u(a)u(a) to u(b)u(b).

On the blackboard, both sides are computed to the same expression by two applications of FTC:

∫abf(u(t))u′(t)dt=(1)W(u(b))−W(u(a))=(2)∫u(a)u(b)f(x)dx.\int_a^b f(u(t))\,u'(t)\,dt \;\overset{(1)}{=}\; W(u(b)) - W(u(a)) \;\overset{(2)}{=}\; \int_{u(a)}^{u(b)} f(x)\,dx.

Equality (2) is FTC applied directly to WW and ff between the endpoints u(a)u(a) and u(b)u(b). Equality (1) is FTC applied to the composite W∘uW \circ u between aa and bb — legitimate because the chain rule computes the composite’s derivative:

(W∘u)′(t)=W′(u(t))⋅u′(t)=f(u(t))⋅u′(t),(W \circ u)'(t) \;=\; W'(u(t)) \cdot u'(t) \;=\; f(u(t)) \cdot u'(t),

which is exactly the integrand on the left. The Coq proof, verbatim:

  Theorem int_substitution : forall (f W u du : F -> F) (a b : F),
    Proper (equ ==> equ) W ->
    (forall x : F, is_deriv W x (lm_scale_map (f x))) ->
    (forall t : F, is_deriv u t (lm_scale_map (du t))) ->
    int (fun t : F => f (u t) * du t) a b == int f (u a) (u b).
  Proof.
    intros f W u du a b PW HW Hu.
    calc (int (fun t : F => f (u t) * du t) a b)
      == (W (u b) + - W (u a))
      == (int f (u a) (u b)).
    - apply (ftc (fun t : F => W (u t)) (fun t : F => f (u t) * du t) a b).
      intros t.
      apply (is_deriv_L_equ (fun s : F => W (u s)) t
        (lm_comp (lm_scale_map (f (u t))) (lm_scale_map (du t)))).
      + exact (lm_comp_scale_scale (du t) (f (u t))).
      + exact (deriv_comp u W t (lm_scale_map (du t))
          (lm_scale_map (f (u t))) PW (Hu t) (HW (u t))).
    - symmetry. exact (ftc W f (u a) (u b) HW).
  Qed.

The walk. The calc tactic states the blackboard chain directly — left side, middle expression W (u b) + - W (u a), right side — and leaves one goal per step. The first goal is equality (1): apply ftc with G:=W∘uG := W \circ u, which reduces it to showing that W∘uW \circ u has derivative f(u(t))⋅u′(t)f(u(t)) \cdot u'(t) at every tt. That is the chain rule: deriv_comp produces the derivative of the composite as the composition of the two linear maps, lm_comp of scaling by f (u t) after scaling by du t — note PW, the Proper hypothesis on W, being consumed right there. The lemma lm_comp_scale_scale says composing two scalings is scaling by the product, and is_deriv_L_equ transports the derivative fact across that pointwise equality of linear maps, landing on lm_scale_map (f (u t) * du t) — the form ftc wants. The second goal is equality (2): ftc applied to W and f directly, with a symmetry because the chain reads left to right.

The abbreviated action: from position to time

Theorem. The integral of momentum over position from q(a)q(a) to q(b)q(b) equals the integral of mv2m\,v^2 over time from aa to bb.

On the blackboard, writing p(x)=mφ(x)p(x) = m\,\varphi(x) for momentum as a function of position:

∫q(a)q(b)mφ(x)dx=(1)m∫q(a)q(b)φ(x)dx=(2)m∫abφ(q(t))v(t)dt=(3)∫abmφ(q(t))v(t)dt=(4)∫abmv(t)2dt.\int_{q(a)}^{q(b)} m\,\varphi(x)\,dx \;\overset{(1)}{=}\; m \int_{q(a)}^{q(b)} \varphi(x)\,dx \;\overset{(2)}{=}\; m \int_a^b \varphi(q(t))\,v(t)\,dt \;\overset{(3)}{=}\; \int_a^b m\,\varphi(q(t))\,v(t)\,dt \;\overset{(4)}{=}\; \int_a^b m\,v(t)^2\,dt.

Step (1) pulls the constant mass out of the integral — linearity. Step (2) is the substitution theorem just proved, run from right to left with f:=φf := \varphi, u:=qu := q, u′:=vu' := v. Step (3) pushes the mass back inside — linearity again. Step (4) rewrites φ(q(t))\varphi(q(t)) to v(t)v(t) under the integral sign, using the hypothesis that speed is a function of position along this segment. The Coq proof, verbatim:

    Theorem abbreviated_action : forall a b : F,
      int (fun x : F => m * phi x) (q a) (q b)
        == int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b.
    Proof.
      intros a b.
      calc (int (fun x : F => m * phi x) (q a) (q b))
        == (m *: int phi (q a) (q b))
        == (m *: int (fun t : F => phi (q t) * v t) a b)
        == (int (fun t : F => m * (phi (q t) * v t)) a b)
        == (int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b).
      - exact (int_linear_scale m phi (q a) (q b)).
      - apply field_sc_proper.
        + reflexivity.
        + symmetry.
          exact (int_substitution phi Phi q v a b
            Phi_proper Phi_antideriv q_deriv).
      - symmetry.
        exact (int_linear_scale m (fun t : F => phi (q t) * v t) a b).
      - assert (Hpt : pointwise_relation F equ
            (fun t : F => m * (phi (q t) * v t))
            (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t))).
        { intros t.
          apply ring_mul_proper; [ reflexivity | ].
          apply ring_mul_proper; [ symmetry; exact (speed_of_position t) | ].
          reflexivity. }
        exact (int_proper (fun t : F => m * (phi (q t) * v t))
          (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) Hpt
          a a (reflexivity a) b b (reflexivity b)).
    Qed.

The walk, one step of the calc chain at a time. Step (1) is the integral law int_linear_scale. Step (2) applies int_substitution with f := phi, W := Phi, u := q, du := v, handing it the three hypotheses it demands (Phi_proper, Phi_antideriv, q_deriv); field_sc_proper lets the rewrite happen under the m *:, and symmetry flips the substitution equation because the chain moves from the position integral toward the time integral. Step (3) is int_linear_scale again, backwards. Step (4) builds the pointwise fact that the two integrands agree at every t — two congruence steps peel off m * and * v t, and speed_of_position rewrites phi (q t) to v t — and then feeds it to int_proper, the class’s own law that the integral respects pointwise-equivalent integrands.

The two scaling laws

The dimensional story said the action scales linearly in the mass and adds over consecutive stretches. Both are single integral laws, no FTC involved.

Linearity in the mass: ∫ab(cm)v(t)2dt=c∫abmv(t)2dt\int_a^b (c\,m)\,v(t)^2\,dt = c \int_a^b m\,v(t)^2\,dt.

    Theorem action_mass_linear : forall (c a b : F),
      int (fun t : F => (c * m) * (v t * v t)) a b
        == c *: int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b.
    Proof.
      intros c a b.
      calc (int (fun t : F => (c * m) * (v t * v t)) a b)
        == (int (fun t : F => c *: (m * (v t * v t))) a b)
        == (c *: int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b).
      - assert (Hpt : pointwise_relation F equ
            (fun t : F => (c * m) * (v t * v t))
            (fun t : F => c *: (m * (v t * v t)))).
        { intros t. exact (ring_mul_assoc c m (v t * v t)). }
        exact (int_proper (fun t : F => (c * m) * (v t * v t))
          (fun t : F => c *: (m * (v t * v t))) Hpt
          a a (reflexivity a) b b (reflexivity b)).
      - exact (int_linear_scale c (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b).
    Qed.

Associativity regroups (cm)⋅v2(c\,m) \cdot v^2 as c⋅(mv2)c \cdot (m\,v^2) pointwise, and int_linear_scale pulls the c out.

Additivity over consecutive time intervals: ∫abmv(t)2dt+∫bcmv(t)2dt=∫acmv(t)2dt\int_a^b m\,v(t)^2\,dt + \int_b^c m\,v(t)^2\,dt = \int_a^c m\,v(t)^2\,dt.

    Theorem action_path_additive : forall a b c : F,
      int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b
        + int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) b c
        == int (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a c.
    Proof.
      intros a b c.
      exact (int_additivity (fun t : F => m * (v t * v t)) a b c).
    Qed.

This one is the class law int_additivity applied to the action’s integrand, nothing more — running from aa to bb and then from bb to cc costs the action of running from aa to cc.

What a reader gets out of this

Everything above is proved against the integral specification, so when a model of the class is eventually constructed — Riemann sums over a complete ordered field is the intended one — every theorem here transfers to it by instantiation, with no new proof work beyond discharging the ftc hypothesis, which is precisely the theorem such a construction exists to provide. A caller who has a concrete mass, path, velocity, and a speed-of-position function on some trajectory segment supplies the five section hypotheses and immediately owns the position-to-time identity for every pair of endpoints on that segment.

And the hypotheses are the interface — nothing is smuggled in as a global axiom. The file ends by recording exactly that check:

(* Executed once during development; each reports "Closed under the
   global context" — ftc and the trajectory facts are explicit
   hypotheses visible in the statements, not axioms. *)
(* Print Assumptions int_substitution. *)
(* Print Assumptions abbreviated_action. *)
(* Print Assumptions action_mass_linear. *)
(* Print Assumptions action_path_additive. *)

Print Assumptions lists every axiom a proof term depends on; “closed under the global context” is Coq’s phrase for none. Every physical assumption (the trajectory facts) and every analytic one (FTC, the antiderivative) sits in plain sight in a Context line or a theorem statement, where a reader — or a future model — can see exactly what remains to be paid, and where.
