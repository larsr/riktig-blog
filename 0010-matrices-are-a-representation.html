riktig #10 — Matrix multiplication is not a definition

Tenth post documenting the riktig project; second in the linear-algebra course thread of post #9, with proofs shown blackboard-style and as they live in the repository.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #9 built the space of linear maps without ever mentioning coordinates. Today coordinates arrive — in their proper place. A matrix is not what a linear map is; it is how a linear map is written down once you choose bases. And the row-times-column rule you memorized is a theorem: matrix multiplication is function composition, transported along that choice.

The carrier costs nothing

A matrix is a grid of scalars (Impl/Matrix.v):

Definition Matrix (m n : nat) (F : Type) := Fin.t m -> Fin.t n -> F.

Functions into functions into a field — so by the pointwise construction (post #6), applied twice, matrices form a vector space with zero new proofs. Multiplication is the familiar sum, (AB)ik=∑jAijBjk\;(AB)_{ik} = \sum_j A_{ij} B_{jk}, and its laws are finite-sum algebra: associativity is the interchange of a double sum (∑i∑j=∑j∑i\sum_i \sum_j = \sum_j \sum_i — Fubini for finite sums, proved by induction in Theory/BigOp.v), and the identity laws are the delta collapse from post #8’s basis work. Square matrices form a ring — noncommutative, like End V from post #9. That’s no coincidence, as we’re about to see.

Writing a map down

Fix a basis b1U,…,bnUb^U_1,\dots,b^U_n of UU and b1V,…,bmVb^V_1,\dots,b^V_m of VV (recall post #8: a basis is a linear coordinate map with two round-trip laws). Every linear map f:U→Vf : U \to V gets a matrix: the jj-th column holds the coordinates of ff’s value on the jj-th basis vector.

Definition mat_of (f : LinearMap F U V) : Matrix m n F :=
  fun i j => basis_coords BV (f (basis_vec BU j)) i.

The theorem package (Theory/MatRep.v) says this is a faithful translation: mat_of has an inverse hom_of, both are linear, and the pair is a representation isomorphism in the sense of post #6:

$$\mathrm{Hom}(U,V) \;\cong\; F^{m\times n} \qquad\text{(\texttt{vsiso\_matrix})}$$

The theorem you memorized as a definition

Blackboard. Let f:U→Vf : U \to V, g:V→Wg : V \to W. What is the matrix of g∘fg \circ f? Entry (i,j)(i,j): expand f(bjU)f(b^U_j) in the VV-basis, f(bjU)=∑kckbkVf(b^U_j) = \sum_k c_k\, b^V_k where ck=[[f]]kjc_k = [\![f]\!]_{kj}, and push gg through the sum by linearity:

[[g∘f]]ij=[g(∑k[[f]]kjbkV)]i=[∑k[[f]]kjg(bkV)]i=∑k[[g]]ik[[f]]kj.[\![g \circ f]\!]_{ij} = \Big[g\Big(\textstyle\sum_k [\![f]\!]_{kj}\, b^V_k\Big)\Big]_i = \Big[\textstyle\sum_k [\![f]\!]_{kj}\, g(b^V_k)\Big]_i = \textstyle\sum_k [\![g]\!]_{ik} [\![f]\!]_{kj}.

The last expression is exactly the (i,j)(i,j) entry of [[g]][[f]][\![g]\!] [\![f]\!]. So the row-times-column rule is forced: it is the unique rule that makes “write down, then multiply” agree with “compose, then write down”. ∎

Repository. The three blackboard steps are one reusable computation rule — coordinates of an image, as a matrix-weighted sum of coordinates:

Theorem mat_of_coords : forall (f : LinearMap F U V) (u : U)
    (i : Fin.t m),
  basis_coords BV (f u) i == hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u i.

and with it the headline is a one-line proof:

Theorem mat_of_comp : forall (g : LinearMap F V W) (f : LinearMap F U V),
  mat_of BU BW (lm_comp g f)
    == mat_mul (mat_of BV BW g) (mat_of BU BV f).
Proof.
  intros g f i j.
  exact (mat_of_coords BV BW g (f (basis_vec BU j)) i).
Qed.

The lemma types you need to believe it: lm_lincomb (linear maps commute with linear combinations — the “push gg through the sum” step, itself proved by induction from lm_zero, lm_add, and lm_scale), and coords_vec : basis_coords B (basis_vec B j) == e j (a basis vector’s coordinates are a delta — post #8’s round trip laws, specialized). Everything you did in first-year exercises — expand, push through, collect — is now a named rewrite.

Why this matters beyond the aesthetics

Because mat_of is an isomorphism, every question about matrices can be answered on whichever side is easier, and the answer transports. Associativity of mat_mul? Prove it directly by Fubini on double sums — that proof is in the repo (mat_mul_assoc, Impl/Matrix.v) — or observe that matrix multiplication is composition, which is associative, and transport along mat_of_comp. Change of basis? Conjugation along two vsiso_matrixes. And when the derivative arrives (the analysis track is already at limits), the Jacobian will need no new theory: it is mat_of applied to the total derivative. The chain rule will BE mat_of_comp.
