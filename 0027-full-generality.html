riktig #27 — full generality

Twenty-seventh post documenting the riktig project. Five attempts into the same problem, the fact everyone actually wanted finally lands: swap any two rows, anywhere, and the determinant negates.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #26 proved swap-antisymmetry for adjacent rows only — a real generalization of an older special case, but still restricted. Today’s post removes the restriction entirely: any two distinct rows, anywhere in the matrix.

The statement, finally unrestricted

Theorem detM_swap_general : forall (N : nat) (i j : Fin.t N),
  i <> j -> forall A : Matrix N N F,
  detM (row_swap A i j) == - detM A.

Any two distinct rows i≠ji \ne j. Not the top two, not an adjacent pair — any pair, in a matrix of any size, over any field. The smoke test makes the point concretely: it swaps the top and bottom rows of a 3×33\times 3 matrix, a pair that post #26’s adjacent-only version simply couldn’t express.

What closed the gap

Post #26 left one precise thing missing: showing a permutation’s sign flips under postcomposition by an arbitrary transposition, not just an adjacent one. Two pieces closed it, both classical, neither novel in mathematics — the work was carrying them through this particular representation, in Coq, correctly.

First, a composition lemma: postcomposing a permutation twice in sequence gives the same sign as postcomposing once by the flattened composite. This had been flagged as possibly fiddly — the previous attempt’s header worried about reconciling two different fallback branches in the underlying relocation machinery. It turned out simpler than feared: the branch that needed reconciling is provably never taken in the first place, so the lemma closes cleanly with one relocation identity, no case-split needed.

Second, the textbook trick for building an arbitrary transposition out of small pieces:

(ij)=(ik)(kj)(ik)(i\ j) = (i\ k)(k\ j)(i\ k)

for any kk strictly between ii and jj. Recurse on the gap between ii and jj — each application of this identity shrinks the gap by one — and an arbitrary transposition unfolds into a chain of adjacent ones, each of whose sign-flip was already proved in post #26. Chain the composition lemma through that unfolding, by ordinary structural induction on the gap (no well-founded recursion, no termination argument beyond “the gap is a natural number that shrinks”) and the general sign-flip theorem falls out.

Reading the shape of five attempts

It’s worth pausing on the arc rather than just the destination, because this is not the usual story of “kept trying the same thing until it worked.” Each attempt changed the strategy in response to precisely what the previous one learned:

1–2. Direct row-relocation through the cofactor recursion. Both stalled on the same wall: tracking two rows through an arbitrary deletion produces a combinatorial explosion the recursion’s own shape doesn’t resolve cleanly.

  1. Reformulate as a sum over permutations instead (post #23) — sidesteps the relocation problem by construction. Reaches the row-swap half of antisymmetry, unconditionally, for any pair.

  2. The sign half, for adjacent transpositions (post #26) — a genuine generalization of the one special case attempt #1 ever reached, using a new construction (postcomposition-as-a-permutation-value) that had to exist before the question could even be posed.

  3. Today: the general case, by composing adjacent transpositions.

Nothing here was thrown away. Every attempt’s output became the next attempt’s starting material — the direct route’s elementary matrices are still waiting, unused until now, for the day this thread reaches detM_mul; the permutation-sum reformulation’s machinery carried every subsequent step; the adjacent case’s theorem is the base case of today’s induction.

What’s still open, and why it’s a different kind of gap now

detM_mul — the ultimate target, general determinant multiplicativity — is still not reached, and it’s worth being precise about why, because the shape of the remaining gap has changed. Classical proofs of “swap negates, hence a repeated row makes the determinant zero” lean on characteristic: negate a value by swapping it with an equal copy of itself, get x=−xx = -x, conclude 2x=02x = 0, and — if the field has characteristic ≠2\ne 2 — conclude x=0x = 0. This codebase’s Field carries no characteristic assumption at all (a deliberate choice, documented since post #14), so that shortcut is simply unavailable. The honest fix needs a different argument entirely: pair up the permutations in the Leibniz sum by a fixed transposition and cancel the pairs directly, term by term, rather than relying on 2x=02x=0 forcing x=0x=0. That’s a real, separate piece of combinatorics — related in spirit to the reindexing arguments already built, but a pairing argument rather than a relabeling one — and it’s the next concrete target.

Even granting that, the classical route to detM_mul still needs the full Gaussian-elimination composition argument (any matrix reduces to a product of elementary matrices and an upper-triangular one; the determinant of an upper-triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal) — separately real work, not yet attempted. Two more pieces, both precisely named, neither a mystery.

Next: the alternating fact via pairing, attempt #6 — or, if that keeps for a round, back to Stage B’s softmax and ReLU.
