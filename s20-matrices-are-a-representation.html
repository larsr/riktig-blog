Matrices are a representation, not a definition

A linear map is a function between two vector spaces that preserves their structure: it carries sums to sums and scalings to scalings. A matrix is a rectangle of numbers, multiplied by the row-times-column rule everyone memorizes. Most first courses start from the rectangle and build linear algebra on top of it — but the rectangle depends on a choice nothing in the map itself dictates: a coordinate system. The question this post answers precisely is which of the two objects is primary, and why the memorized multiplication rule could not have been anything else. The answer: the map comes first, and a matrix is what a map looks like after you have chosen coordinates. Once a basis is fixed for the source and for the target, every linear map between them corresponds to exactly one rectangle of scalars, composition of maps corresponds to multiplication of those rectangles, and the correspondence is a bijection that respects addition and scaling. That statement is the representation theorem. Matrices fall out of it as a consequence; they are not where linear algebra begins.

The formalization makes the priority explicit. A linear map is a record carrying the underlying function together with proofs of the two preservation laws, f (u + v) = f u + f v and f (c *: u) = c *: f u — from Interface/LinearMap.v:

Record LinearMap (F U V : Type) {FF : Field F}
    {VU : VectorSpace F U} {VV : VectorSpace F V} := {
  lm_fun :> U -> V;
  #[global] lm_proper :: Proper (equ ==> equ) lm_fun;
  lm_add : forall u v : U, lm_fun (u + v) == lm_fun u + lm_fun v;
  lm_scale : forall (a : F) (u : U), lm_fun (a *: u) == a *: lm_fun u
}.

Notice what is not here: no grid of numbers, no rows and columns.

Coordinates: a vector written in a basis

A basis of an n-dimensional space is a choice of n vectors such that every vector is a unique combination c_1 *: b_1 + ... + c_n *: b_n. The scalars c_i are the vector’s coordinates; the library’s basis_coords B reads them off (as a linear map into the space of coordinate functions Fin.t n -> F), and basis_vec B recovers the basis vectors. The one fact we need is that these round-trip: rebuilding a vector from its coordinates gives the vector back. With that, a coordinate-free vector and its column of scalars carry the same information — the basis is the dictionary between them.

Reading a map off in bases, and rebuilding it

Fix a source basis BU of an n-dimensional space U and a target basis BV of an m-dimensional space V. A matrix here is just a rectangle of scalars, Matrix m n F := Fin.t m -> Fin.t n -> F. To read a linear map f as a matrix, record in column j the coordinates of what f does to the j-th source basis vector. From Theory/MatRep.v:

Definition mat_of (f : LinearMap F U V) : Matrix m n F :=
  fun i j => basis_coords BV (f (basis_vec BU j)) i.

Entry (i, j) is the i-th coordinate of f (basis_vec BU j): what the j-th source basis vector becomes, expressed in the target basis. The columns of the matrix are the images of the basis vectors.

The reverse — rebuilding a map from a rectangle of scalars — reads each output coordinate as a row of the matrix against the input’s coordinate column, then reassembles the target vector from those coordinates:

Definition hom_of_coeff (A : Matrix m n F) (u : U) : Fin.t m -> F :=
  fun i => bigopF (fun j => A i j * basis_coords BU u j).

Definition hom_of_fun (A : Matrix m n F) (u : U) : V :=
  lincomb (basis_vec BV) (hom_of_coeff A u).

hom_of_coeff A u i is the sum Σ_j A i j * (coordinate j of u) — row i of A paired with u’s coordinate column, which is exactly one entry of “matrix times vector.” hom_of_fun then combines the resulting coordinates against the target basis. Packaged with proofs that it is linear, this becomes a genuine LinearMap:

Definition hom_of (A : Matrix m n F) : LinearMap F U V :=
  @Build_LinearMap F U V FF VU VV (hom_of_fun A)
    (hom_of_fun_proper A) (hom_of_fun_add A) (hom_of_fun_scale A).

The computation rule: applying the matrix computes the map

Everything rests on one identity: the coordinates of f u are the matrix of f applied to the coordinates of u. Writing [v]i[v]_i for the ii-th coordinate of a vector vv and [f]ij[f]_{ij} for entry (i,j)(i,j) of the matrix read off from ff,

[fu]i=[f(∑j[u]jbj)]i{expand u in the source basis}=[∑j[u]jf(bj)]i{f is linear}=∑j[f]ij[u]j{expand each f(bj),collect}[f\,u]_i \;=\; \Bigl[\, f\bigl(\textstyle\sum_j [u]_j\, b_j\bigr) \Bigr]_i \quad\{\text{expand } u \text{ in the source basis}\} \;=\; \Bigl[\, \textstyle\sum_j [u]_j\, f(b_j) \Bigr]_i \quad\{f \text{ is linear}\} \;=\; \textstyle\sum_j [f]_{ij}\,[u]_j \quad\{\text{expand each } f(b_j),\ \text{collect}\}

— and the right-hand side is precisely one entry of “matrix times column.” The library isolates this as mat_of_coords:

Theorem mat_of_coords : forall (f : LinearMap F U V) (u : U)
    (i : Fin.t m),
  basis_coords BV (f u) i == hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u i.
Proof.
  intros f u i.
  calc (basis_coords BV (f u) i)
    == (basis_coords BV
          (lincomb (basis_vec BV) (hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u)) i)
    == (hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u i).
  - exact (lm_proper (basis_coords BV) (f u)
             (lincomb (basis_vec BV) (hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u))
             (hom_expand f u) i).
  - exact (basis_coords_lincomb BV (hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u) i).
Qed.

Walking it:

  • The calc chain routes through an intermediate expression: the target vector rebuilt from the coordinate column hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u, read at coordinate i.
  • The first link is hom_expand f u, the expand-and-collect computation above, which says f u equals that rebuilt vector; lm_proper transports the equality under basis_coords BV and reads it at index i.
  • The second link, basis_coords_lincomb, is the round-trip fact: reading coordinate i back off a reconstruction returns coordinate i of the column you built it from.

Reading off and rebuilding are inverse

This is one half of the representation isomorphism Hom(U,V) ≅ Matrix: rebuilding a map from its matrix returns the original map. On the blackboard it is hom_of (mat_of f) = f, and the argument is that both sides agree at every input u, because their coordinates agree by the computation rule:

fu=∑i(coord i of fu)bi=∑i(𝗆𝖺𝗍_𝗈𝖿f applied to u)ibi=(𝗁𝗈𝗆_𝗈𝖿(𝗆𝖺𝗍_𝗈𝖿f))u.f\,u \;=\; \sum_i \big(\text{coord } i \text{ of } f\,u\big)\, b_i \;=\; \sum_i \big(\mathsf{mat\_of}\,f \text{ applied to } u\big)_i\, b_i \;=\; (\mathsf{hom\_of}\,(\mathsf{mat\_of}\,f))\,u.

Verbatim from Theory/MatRep.v:

Theorem hom_of_mat_of : forall f : LinearMap F U V,
  hom_of (mat_of f) == f.
Proof.
  intros f u.
  calc (hom_of (mat_of f) u)
    == (lincomb (basis_vec BV) (basis_coords BV (f u)))
    == (f u).
  - apply (lincomb_proper m (basis_vec BV)
             (hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u) (basis_coords BV (f u))).
    intros i.
    symmetry.
    exact (mat_of_coords f u i).
  - exact (basis_recon BV (f u)).
Qed.

Walking it:

  • intros f u — equality of linear maps is agreement at every point, so it suffices to show hom_of (mat_of f) u == f u.
  • The calc chain routes through lincomb (basis_vec BV) (basis_coords BV (f u)), the reconstruction of f u from its own coordinates.
  • The first link matches the two coordinate columns entry by entry: mat_of_coords (used with symmetry) says the column hom_of_coeff (mat_of f) u is exactly the true coordinates of f u, and lincomb_proper lifts that agreement through the reassembly.
  • The second link, basis_recon, is the basis round-trip: a vector rebuilt from its own coordinates is itself.

The other direction, mat_of_hom_of, says reading off a rebuilt map returns the original rectangle; together they package as vsiso_matrix, an isomorphism of vector spaces Hom(U,V) ≅ Matrix m n F. So the space of linear maps and the space of matrices are, once bases are fixed, the same vector space wearing two notations.

Composition is multiplication

Here is why matrix multiplication has the shape it does. Multiplication of matrices is defined in Impl/Matrix.v as a double-indexed sum, with no reference to linear maps at all:

Definition mat_mul {m n p : nat} (A : Matrix m n F) (B : Matrix n p F)
    : Matrix m p F :=
  fun i k => bigopF (fun j => A i j * B j k).

Entry (i, k) sums A i j * B j k over the shared index j — the familiar row-times-column rule, and nothing more; Impl/Matrix.v proves its bookkeeping laws (associativity, units, distributivity) purely as facts about sums. The claim that this operation means composition is a theorem, and it belongs to the representation, in Theory/MatRep.v: reading off a composite g ∘ f in bases gives the product of the two matrices read off separately.

[g∘f]=[g]⋅[f],\lbrack\, g \circ f \,\rbrack \;=\; \lbrack\, g \,\rbrack \,\cdot\, \lbrack\, f \,\rbrack,

where [ · ] denotes “the matrix of, in the fixed bases.”

The proof is one line, because the computation rule already did the work. Entry (i, j) of the composite’s matrix is, by definition of mat_of, the i-th coordinate of g (f (basis_vec BU j)) — and that is mat_of_coords for g, read at the point f (basis_vec BU j), whose right-hand side is exactly the sum Σ_k ⟦g⟧ i k * ⟦f⟧ k j that mat_mul computes. Verbatim:

Theorem mat_of_comp : forall (g : LinearMap F V W) (f : LinearMap F U V),
  mat_of BU BW (lm_comp g f)
    == mat_mul (mat_of BV BW g) (mat_of BU BV f).
Proof.
  intros g f i j.
  exact (mat_of_coords BV BW g (f (basis_vec BU j)) i).
Qed.

Walking it:

  • intros g f i j — matrices are equal when equal entrywise, so fix an entry (i, j).
  • The left side, mat_of BU BW (lm_comp g f) i j, unfolds to basis_coords BW (g (f (basis_vec BU j))) i. The right side, mat_mul (mat_of BV BW g) (mat_of BU BV f) i j, unfolds to hom_of_coeff (mat_of BV BW g) (f (basis_vec BU j)) i.
  • Those two are precisely the two sides of mat_of_coords for g at the point f (basis_vec BU j). The single exact supplies it. Composition and multiplication were never two facts to be reconciled — they are one computation read in two notations.

Using the representation

On paper, mat_mul is the multiplication everyone learned — entry (i,k)(i,k) pairs row ii against column kk:

(1234)(5678)=(1⋅5+2⋅71⋅6+2⋅83⋅5+4⋅73⋅6+4⋅8)=(19224350). \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot 5 + 2\cdot 7 & 1\cdot 6 + 2\cdot 8 \\ 3\cdot 5 + 4\cdot 7 & 3\cdot 6 + 4\cdot 8 \end{pmatrix} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}.

The source files do not carry a numeric example like this one; what they do carry are two checks that the whole structure assembles by instance resolution alone. Square matrices form a ring, with mat_mul as the multiplication and mat_id as its unit — the := _ asks Coq to find the instance, and it does:

Example matrix_ring_check (F : Type) (FF : Field F) (n : nat) :
  Ring (Matrix n n F) := _.

And the representation isomorphism itself instantiates at the coordinate space Fin.t n -> F with its standard basis on both sides:

Example vsiso_matrix_check (F : Type) (FF : Field F) (m n : nat) :=
  vsiso_matrix (FinVec_Basis (F := F) n) (FinVec_Basis m).

By the representation theorem, running a matrix multiplication is the same as composing the two linear maps the matrices stand for and reading the result off in coordinates. The numbers are the shadow; the maps are the thing casting it.
