riktig #17 — Everything sent eventually arrives

Seventeenth post documenting the riktig project. The channels thread closes its opening arc: post #16 proved the alternating-bit protocol safe; today it is proved live — under the one hypothesis an honest liveness theorem has to have.

What we are doing, and why. Verified software in Coq, built for modifiability. Post #16 proved the alternating-bit protocol never lets a corrupted or duplicated message through — safety, unconditional. Safety alone is a low bar: a protocol that discards every packet is perfectly safe. Today’s other half is liveness — does anything actually get through? — and the interesting part is not the proof but the hypothesis: what is the weakest assumption about the wire under which delivery can be guaranteed at all?

Safety was free; liveness has a price

Post #16’s theorem held for any channel behavior — total packet loss included, in which case delivery is silently empty. That’s honest but unsatisfying: you also want a guarantee that messages arrive. That guarantee cannot be unconditional — a channel that eats every packet forever really does deliver nothing, and no proof can say otherwise. So liveness needs a hypothesis, and the whole craft is finding the weakest one that’s still true of real networks.

The hypothesis lives in Channel/Fairness.v, and it mirrors the sender’s own structure: the sender emits one burst of k identical copies per message, and the wire trace is required to split into per-burst fragments in lock-step with the message list —

Fixpoint fair_wire (b : bit) (ms : list M) (k : nat) (wire : list Packet) : Prop :=
  match ms with
  | [] => wire = []
  | m :: ms' =>
      exists w1 w2,
        wire = w1 ++ w2 /\
        lossy_dup (sender_burst b m k) w1 /\
        w1 <> [] /\
        fair_wire (negb b) ms' k w2
  end.

Read the third conjunct: w1 <> [] — at least one copy of this message’s retransmission burst survives. That’s the entire fairness assumption. Everything else is unchanged: within each burst the channel can still drop some copies and duplicate others — anything lossy_dup allows — as long as it doesn’t drop all of them. (Reordering is not among lossy_dup’s powers — post #15 proved reorder_not_lossy_dup — and within a burst it would be invisible anyway, since every copy is the identical packet.) Fairness is not “no loss”; it is “not total loss.”

And the hypothesis is not an unrelated model bolted onto the side: a sanity-check lemma, fair_wire_lossy_dup, shows every fair wire trace is in particular a lossy_dup mangling of the full sender stream — exactly the adversary class abp_safety already quantified over, now with per-burst survival added on top. Fairness refines the safety model; it doesn’t replace it.

The theorem

Theorem abp_liveness : forall (k : nat) (b0 : bit) (ms : list M) (wire : list Packet),
  fair_wire b0 ms k wire -> deliver b0 wire = ms.

Under that one assumption, delivery is exactly the sent list — no subsequence slack, no missing messages. Compare the two theorems side by side and you can see the whole shape of the argument: abp_safety needed no assumption and delivered “a subsequence of”; abp_liveness needed “at least one copy per burst” and delivered “exactly.” The strength of the conclusion tracks the strength of the hypothesis, with nothing hidden in between — that correspondence is what a liveness proof is supposed to show you.

A companion theorem confirms the endpoint everyone actually cares about: over the perfect channel (no loss at all), delivery is exact too —

Theorem abp_collapse_perfect : forall (k : nat) (b0 : bit) (ms : list M) (wire : list Packet),
  k > 0 -> perfect (sender_stream b0 ms k) wire -> deliver b0 wire = ms.

— with k > 0 earning its keep as a genuine, necessary hypothesis: with a retransmission budget of zero, nothing is ever sent, so of course nothing arrives. (Small, honest detail: abp_liveness needed no such side condition — fair_wire with k = 0 is simply unsatisfiable for a nonempty message, since an empty burst can never contain a surviving packet. The right hypothesis makes the degenerate case impossible instead of needing a bolt-on exception.)

One scope note, stated in the file’s own header rather than discovered later: this is finite fairness, over finite message lists and wire traces. The stream-level statement — “a message offered infinitely often is eventually delivered” — needs coinductive traces, and is deliberately deferred until the library models runs as streams. The finite setting turned out to admit a clean finitary hypothesis, so nothing weaker than the honest claim is being sold.

This closes the arc the roadmap laid out: T1 gave channels a vocabulary (relations, not probabilities), T2 gave protocols a shape (state machines composed through a channel), T3 proved safety unconditionally, T4 proved liveness under the one hypothesis reality actually satisfies. The next protocols on the roadmap — Go-Back-N, selective repeat, the reliability core of TCP — inherit this whole structure; a new retransmission scheme only ever owes its own burst-delivery argument.
