The determinant, coordinate-free

A linear map f:U→Uf : U \to U sends a vector space to itself while respecting its two operations: f(u+v)=f(u)+f(v)f(u+v) = f(u) + f(v) and f(a*:u)=a*:f(u)f(a *: u) = a *: f(u). Once a basis is fixed — a chosen list of vectors from which every other vector is a unique combination — such a map turns into a square grid of scalars, its matrix: column jj records where the jj-th basis vector lands, read back in coordinates. Composition of maps becomes multiplication of matrices, exactly. This post is about a single scalar attached to such a map, the determinant, and the one theorem that makes it worth having: it multiplies.

What the determinant measures, before any basis

Fix an nn-dimensional space VV. A top form on it is a function ω\omega that eats nn vectors and returns a scalar, is linear in each of its nn slots separately, and alternates — it collapses to 00 the moment two of its arguments are equal. On the usual real plane the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by two vectors is such a form: double one edge and the area doubles (linearity), let the two edges coincide and the parallelogram is degenerate with area 00 (alternation). A top form is a chosen “unit of oriented volume.”

A linear map acts on a top form by pullback: feed the map’s outputs into the form instead of the raw vectors,

(f⋆ω)(v1,…,vn)=ω(f(v1),…,f(vn)).(f^\star \omega)(v_1,\dots,v_n) \;=\; \omega\big(f(v_1),\dots,f(v_n)\big).

The first thing to check is that this is still a top form — that precomposing every slot with one fixed linear map preserves both the per-slot linearity and the collapse-on-repeats law. The alternation half is proved directly in the file: if ω\omega is alternating, so is f⋆ωf^\star\omega. On the blackboard,

vi=vj(i≠j)⇒f(vi)=f(vj)⇒(f⋆ω)(v1,…,vn)=ω(f(v1),…,f(vn))=0.v_i = v_j \ (i \neq j) \;\Rightarrow\; f(v_i) = f(v_j) \;\Rightarrow\; (f^\star\omega)(v_1,\dots,v_n) = \omega\big(f(v_1),\dots,f(v_n)\big) = 0.

  Theorem fstar_alternating : Alternating omega -> Alternating fstar.
  Proof.
    intros Halt.
    apply Build_Alternating.
    intros vs i j Hij Heq.
    exact (alt_repeat Halt (fun k => f (vs k)) i j Hij
             (lm_proper f (vs i) (vs j) Heq)).
  Qed.

If two input vectors vi,vjv_i, v_j coincide, then their images f(vi),f(vj)f(v_i), f(v_j) coincide too — that is lm_proper f, the fact that a linear map respects the setoid equality — so alt_repeat, the collapse law of the original ω\omega, fires on the image family and the pulled-back form also returns 00.

Here is the coordinate-free definition the picture points at. On an nn-dimensional space the top forms make up a one-dimensional space: any two of them are proportional. So f⋆ωf^\star\omega, being another top form, must be a fixed scalar multiple of ω\omega,

f⋆ω=det⁡(f)*:ω,f^\star \omega \;=\; \det(f) *: \omega,

and that scalar — the same for every ω\omega, because they are all proportional — is the determinant. No basis appears. Here is exactly where the file’s formalization stops: the pullback-is-a-top-form direction above is proved, but the “top forms are one-dimensional” fact that turns proportionality into a single well-defined number is a real theorem not carried out here. What the file does instead is compute the same number through a basis, and then prove it does not depend on which basis.

The determinant through a basis, computed by Laplace expansion

Pick a basis BB. The map ff becomes a matrix mat_of B B f, and the determinant of a matrix is defined by expansion along its first column:

  Fixpoint detM {n : nat} (A : Matrix n n F) {struct n} : F :=
    match n as n0 return Matrix n0 n0 F -> F with
    | O => fun _ => 1
    | S m => fun B =>
        bigopF (fun i : Fin.t (S m) =>
          sign_at i * (B i Fin.F1 * detM (minor B i)))
    end A.

The empty matrix has determinant 11. Otherwise, run over the rows ii; each contributes the sign at ii (which is 1,−1,1,…1, -1, 1, \dots by recursion on the index, no integers involved) times the first-column entry Bi,1B_{i,1} times the determinant of the minor — the smaller matrix left after deleting row ii and the first column. That is the schoolbook cofactor expansion, and it is a genuine recursive program: over ℚ\mathbb{Q} it runs.

Example det2_Q :
  detM (mat2 (1#1)%Q (2#1)%Q (3#1)%Q (4#1)%Q) == ((-2)#1)%Q.
Proof.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

The determinant of 1234\begin{smallmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{smallmatrix} is 1⋅4−3⋅2=−21\cdot 4 - 3\cdot 2 = -2, and reflexivity closes it: the two sides are equal by computation alone, nothing to prove by hand.

The determinant of the map is then just this, read through the basis:

  Definition det (B : Basis n F U) (f : LinearMap F U U) : F :=
    detM (mat_of B B f).

The identity map has determinant one

The simplest sanity check: the map that leaves every vector alone scales the unit of volume by 11.

det⁡(id)=det⁡(I)=1.\det(\mathrm{id}) \;=\; \det(I) \;=\; 1.

  Theorem det_id : forall B : Basis n F U, det B (@lm_id F U FF VU) == 1.
  Proof.
    intros B.
    unfold det.
    calc (detM (mat_of B B (@lm_id F U FF VU)))
      == (detM (@mat_id F FF n))
      == (1 : F).
    - exact (detM_proper n (mat_of B B (@lm_id F U FF VU)) mat_id (mat_of_id B)).
    - exact (det_id n).
  Qed.

Two links in the calc chain. First, mat_of_id says the identity map’s matrix in any basis is the identity matrix II, and detM_proper — the determinant respects equality of matrices — carries the goal over to det⁡(I)\det(I). Second, det_id n is the matrix-level fact that det⁡(I)=1\det(I) = 1: expanding the identity matrix along its first column, only the top-left term survives (every other first-column entry is 00), its minor is again an identity matrix one size smaller, and the recursion bottoms out at 11.

The determinant multiplies

This is the theorem that justifies having the notion at all. Doing gg then ff scales volume by the product of the two scalings:

det⁡(f∘g)=det⁡(mat(f)⋅mat(g)){matrix of a composite}=det⁡(f)⋅det⁡(g){determinant of a product}.\det(f \circ g) \;=\; \det\bigl(\mathrm{mat}(f)\cdot\mathrm{mat}(g)\bigr) \quad\{\text{matrix of a composite}\} \;=\; \det(f)\cdot\det(g) \quad\{\text{determinant of a product}\}.

  Theorem det_comp : forall (B : Basis n F U) (f g : LinearMap F U U),
    det B (lm_comp f g) == det B f * det B g.
  Proof.
    intros B f g.
    unfold det.
    calc (detM (mat_of B B (lm_comp f g)))
      == (detM (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (mat_of B B g)))
      == (detM (mat_of B B f) * detM (mat_of B B g)).
    - exact (detM_proper n (mat_of B B (lm_comp f g))
               (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (mat_of B B g)) (mat_of_comp B B B f g)).
    - exact (detM_mul n (mat_of B B f) (mat_of B B g)).
  Qed.

The proof is two rewrites along the blackboard chain. mat_of_comp is the representation fact from the matrices post: the matrix of a composition is the product of the matrices, mat(f∘g)=mat(f)⋅mat(g)\mathrm{mat}(f\circ g) = \mathrm{mat}(f)\cdot\mathrm{mat}(g); detM_proper moves the goal onto that product. Then detM_mul — the determinant of a product of matrices is the product of their determinants — finishes it.

That second lemma, detM_mul, is the entire content. In Theory/Determinant.v it is not proved but assumed, as a named local hypothesis rather than a global axiom:

  Context (detM_mul : forall (m : nat) (A A' : Matrix m m F),
    detM (mat_mul A A') == detM A * detM A').

Anyone using det_comp must supply it. And it is supplied, over the rationals, in Impl/DetMul.v: a certified Gauss-Jordan reduction takes any square matrix by a list of elementary row operations either to the identity or to a matrix with a visible zero row, each operation multiplying the determinant by a known invertible factor, and the product law falls out by regrouping those factors — no division at proof time. The result is a real theorem for every field with decidable equality, and unconditional over ℚ\mathbb{Q}:

Corollary detM_mul_Q : forall (n : nat) (A B : Matrix n n Q),
  detM (mat_mul A B) == detM A * detM B.
Proof.
  exact (detM_mul_dec Q_DecEq).
Qed.

Discharging the hypothesis makes det_comp unconditional over ℚ\mathbb{Q} too:

Corollary det_comp_Q : forall (U : Type) (VU : VectorSpace Q U) (n : nat)
    (B : Basis n Q U) (f g : LinearMap Q U U),
  det B (lm_comp f g) == det B f * det B g.
Proof.
  intros U VU n B f g.
  exact (det_comp (fun m A A' => detM_mul_Q m A A') B f g).
Qed.

And it computes. Take the standard nonsingular matrix M=1234567810M = \begin{smallmatrix}1&2&3\\4&5&6\\7&8&10\end{smallmatrix}, whose determinant is −3-3, times a second matrix whose determinant is 55; both sides of the product law evaluate to −15-15 independently, by conversion:

Example detM_mul_Q_compute_lhs :
  detM (mat_mul mat3_ns mat3_b) == ((-15)#1)%Q.
Proof.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

Example detM_mul_Q_compute_rhs :
  detM mat3_ns * detM mat3_b == ((-15)#1)%Q.
Proof.
  reflexivity.
Qed.

Why the basis does not matter

Multiplicativity is what makes the basis drop out. Two bases B,B′B, B' are related by a change-of-basis matrix and its inverse, P=cobB′BP = \mathrm{cob}\, B'\, B and P−1=cobBB′P^{-1} = \mathrm{cob}\, B\, B', which multiply to the identity. Their determinants are therefore reciprocal:

det⁡(P)⋅det⁡(P−1)=det⁡(P⋅P−1){multiplicativity}=det⁡(I){P⋅P−1=I}=1{det⁡(I)=1}.\det(P)\cdot\det(P^{-1}) \;=\; \det(P\cdot P^{-1}) \quad\{\text{multiplicativity}\} \;=\; \det(I) \quad\{P\cdot P^{-1} = I\} \;=\; 1 \quad\{\det(I) = 1\}.

  Lemma cob_det_inv : forall B B' : Basis n F U,
    detM (cob B' B) * detM (cob B B') == 1.
  Proof.
    intros B B'.
    calc (detM (cob B' B) * detM (cob B B'))
      == (detM (mat_mul (cob B' B) (cob B B')))
      == (detM (@mat_id F FF n))
      == (1 : F).
    - symmetry. exact (detM_mul n (cob B' B) (cob B B')).
    - exact (detM_proper n (mat_mul (cob B' B) (cob B B')) mat_id
               (cob_inv B B')).
    - exact (det_id n).
  Qed.

detM_mul (right to left) fuses the two determinants into the determinant of the product; cob_inv says that product is II; det_id evaluates det⁡(I)\det(I) to 11. With that, the same map read in the two bases has the same determinant:

Writing MM for matB(f)\mathrm{mat}_B(f), the blackboard chain is:

det⁡B′(f)=det⁡(P⋅(M⋅P−1)){conjugation: the matrix of f in B′}=det⁡(P)⋅det⁡(M⋅P−1){multiplicativity} \det{}_{B'}(f) \;=\; \det(P \cdot (M \cdot P^{-1})) \quad\{\text{conjugation: the matrix of } f \text{ in } B'\} \;=\; \det(P)\cdot\det(M \cdot P^{-1}) \quad\{\text{multiplicativity}\} =det⁡(P)⋅(det⁡(M)⋅det⁡(P−1)){multiplicativity again}=det⁡(M)⋅(det⁡(P)⋅det⁡(P−1)){commute and regroup} \;=\; \det(P)\cdot\bigl(\det(M)\cdot\det(P^{-1})\bigr) \quad\{\text{multiplicativity again}\} \;=\; \det(M)\cdot\bigl(\det(P)\cdot\det(P^{-1})\bigr) \quad\{\text{commute and regroup}\} =det⁡(M)⋅1{det⁡(P)⋅det⁡(P−1)=1}=det⁡(M). \;=\; \det(M)\cdot 1 \quad\{\det(P)\cdot\det(P^{-1}) = 1\} \;=\; \det(M).

And the Coq proof is that chain, link for link:

  Theorem det_basis_indep : forall (B B' : Basis n F U) (f : LinearMap F U U),
    det B' f == det B f.
  Proof.
    intros B B' f.
    unfold det.
    calc (detM (mat_of B' B' f))
      == (detM (mat_mul (cob B' B) (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (cob B B'))))
      == (detM (cob B' B) * detM (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (cob B B')))
      == (detM (cob B' B) * (detM (mat_of B B f) * detM (cob B B')))
      == (detM (mat_of B B f) * (detM (cob B' B) * detM (cob B B')))
      == (detM (mat_of B B f) * 1)
      == (detM (mat_of B B f)).
    - exact (detM_proper n (mat_of B' B' f)
               (mat_mul (cob B' B) (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (cob B B')))
               (mat_of_conj B B' f)).
    - exact (detM_mul n (cob B' B) (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (cob B B'))).
    - exact (ring_mul_proper (detM (cob B' B)) (detM (cob B' B))
               (reflexivity (detM (cob B' B)))
               (detM (mat_mul (mat_of B B f) (cob B B')))
               (detM (mat_of B B f) * detM (cob B B'))
               (detM_mul n (mat_of B B f) (cob B B'))).
    - exact (mul_comm_swap (detM (cob B' B)) (detM (mat_of B B f))
               (detM (cob B B'))).
    - exact (ring_mul_proper (detM (mat_of B B f)) (detM (mat_of B B f))
               (reflexivity (detM (mat_of B B f)))
               (detM (cob B' B) * detM (cob B B')) 1
               (cob_det_inv B B')).
    - exact (ring_mul_1r (detM (mat_of B B f))).
  Qed.

Walking the links: mat_of_conj is the conjugation fact — the matrix of ff in B′B' is P⋅matB(f)⋅P−1P \cdot \mathrm{mat}_B(f) \cdot P^{-1} — and detM_proper moves the determinant onto that product. detM_mul splits off det⁡(P)\det(P), then (under ring_mul_proper, which rewrites inside the right factor) splits the remaining product. mul_comm_swap regroups the three scalars so that det⁡(P)\det(P) meets det⁡(P−1)\det(P^{-1}); cob_det_inv collapses that pair to 11; and ring_mul_1r erases the unit. The scalar the identity-map check, the multiplicativity theorem, and the basis-independence theorem all name is one and the same number — the coordinate-free determinant, arrived at by a route that does happen to pick coordinates but provably does not depend on the choice.
