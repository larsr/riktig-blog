Continuity and the derivative as a linear map

A limit records the value a function’s output settles toward as its input is driven arbitrarily close to a point. Written out with tolerances: L is the limit of f at a when, for every tolerance eps, there is a radius delta such that every input within delta of a produces an output within eps of L. The two “within” clauses are measured by norms — the length of a difference vector — so this makes sense between any two spaces that have a norm, not only on the real line. This post takes that definition, defines continuity from it, and then builds the derivative on top: not as a number, but as a linear map, which is the only shape that survives into more than one dimension.

Continuity is a limit that lands where you expect

Here is the limit definition, from Theory/Continuity.v. Both distances are norms of differences; ‖ x ‖ is the length of x, and u + - a is u minus a:

Definition limit_at (f : U -> V) (a : U) (L : V) : Prop :=
  forall eps : F, 0 < eps ->
    exists delta : F, 0 < delta /\
      forall u : U, ‖ u + - a ‖ < delta -> ‖ f u + - L ‖ < eps.

A function is continuous at a when its limit there is the value it actually takes — no jump, no removable hole:

Definition continuous_at (f : U -> V) (a : U) : Prop :=
  limit_at f a (f a).

Definition continuous (f : U -> V) : Prop :=
  forall a : U, continuous_at f a.

Unfolding the first: f is continuous at a when for every eps > 0 there is a delta > 0 so that ‖ u + - a ‖ < delta forces ‖ f u + - f a ‖ < eps. Inputs close to a have outputs close to f a.

Composition of continuous functions is continuous

The first real theorem. If f is continuous at a and g is continuous at the point f a, then the composite u ↦ g (f u) is continuous at a. On the blackboard it is a chase through two radii. To force g (f u) within eps of g (f a), continuity of g supplies a radius d1: any v within d1 of f a has g v within eps of g (f a). Now aim f at that d1: continuity of f supplies a radius d2 so that any u within d2 of a has f u within d1 of f a. Feed d2-close inputs in and the two guarantees compose:

‖u−a‖<d2⇒‖fu−fa‖<d1{continuity of f at a}⇒‖g(fu)−g(fa)‖<ε{continuity of g at fa}. \lVert u - a\rVert < d_2 \;\Rightarrow\; \lVert f\,u - f\,a\rVert < d_1 \quad\{\text{continuity of } f \text{ at } a\} \;\Rightarrow\; \lVert g(f\,u) - g(f\,a)\rVert < \varepsilon \quad\{\text{continuity of } g \text{ at } f\,a\}.

Here is the proof exactly as it stands in Theory/Continuity.v:

Theorem continuous_comp : forall (f : U -> V) (g : V -> W) (a : U),
  continuous_at f a -> continuous_at g (f a) ->
  continuous_at (fun u : U => g (f u)) a.
Proof.
  intros f g a Hf Hg eps Heps.
  destruct (Hg eps Heps) as [d1 [Hd1 Hg1]].
  destruct (Hf d1 Hd1) as [d2 [Hd2 Hf2]].
  exists d2.
  split.
  - exact Hd2.
  - intros u Hu.
    apply Hg1.
    apply Hf2.
    exact Hu.
Qed.

Walking it:

  • intros f g a Hf Hg eps Heps fixes a tolerance eps > 0 for the composite; the goal is to produce a radius that works.
  • destruct (Hg eps Heps) reads off g’s guarantee at f a: a radius d1 (positive, Hd1) with Hg1 saying anything within d1 of f a has its g-image within eps of g (f a).
  • destruct (Hf d1 Hd1) feeds that d1 back in as the tolerance for f at a, yielding a radius d2 with Hf2: within d2 of a, f is d1-close to f a.
  • exists d2 offers d2 as the composite’s radius, and split peels off its positivity (exact Hd2).
  • intros u Hu assumes ‖ u + - a ‖ < d2. Then apply Hg1 turns the goal into ‖ f u + - f a ‖ < d1, apply Hf2 turns that into ‖ u + - a ‖ < d2, and exact Hu is exactly the hypothesis. The two radii telescope.

The derivative is a linear map, not a number

In one variable the derivative is a slope — a single number f'(a), the factor by which f stretches a tiny displacement near a. That reading stops working the moment the input or output has more than one coordinate. A map from a plane to space cannot be summarized by one number; the right object is the whole best linear approximation near a. Write the approximation as

f(a+h)≈f(a)+Lh,f(a + h) \;\approx\; f(a) + L\,h,

where L is a linear map — a function that preserves addition and scaling, L (u + v) = L u + L v and L (c *: u) = c *: L u. The derivative is that L. “Best” means the leftover error is small compared to the size of the displacement h: it shrinks faster than h does, so that near a the linear term dominates. Nothing in this sentence mentions coordinates or dimension. The classical one-variable slope is just the special case where L is multiplication by a number.

The library states “best linear approximation” without dividing by h (division would need h nonzero and force a punctured neighborhood). Instead it bounds the leftover directly. From Theory/Derivative.v:

Definition is_deriv (f : U -> V) (a : U) (L : LinearMap F U V) : Prop :=
  lm_bounded L /\
  forall eps : F, 0 < eps ->
    exists delta : F, 0 < delta /\
      forall h : U, ‖ h ‖ < delta ->
        ‖ f (a + h) + - (f a + L h) ‖ <= eps * ‖ h ‖.

Read the second half as: for every eps, once h is small enough (‖ h ‖ < delta), the remainder f (a + h) - (f a + L h) is no larger than eps * ‖ h ‖. Since eps can be any positive number, the remainder is eventually a vanishingly small fraction of ‖ h ‖ — that is what “shrinks faster than h” means, stated without any division. At h = 0 both sides are zero, so no point is excluded.

The first half, lm_bounded L, is the requirement that L does not stretch vectors by an unbounded factor:

Definition lm_bounded (f : LinearMap F U V) : Prop :=
  exists C : F, 0 <= C /\ forall u : U, ‖ f u ‖ <= C * ‖ u ‖.

A single constant C caps the stretch: ‖ f u ‖ <= C * ‖ u ‖ for every u. This is exactly the condition that makes a linear map continuous.

The derivative is unique

There is only one such L. Suppose L1 and L2 both approximate f best at a. Fix any direction u. Both remainders are small, so their difference — which at first order is (L1 - L2) u — is smaller than eps * ‖ u ‖ for every positive eps. On the blackboard:

∀ε>0.‖L1u−L2u‖≤ε‖u‖⇒L1u=L2u,\forall \varepsilon > 0.\quad \lVert L_1 u - L_2 u\rVert \;\le\; \varepsilon\,\lVert u\rVert \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad L_1 u = L_2 u,

because a nonnegative quantity below every positive bound is zero, and this holds in every direction u. The bound on the left is the content of a supporting lemma, which tests both approximations along the scaled points t *: u and lets the common factor t cancel:

Lemma deriv_diff_bound : forall (f : U -> V) (a : U)
    (L1 L2 : LinearMap F U V) (eps : F) (u : U),
  is_deriv f a L1 -> is_deriv f a L2 -> 0 < eps ->
  ‖ L1 u + - L2 u ‖ <= eps * ‖ u ‖.

With that in hand, uniqueness is the “below every eps, hence zero” argument. Here is deriv_unique in full, verbatim from Theory/Derivative.v:

Theorem deriv_unique : forall (f : U -> V) (a : U)
    (L1 L2 : LinearMap F U V),
  is_deriv f a L1 -> is_deriv f a L2 -> L1 == L2.
Proof.
  intros f a L1 L2 HD1 HD2 u.
  apply sub_eq_zero.
  apply norm_def.
  apply nonneg_below_all_pos_is_zero.
  - apply norm_nonneg.
  - intros eps Heps.
    apply (le_trans (‖ L1 u + - L2 u ‖)
      ((eps * inv (1 + ‖ u ‖)) * ‖ u ‖) eps).
    + apply (deriv_diff_bound f a L1 L2 (eps * inv (1 + ‖ u ‖)) u HD1 HD2).
      apply mul_pos.
      * exact Heps.
      * apply pos_inv. apply one_plus_nonneg_pos. apply norm_nonneg.
    + apply (le_rewrite_l (‖ u ‖ * (eps * inv (1 + ‖ u ‖)))).
      * apply field_mul_comm.
      * apply mul_pad_div_le.
        -- apply lt_le. exact Heps.
        -- apply norm_nonneg.
Qed.

Walking it:

  • intros ... u — equality of linear maps means agreement at every point, so it suffices to prove L1 u == L2 u for an arbitrary u.
  • apply sub_eq_zero reduces L1 u == L2 u to showing the difference L1 u + - L2 u is zero, and apply norm_def reduces that to its norm being zero — a vector is zero exactly when its length is.
  • apply nonneg_below_all_pos_is_zero splits the goal into two: the norm is nonnegative (apply norm_nonneg), and it lies below every positive eps.
  • For the second, deriv_diff_bound is invoked at the padded tolerance eps * inv (1 + ‖ u ‖), giving ‖ L1 u + - L2 u ‖ <= (that) * ‖ u ‖; the padding lemma mul_pad_div_le then collapses (eps * inv (1 + ‖ u ‖)) * ‖ u ‖ to at most eps. Below every eps, so zero.

Linearity: the derivative of a negation

The derivative respects the vector-space operations: the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives (deriv_add), of a scaling is the same scaling of the derivative (deriv_scale), and of a negation is the negation of the derivative (deriv_neg). The cleanest of the three to show in full is the negation: if ff has derivative LL at aa, then −f-f has derivative −L-L there,

(−f)′(a)=−f′(a).(-f)'(a) \;=\; -\,f'(a).

Its whole content is one norm identity: the remainder of - f against - Lf is the negation of the remainder of f against Lf, and negation does not change a length —

∥−p−(−q−x)∥=∥−(p−(q+x))∥{push the minus out}=∥p−(q+x)∥{‖−v‖=‖v‖}.\bigl\lVert -p - (-q - x)\bigr\rVert \;=\; \bigl\lVert -(p - (q + x))\bigr\rVert \quad\{\text{push the minus out}\} \;=\; \bigl\lVert p - (q + x)\bigr\rVert \quad\{\lVert -v\rVert = \lVert v\rVert\}.

That identity is isolated as a lemma:

Lemma neg_remainder_norm : forall p q x : V,
  ‖ - p + - (- q + - x) ‖ == ‖ p + - (q + x) ‖.

And the rule itself:

Theorem deriv_neg : forall (f : U -> V) (a : U) (Lf : LinearMap F U V),
  is_deriv f a Lf -> is_deriv (fun u : U => - f u) a (- Lf).
Proof.
  intros f a Lf [Hbf HDf].
  split.
  - exact (bounded_neg Lf Hbf).
  - intros eps Heps.
    destruct (HDf eps Heps) as [d [Hd HR]].
    exists d.
    split.
    + exact Hd.
    + intros h Hh.
      apply (le_rewrite_l (‖ f (a + h) + - (f a + Lf h) ‖)).
      * symmetry.
        exact (neg_remainder_norm (f (a + h)) (f a) (Lf h)).
      * apply HR. exact Hh.
Qed.

Walking it:

  • intros f a Lf [Hbf HDf] unpacks the hypothesis into Hbf (the map Lf is bounded) and HDf (its remainder estimate). The split matches the two halves of is_deriv.
  • The boundedness half is bounded_neg Lf Hbf: negating a bounded map keeps the same bounding constant.
  • For the remainder half, destruct (HDf eps Heps) grabs the radius d and estimate HR that already work for f. The same d is offered (exists d) for - f.
  • apply (le_rewrite_l ...) rewrites the negated remainder into the original one via neg_remainder_norm (used with symmetry), after which apply HR closes the goal with f’s own estimate. The negation costs nothing — same radius, same bound.

The additive rule deriv_add splits the remainder of a sum into the two summand remainders and runs each at eps/2; the scaling rule deriv_scale pulls the scalar’s absolute value out of the norm. Both follow the same pattern: reduce to a norm identity, reuse the estimate.

The chain rule

The reason to make the derivative a linear map, rather than a number, appears here. The derivative of a composite is the composite of the derivatives:

(g∘f)′(a)=g′(fa)∘f′(a).(g \circ f)'(a) \;=\; g'(f\,a) \;\circ\; f'(a).

In the library the right-hand side is literally lm_comp Lg Lf, the composition of two linear maps — one line of linear algebra. The work is in the analysis: showing that the composite’s remainder really is small. Boundedness of the composite comes first, and it is short — the stretch factors multiply: if ‖gv‖≤Cg‖v‖\lVert g\,v\rVert \le C_g \lVert v\rVert and ‖fu‖≤Cf‖u‖\lVert f\,u\rVert \le C_f \lVert u\rVert, then

‖g(fu)‖≤Cg‖fu‖{g’s bound}≤Cg(Cf‖u‖){f’s bound, monotonicity}=(CgCf)‖u‖.\lVert g(f\,u)\rVert \;\le\; C_g\,\lVert f\,u\rVert \quad\{g\text{'s bound}\} \;\le\; C_g\,(C_f\,\lVert u\rVert) \quad\{f\text{'s bound, monotonicity}\} \;=\; (C_g C_f)\,\lVert u\rVert.

Lemma bounded_comp : forall (g : LinearMap F V W) (f : LinearMap F U V),
  lm_bounded g -> lm_bounded f -> lm_bounded (lm_comp g f).
Proof.
  intros g f [Cg [HCg Hbg]] [Cf [HCf Hbf]].
  exists (Cg * Cf).
  split.
  - apply mul_nonneg.
    + exact HCg.
    + exact HCf.
  - intros u.
    apply (le_trans _ (Cg * ‖ f u ‖) _).
    + exact (Hbg (f u)).
    + apply (le_trans _ (Cg * (Cf * ‖ u ‖)) _).
      * apply mul_le_mono_l.
        -- exact HCg.
        -- exact (Hbf u).
      * apply eq_le. symmetry. apply ring_mul_assoc.
Qed.

If g stretches by at most Cg and f by at most Cf, then g ∘ f stretches by at most Cg * Cf: apply f’s bound, then g’s bound on top, and reassociate the product.

The central step is a two-term split of the composite’s remainder. Take b = f a, y = f (a + h), and x = Lf h. The remainder of g ∘ f — measured against the candidate derivative Lg (Lf h) — is bounded by two pieces: g’s own remainder at the actual increment y - b = f (a + h) - f a, plus Lg applied to f’s remainder y - (b + x). In symbols,

∥gy−(gb+Lgx)∥≤∥g(b+(y−b))−(gb+Lg(y−b))∥⏟g’s remainder at the increment+∥Lg(y−(b+x))∥⏟Lg of f’s remainder. \bigl\lVert g\,y - (g\,b + L_g\,x)\bigr\rVert \;\le\; \underbrace{\bigl\lVert g(b + (y - b)) - (g\,b + L_g(y - b))\bigr\rVert}_{g\text{'s remainder at the increment}} \;+\; \underbrace{\bigl\lVert L_g\bigl(y - (b + x)\bigr)\bigr\rVert}_{L_g \text{ of } f\text{'s remainder}}. 

Lemma comp_remainder_split : forall (g : V -> W) (Lg : LinearMap F V W),
  Proper (equ ==> equ) g ->
  forall b y x : V,
  ‖ g y + - (g b + Lg x) ‖ <=
  ‖ g (b + (y + - b)) + - (g b + Lg (y + - b)) ‖ + ‖ Lg (y + - (b + x)) ‖.

The first term is small because g is differentiable at b = f a; the second is small because Lg is bounded and f’s remainder is small. Each is driven below eps/2 * ‖ h ‖, and the two halves add to eps * ‖ h ‖. The full proof, verbatim from Theory/Derivative.v:

Theorem deriv_comp : forall (f : U -> V) (g : V -> W) (a : U)
    (Lf : LinearMap F U V) (Lg : LinearMap F V W),
  Proper (equ ==> equ) g ->
  is_deriv f a Lf -> is_deriv g (f a) Lg ->
  is_deriv (fun u : U => g (f u)) a (lm_comp Lg Lf).
Proof.
  intros f g a Lf Lg Pg [Hbf HDf] [Hbg HDg].
  split.
  - exact (bounded_comp Lg Lf Hbg Hbf).
  - destruct Hbf as [Cf [HCf HbF]].
    destruct Hbg as [Cg [HCg HbG]].
    intros eps Heps.
    destruct (pos_min_exists 1 (half eps * inv (1 + Cg)) lt_0_1
        (mul_pos (half eps) (inv (1 + Cg)) (half_pos eps Heps)
           (pos_inv (1 + Cg) (one_plus_nonneg_pos Cg HCg))))
      as [e1 [He1 [He1_1 He1_g]]].
    destruct (HDf e1 He1) as [df [Hdf HRf]].
    destruct (HDg (half eps * inv (1 + (1 + Cf)))
        (mul_pos (half eps) (inv (1 + (1 + Cf))) (half_pos eps Heps)
           (pos_inv (1 + (1 + Cf))
              (one_plus_nonneg_pos (1 + Cf)
                 (lt_le 0 (1 + Cf) (one_plus_nonneg_pos Cf HCf))))))
      as [dg [Hdg HRg]].
    destruct (pos_min_exists df (dg * inv (1 + Cf)) Hdf
        (mul_pos dg (inv (1 + Cf)) Hdg
           (pos_inv (1 + Cf) (one_plus_nonneg_pos Cf HCf))))
      as [d [Hd [Hd_f Hd_g]]].
    exists d.
    split.
    + exact Hd.
    + intros h Hh.
      apply (le_trans _
        (‖ g (f a + (f (a + h) + - f a))
           + - (g (f a) + Lg (f (a + h) + - f a)) ‖
         + ‖ Lg (f (a + h) + - (f a + Lf h)) ‖) _).
      * exact (comp_remainder_split g Lg Pg (f a) (f (a + h)) (Lf h)).
      * apply (le_trans _ (half eps * ‖ h ‖ + half eps * ‖ h ‖) _).
        -- apply add_le_mono.
           (* g's remainder at the increment k h := f (a+h) - f a. *)
           ++ apply (le_trans _
                ((half eps * inv (1 + (1 + Cf)))
                 * ‖ f (a + h) + - f a ‖) _).
              ** apply HRg.
                 apply (le_lt_trans _ ((1 + Cf) * ‖ h ‖) _).
                 --- exact (deriv_increment_bound f a h Lf e1 Cf HCf HbF
                       He1_1 (HRf h (lt_le_trans (‖ h ‖) d df Hh Hd_f))).
                 --- apply (lt_le_trans _
                       ((1 + Cf) * (dg * inv (1 + Cf))) _).
                     +++ apply mul_lt_mono_l.
                         *** apply one_plus_nonneg_pos. exact HCf.
                         *** exact (lt_le_trans (‖ h ‖) d
                               (dg * inv (1 + Cf)) Hh Hd_g).
                     +++ apply eq_le.
                         apply field_mul_div_cancel.
                         apply pos_neq_0.
                         apply one_plus_nonneg_pos.
                         exact HCf.
              ** apply (le_trans _
                   ((half eps * inv (1 + (1 + Cf)))
                    * ((1 + Cf) * ‖ h ‖)) _).
                 --- apply mul_le_mono_l.
                     +++ apply mul_nonneg.
                         *** apply lt_le. apply half_pos. exact Heps.
                         *** apply lt_le. apply pos_inv.
                             apply one_plus_nonneg_pos.
                             apply lt_le. apply one_plus_nonneg_pos.
                             exact HCf.
                     +++ exact (deriv_increment_bound f a h Lf e1 Cf HCf
                           HbF He1_1
                           (HRf h (lt_le_trans (‖ h ‖) d df Hh Hd_f))).
                 --- apply (le_rewrite_l
                       (((half eps * inv (1 + (1 + Cf))) * (1 + Cf))
                        * ‖ h ‖)).
                     +++ apply ring_mul_assoc.
                     +++ apply mul_le_mono_r.
                         *** apply norm_nonneg.
                         *** apply (le_rewrite_l
                               ((1 + Cf)
                                * (half eps * inv (1 + (1 + Cf))))).
                             ---- apply field_mul_comm.
                             ---- apply mul_pad_div_le.
                                  ++++ apply lt_le. apply half_pos.
                                       exact Heps.
                                  ++++ apply lt_le.
                                       apply one_plus_nonneg_pos.
                                       exact HCf.
           (* Lg applied to f's remainder. *)
           ++ apply (le_trans _ (Cg * ‖ f (a + h) + - (f a + Lf h) ‖) _).
              ** exact (HbG (f (a + h) + - (f a + Lf h))).
              ** apply (le_trans _ (Cg * (e1 * ‖ h ‖)) _).
                 --- apply mul_le_mono_l.
                     +++ exact HCg.
                     +++ exact (HRf h (lt_le_trans (‖ h ‖) d df Hh Hd_f)).
                 --- apply (le_rewrite_l ((Cg * e1) * ‖ h ‖)).
                     +++ apply ring_mul_assoc.
                     +++ apply mul_le_mono_r.
                         *** apply norm_nonneg.
                         *** apply (le_trans _
                               (Cg * (half eps * inv (1 + Cg))) _).
                             ---- apply mul_le_mono_l.
                                  ++++ exact HCg.
                                  ++++ exact He1_g.
                             ---- apply mul_pad_div_le.
                                  ++++ apply lt_le. apply half_pos.
                                       exact Heps.
                                  ++++ exact HCg.
        -- apply eq_le. apply half_mul_sum.
Qed.

The proof is long because it is bookkeeping three tolerances at once, but its shape is exactly the blackboard argument. Walking the structure:

  • split produces the two halves. The first is bounded_comp Lg Lf, the composite is bounded.
  • The tolerances are chosen up front. e1 is f’s tolerance, kept at most 1 (so f’s increment stays controlled) and at most (eps/2) / (1 + Cg) (so that Lg of f’s remainder is under eps/2). dg is g’s radius, run at tolerance (eps/2) / (1 + (1 + Cf)). The composite’s radius d is the smaller of df and dg / (1 + Cf), the second factor accounting for f’s own stretch.
  • intros h Hh and the first le_trans install comp_remainder_split: the composite remainder is at most the two-term sum.
  • The second le_trans targets eps/2 * ‖ h ‖ + eps/2 * ‖ h ‖, split by add_le_mono into one bound per term.
  • The first term (g’s remainder at the true increment f (a+h) - f a) uses deriv_increment_bound — the increment is (1 + Cf)-controlled — fed into g’s estimate HRg, then the padding lemma trims the constant to eps/2.
  • The second term (Lg of f’s remainder) uses the bound on Lg (constant Cg) and f’s estimate HRf at tolerance e1; the choice e1 <= (eps/2) / (1 + Cg) brings it under eps/2.
  • The closing half_mul_sum adds the two halves back to eps * ‖ h ‖.

The linear-map framing is what makes the identity (g ∘ f)' = g' ∘ f' literally the composition lm_comp Lg Lf: no matrices, no coordinates, no separate one-variable and many-variable statements. The one-variable chain rule and the Jacobian chain rule are the same theorem read in different spaces.
