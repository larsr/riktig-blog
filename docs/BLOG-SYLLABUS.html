Blog syllabus — the mental tools, in order, before anything else

Every post here follows docs/BLOG-STYLE.md without exception. The order matters: each entry may assume the reader has read the ones above it (recapped in a few sentences, not by reference), and nothing below it. No post in this list mentions “riktig,” “the project,” or why any of it matters — that framing is reserved for a single capstone post at the very end of the whole syllabus (not written yet, not scheduled until everything above it exists).

Status markers: [has code] — a real .v file exists, write the post from it. [needs code] — no implementation exists yet; do not write the post until the Coq exists. [rewrite] — an old post covers this ground but needs a full rewrite under the new style, not an edit. [new] — no old post exists for this at all.

Part 1 — The mathematical hierarchy

  1. What is a mathematical structure? [rewrite of #0001] — interface vs. structure (vocabulary + laws), the monoid, why “prove once, hold everywhere” matters. Show id_unique/zero_unique_l in full, blackboard + Coq.
  2. Groups: undoing, and the cancellation trick [new, split out of old #0001] — group axioms, cancel_l/cancel_r proved in full (both directions), neg_involutive, the group-hom lemmas (hom_zero/hom_neg) with a concrete instance shown running. 2a. What a calculational proof compiles to [has code: Lib/Calc.v, Lambda/CalcExamples.v] — the calc x == y { law } == z { law } chains used throughout the series are a tactic, not a language feature: the written chain’s endpoints are unified against the goal, each step becomes a transitivity over the registered equivalence relation, and each justification is closed by trying rewrite forward, rewrite backward, apply, symmetric apply. The post must also explain the three ideas that make this work and that the whole series leans on: setoid equality (why == is a chosen equivalence relation per type, not Coq’s built-in = — Q’s 1/2 == 2/4 as the motivating case), typeclass resolution (how Coq finds the Transitive instance for the relation in the goal), and implicit arguments (why calc steps can omit the carrier and relation entirely). Filename s02a-... so it sorts between posts 2 and 3.
  3. Commutative structure: abelian groups and rings [new] — AbGroup’s reshuffling kit (add_interchange), then Ring (distributivity, the Assoc/Commutes/Idl/Idr generic-law trick that lets one proof serve + and * both), a concrete Z instance computing something.
  4. Fields, and why Q is more interesting than it looks [new] — field axioms, mul_cancel_l, and the payoff: Q with Qeq as its equality, proving 1/2 == 2/4 is a real, load-bearing fact, not a footnote.
  5. Vector spaces without coordinates [rewrite of #0009] — Hom(U,V) as a vector space, End V as the first noncommutative ring, one full proof shown end to end (hom_zero reused, or cancel_l reused inside a vector-space proof — show the reuse, that’s the point).

Part 11 — New ground (2026-07-18 wave)

  1. The determinant, characterized abstractly, and multiplied for any field [has code: Interface/Determinant.v, Impl/DetInterface.v, Impl/DetMulGeneral.v] — sequel to entry 21. A Determinant class: multilinear, alternating, normalized against a chosen basis, with no reference to any concrete computation; detM satisfies it; and det(AB) = det(A)det(B) unconditionally for any field, via the “uniqueness of an alternating multilinear form” argument rather than the Leibniz-sum route the name “Cauchy-Binet” suggests.
  2. A subspace always has a basis [has code: Theory/SubspaceBasis.v] — sequel to entry 38. Induction on ambient dimension: split off a hyperplane, recurse, and either the subspace was already inside the hyperplane or a witness vector gets adjoined — the two disclosed axioms this needs (classical choice, indefinite description) and exactly why an abstract field forces them.
  3. Safety isn’t enough: prefix delivery needs an acknowledgement [has code: Channel/Ack.v, Channel/GoBackN.v, Channel/ABP.v] — sequel to entries 25-26. A concrete counterexample where the alternating-bit protocol delivers a genuine subsequence that is NOT a prefix, why sliding-window delivery gets prefix for free, and the stop-and-wait-with-ack protocol that recovers it for the two-value protocol by halting on silence.
  4. When the obvious compiler is wrong: SKI, revisited [has code: Lambda/SKI.v] — sequel to entry 13. A machine-checked counterexample showing the textbook bracket-abstraction translation does not simulate lambda-calculus reduction step for step (weak combinatory reduction isn’t extensional), and the one-line occurs-check fix that restores it, with a real simulation theorem.
  5. What it means for a parser combinator to be correct, and using one to read a real message format [has code: Theory/Parser.v, Programs/ABNF/*.v] — sequel to entries 33 and 35. Soundness (“never invents input”) and completeness (“never spuriously fails”) stated and proved for the combinator library itself, not just checked on worked examples; then a full worked walkthrough of taking a small but realistic message format (not the one-rule toy grammar in entry 35), writing its ABNF grammar as text, and using parse_with_abnf to get a working parser with no hand-written parsing code.
  6. exp’s derivative, log, and an honest kink in ReLU [has code: Theory/Log.v, Theory/MLDerivatives.v] — sequel to entry 44. Why log and exp’s own derivative both need a hypothesis this codebase’s four Exp laws don’t supply (surjectivity, i.e. completeness) and say so explicitly instead of hiding it; ReLU’s one-sided derivatives, proved for real away from zero, with no claim at the kink where the classical function genuinely isn’t differentiable.
  7. Curry, uncurry, and what “isomorphism” buys you over “round trip” [has code: Theory/BilinearVS.v, Theory/VSIso.v] — sequel to entry 5. Bilinear maps form a vector space the same way linear maps do; curry and uncurry already round-tripped, but packaging that round trip as a formal vector-space isomorphism is a strictly stronger, reusable fact — and the same treatment gives every VSIso a categorical ≅, for free.
  8. Teaching ring to a typeclass tower [has code: Theory/Group.v, Theory/Ring.v, Theory/Field.v, Impl/Zr.v, Impl/Qf.v] — an autorewrite database that closes routine group identities in one line, and why Coq’s ring/field tactics cannot be registered on an abstract Ring/Field class at all (commutativity isn’t assumed in general; a Section-local registration doesn’t survive the section closing) — only at a concrete carrier like Z or Q.

Part 12 — Multivariate calculus in use, and a little mechanics

41a. Gradients and partial derivatives you can point to [has code: Theory/Derivative.v (partial_at), Theory/Jacobian.v, Theory/MatVec.v] — how to actually work with functions of several variables in proofs: a derivative is a linear map, a partial derivative is that map applied to one basis vector, the Jacobian is its matrix against chosen bases, and the chain rule is matrix multiplication. Worked usage: extract a partial derivative from a concrete map, and show jacobian_chain_rule consuming two derivatives. Filename s41a-... so it sorts after entry 41. 55. Energy is conserved because the Hamiltonian equations say so [has code: Theory/Hamiltonian.v] — for one-dimensional motion, define H(q,p)=p2/2m+V(q)H(q,p) = p^2/2m + V(q), take Hamilton’s equations q̇=p/m\dot q = p/m, ṗ=−V′(q)\dot p = -V'(q) as hypotheses on a pair of paths, and prove ddtH(q(t),p(t))=0\frac{d}{dt} H(q(t), p(t)) = 0 from the sum, product (bilinear), and chain rules alone. Math first, Coq beside it, per Rule 5. 56. The action: mass times velocity times distance [has code: Theory/Action.v] — the dimensional-analysis observation that a quantity built from a moving body must scale linearly in its mass, its velocity, and the distance travelled, and the theorem behind it: the integral of momentum over position equals the integral of mv2m\,v^2 over time, ∫q(a)q(b)pdq=∫abmv(t)2dt\int_{q(a)}^{q(b)} p\,dq \;=\; \int_a^b m\,v(t)^2\,dt, proved by change of variables (chain rule + the fundamental theorem of calculus, the latter a disclosed hypothesis since the Integral interface deliberately states no FTC law). Scaling in the mass is integral linearity; additivity over path segments is integral additivity. Math notation first, then the Coq rendering of the same statements.

Part 13 — From specification to executable

  1. From a sorting specification to a running binary, one datatype at a time [has code: theories/Programs/Memprog/*; needs code: Theory/Memprog/MemLaws.v — do not write this post before that file exists with at least one proved rewrite law] — the refinement chain: a memory-monad program, its expression level, stack frames, machine code, x86-64 bytes, and an ELF image, each a separate datatype with its own emulator, all agreeing on the same inputs by vm_compute, and the final bytes running as a native Linux process with checked exit codes (make memprog-elf). The post walks the swap program through every level including its actual ELF bytes, shows one within-level program transformation as a calc chain over the memory-law algebra, and states plainly which cross-level links are proved and which are open obligations (Theory/Memprog/Obligations.v is the honest ledger). The problem movement: hand-compilation is a sequence of unchecked design decisions, and every one is a place where correctness silently dies; the post’s claim is that each step can instead be a rewrite with respect to an equivalence on what memory means.
