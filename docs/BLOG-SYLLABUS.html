Blog syllabus — the mental tools, in order, before anything else

Every post here follows docs/BLOG-STYLE.md without exception. The order matters: each entry may assume the reader has read the ones above it (recapped in a few sentences, not by reference), and nothing below it. No post in this list mentions “riktig,” “the project,” or why any of it matters — that framing is reserved for a single capstone post at the very end of the whole syllabus (not written yet, not scheduled until everything above it exists).

Status markers: [has code] — a real .v file exists, write the post from it. [needs code] — no implementation exists yet; do not write the post until the Coq exists. [rewrite] — an old post covers this ground but needs a full rewrite under the new style, not an edit. [new] — no old post exists for this at all.

Part 1 — The mathematical hierarchy

  1. What is a mathematical structure? [rewrite of #0001] — interface vs. structure (vocabulary + laws), the monoid, why “prove once, hold everywhere” matters. Show id_unique/zero_unique_l in full, blackboard + Coq.
  2. Groups: undoing, and the cancellation trick [new, split out of old #0001] — group axioms, cancel_l/cancel_r proved in full (both directions), neg_involutive, the group-hom lemmas (hom_zero/hom_neg) with a concrete instance shown running.
  3. Commutative structure: abelian groups and rings [new] — AbGroup’s reshuffling kit (add_interchange), then Ring (distributivity, the Assoc/Commutes/Idl/Idr generic-law trick that lets one proof serve + and * both), a concrete Z instance computing something.
  4. Fields, and why Q is more interesting than it looks [new] — field axioms, mul_cancel_l, and the payoff: Q with Qeq as its equality, proving 1/2 == 2/4 is a real, load-bearing fact, not a footnote.
  5. Vector spaces without coordinates [rewrite of #0009] — Hom(U,V) as a vector space, End V as the first noncommutative ring, one full proof shown end to end (hom_zero reused, or cancel_l reused inside a vector-space proof — show the reuse, that’s the point).
  6. Inner products and geometric algebra [rewrite, folded from old #0001’s tail section + #0013] — the polarization identity, proved in full with both the blackboard argument and the Coq, exactly like #0001’s best section but given its own post and more room.

Part 2 — Category theory

  1. Categories, in the way a programmer already half-knows them [has code: Interface/Category.v, Theory/Category.v] — objects and arrows, the hom-setoid, identity/associativity, one worked example in a genuinely concrete category (not Set/Type abstractly — pick something with visible arrows).
  2. Functors and natural transformations [has code] — a functor as a structure-preserving map between categories, naturality proved for one real example.
  3. Initial and terminal objects, products, and the universal-property style of proof [has code: Theory/InitialTerminal.v, Theory/Product.v] — uniqueness-up-to-isomorphism proved in full.
  4. Cartesian closed categories [has code: Theory/Exponential.v, the CCC bundle] — exponentials, currying, and why this is exactly the structure a lambda calculus needs — the bridge into Part 3.

Part 3 — Programs as data: lambda calculus and its transformations

  1. The lambda calculus you can run [rewrite of #0004] — de Bruijn terms, the small-step evaluator, step_fn sound and complete, running a real term to a normal form in the post.
  2. Program equality is a setoid, and optimizations are rewrites [rewrite of #0005] — the conv relation, Church booleans, if_of_true proved and walked exactly like a group-theory proof.
  3. Combinators: SKI and bracket abstraction [has code: Lambda/SKI.v] — eliminating variables entirely, babs_correct proved, one term compiled to combinators and run.
  4. Continuation-passing style, concretely [has code: Lambda/CPS.v] — the transform, why it exists (making control flow into data), one real term CPS-translated and its evaluation walked step by step.
  5. Defunctionalization: closures without closures [needs code] — Reynolds’s original technique (Definitional Interpreters for Higher-Order Programming Languages, 1972) and Danvy & Nielsen’s modern treatment (Defunctionalization at Work, 2001): replace a higher-order function space with a first-order datatype plus an apply dispatcher, and prove the two programs compute the same thing. Queue the Coq implementation before this post is written.
  6. A-normal form: naming every intermediate result [needs code] — Flanagan, Sabry, Duba & Felleisen’s ANF transform (The Essence of Compiling with Continuations, 1993): every subexpression gets a name via let, and this is what makes a compiler’s job tractable. Queue the Coq implementation before this post is written.

Part 4 — Analysis and geometry (existing coverage, needs a full rewrite pass)

  1. Order and absolute value, axiomatically [rewrite of #0008’s order section] — why abs is a law-abiding operation, not a case-split on the sign; 0 <= 1 as a genuine theorem.
  2. Normed spaces, and what a limit is [rewrite of #0011] — the ε-N definition, lim_unique proved by the ε/2 argument in full.
  3. Continuity and the derivative as a linear map [rewrite of #0012] — the Fréchet derivative, the chain rule, walked exactly like the polarization proof.
  4. Matrices are a representation, not a definition [rewrite of #0010] — Hom(U,V) ≅ Matrix, matrix multiplication is composition, the representation theorem proved and used.
  5. The determinant, coordinate-free [rewrite/merge of #0014, #0023, #0033] — one post, one throughline: det as “how a map scales a top alternating form,” multiplicativity as the payoff theorem, no scattered side-quests.
  6. Geometric algebra’s payoff: rotations and reflections [rewrite, extends part 1’s polarization post] — now that categories and linear maps exist, show what geometric algebra is actually for.
