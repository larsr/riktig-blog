Electron spin’s double cover, and quark color’s two quantum numbers

Standard quantum mechanics introduces an electron’s spin as a bare postulate. A textbook states that the electron carries an intrinsic angular momentum of one-half a fundamental unit, calls this “spin one-half,” and moves on to using the fact — the number 1/21/2 is simply handed down, not derived from anything smaller. One of its stranger consequences is presented the same way: a spin-1/2 particle’s quantum state has to be rotated a full 720°720°, not 360°360°, before it returns to where it started, and a student is given this rule with no visual or algebraic reason for why 720720 rather than 360360. The equations that govern spin — the Pauli equation, and the Schrödinger equation behind it — contain the imaginary unit ii, and the usual justification for its presence is that the equations do not work without it, not any account of what a complex number is doing inside a description of a spinning particle. The Pauli matrices, the three 2×22\times2 tables that encode spin’s algebra, are typically memorized as a set of anticommutation relations rather than derived from a smaller set of assumptions. None of this is a strawman: it is how the material is actually taught, a working rule with its geometric reason stripped out.

A separate question concerns quarks. A quark carries a property called color charge, with three possible values, and gluons — the particles that carry the strong force between quarks — are tied to that same threefold structure. Where does the number three come from, and is there any geometric reason behind it? Is there a reason a quark needs a color label at all, separate from spin, and does the group that organizes color charge, written SU(3)SU(3), have to have the particular structure it has?

Both questions have partial answers once vectors are allowed to multiply.

Multiplying vectors, and an odd-dimensional stand-in for ii

A geometric algebra multiplies vectors, distributing over addition but not assumed to commute, subject to one governing law: a vector’s square is a plain scalar, its own squared length, vv=‖v‖2v\,v = \lVert v\rVert^2. Splitting the product of two different vectors into its symmetric and antisymmetric halves recovers the ordinary inner product in the symmetric half, and produces a new kind of object, a bivector, in the antisymmetric half whenever the two vectors are independent.

Three-dimensional space generates the algebra Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0): three vectors e1,e2,e3e_1, e_2, e_3, each squaring to 11 and pairwise anticommuting (eiej=−ejeie_ie_j = -e_je_i for i≠ji\ne j), together with everything their products generate. That closes off at eight dimensions: the scalar 11; the three vectors e1,e2,e3e_1,e_2,e_3; the three bivectors e12,e13,e23e_{12},e_{13},e_{23} (products of two distinct generators); and one further object, the trivector e123=e1e2e3e_{123}=e_1e_2e_3. The even part — everything built from an even number of vector factors, spanned by {1,e12,e13,e23}\{1,e_{12},e_{13},e_{23}\} — is closed under multiplication on its own: multiplying two even elements always produces another even element.

e123e_{123}, usually called the pseudoscalar and written I3I_3, plays the algebraic role usually assigned to ii. Unlike ii, it is not postulated: it is an ordinary product of three real, mutually perpendicular unit vectors, an oriented unit of three-dimensional volume. It squares to −1-1:

  Lemma c3_I3_sq : c3_I3 * c3_I3 == - (1 : C3 F).
  Proof. c3_compute_mul. Qed.

and it commutes with every one of the three generators — checked directly for each, not assumed:

  Lemma c3_e1_I3_comm : c3_e1 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e1.
  Proof. c3_compute_mul. Qed.

  Lemma c3_e2_I3_comm : c3_e2 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e2.
  Proof. c3_compute_mul. Qed.

  Lemma c3_e3_I3_comm : c3_e3 * c3_I3 == c3_I3 * c3_e3.
  Proof. c3_compute_mul. Qed.

Commuting with everything is what a stand-in for ii needs, and it holds here for a reason about dimension, not a coincidence about these three lemmas: to move a generator eie_i across I3=e1e2e3I_3=e_1e_2e_3, it has to be walked past the other two generators sharing I3I_3 — two anticommuting swaps, an even number, so the two sign flips cancel and eie_i commutes with I3I_3 after all. Had the ambient space been even-dimensional instead (two generators, not three), the analogous pseudoscalar would only have one other generator to cross — an odd number of swaps — and would anticommute rather than commute. Three is odd; that parity is what makes I3I_3 central, not an accident specific to Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0).

With I3I_3 available as a real, central square root of −1-1, a spin state can be represented without complex numbers at all. Following Hestenes (1967; Am. J. Phys., 1984) — a genuinely published account, textbook-adjacent even though most physicists still learn the complex-matrix version — the nonrelativistic electron’s spin state is a real even multivector, ψ=ρR\psi=\sqrt\rho\,R, where ρ\rho is a nonnegative scalar (a probability density) and RR is a unit even element, with multiplication by I3I_3 standing wherever the standard Pauli equation multiplies by ii. Reproducing the Pauli equation’s actual dynamics from this substitution is Hestenes’s result, not something derived from scratch below; what the algebra above supplies is the fact that makes the substitution legitimate in the first place — a real object, I3I_3, that squares to −1-1 and commutes with every vector, built from three real, geometric directions rather than postulated.

Color charge as an abstract Lie algebra, and why rank two

A Lie algebra is a vector space with an extra operation, a bracket [x,y][x,y], that is bilinear in each argument, antisymmetric ([x,y]=−[y,x][x,y]=-[y,x]), and satisfies the Jacobi identity, [x,[y,z]]+[y,[z,x]]+[z,[x,y]]=0[x,[y,z]]+[y,[z,x]]+[z,[x,y]]=0 for every x,y,zx,y,z. Quark color charge is organized by one particular 8-dimensional Lie algebra, 𝔰𝔲(3)\mathfrak{su}(3): eight generators T1,…,T8T_1,\dots,T_8, and a bracket fixed by nine numbers, the structure constants:

   THE TABLE.  The nonzero values, one representative per orbit under
   the S_3 permutation action (all other entries of the same
   unordered triple follow by the antisymmetry proved below):
     f_123 = 1
     f_147 = f_246 = f_257 = f_345 = 1/2
     f_156 = f_367 = -1/2
     f_458 = f_678 = sqrt(3)/2
   This is the standard table (e.g. Georgi, "Lie Algebras in Particle
   Physics"; Wikipedia's "Gell-Mann matrices" structure-constant
   table).

Every other nonzero value of ff is one of these nine with its sign changed by however many swaps it takes to sort its three indices into increasing order. The bracket itself is built directly from the resulting table, as a double sum over the eight generators:

  Definition bracket (x y : Fin.t 8 -> F) : Fin.t 8 -> F :=
    fun c => bigopF (fun a => bigopF (fun b => x a * y b * f a b c)).

Where does the number three, and SU(3)SU(3)’s particular shape, actually enter? Not by finding a literal “3” anywhere in the table — a table like this simply is what 𝔰𝔲(3)\mathfrak{su}(3) means — but through a subtler fact: which pairs of generators commute with each other. T3T_3 and T8T_8 do:

f38c=0 for every output c, since no line of the table above pairs 3 with 8.f_{38c}=0 \text{ for every output } c, \text{ since no line of the table above pairs } 3 \text{ with } 8.

  Lemma su3_cartan_38 : bracket (e g3) (e g8) == 0.
  Proof.
    intros d.
    rewrite (bracket_e_e g3 g8 d).
    unfold f, gnum, fin_to_nat, sc; cbn.
    reflexivity.
  Qed.

(e i is the standard basis vector, 11 at coordinate ii and 00 elsewhere; g3, g8 name the third and eighth of the eight basis directions.) bracket_e_e (bracket (e i) (e j) d == f i j d) turns the bracket of two basis generators directly into a single structure-constant lookup; unfolding the table at 33 and 88 then finds nothing but zeros, for every output coordinate, closed by reflexivity.

A maximal set of mutually commuting generators is called a Cartan subalgebra, and its size is the algebra’s rank. T3T_3 and T8T_8 commuting is the algebraic fact behind SU(3)SU(3)’s rank being two: two, not one, color-type quantities can be given a definite value on the same state simultaneously. That is the actual content behind quarks needing three colors rather than one: a single label only distinguishes states along one measurable axis, while two independent, simultaneously measurable labels are what it takes to tell red, green, and blue apart as three distinct combinations of two numbers, rather than three points on one line.

No single Clifford algebra is currently known to contain the full Standard Model group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1)SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1) the way Cl(2,0)\mathrm{Cl}(2,0) contains U(1)U(1) and Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) contains SU(2)SU(2); 𝔰𝔲(3)\mathfrak{su}(3) here is built and checked on its own terms, as an abstract Lie algebra, with no embedding into any geometric algebra claimed.

The double cover: a rotor and its negation act identically

Producing a rotation from Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) needs one more operation: reversal, which reverses the order of the vector factors inside every blade. Reversing a product of kk vectors costs k(k−1)/2k(k-1)/2 transpositions of adjacent anticommuting factors, and that count is odd exactly when k∈{2,3}k\in\{2,3\} — so reversal negates exactly the bivector and trivector coefficients, and leaves the scalar and vector parts alone:

  Definition c3_reverse (x : C3 F) : C3 F :=
    mkC3 (c3_s x) (c3_1 x) (c3_2 x) (c3_3 x)
      (- c3_12 x) (- c3_13 x) (- c3_23 x) (- c3_123 x).

Reversal turns a product around, xỹ=ỹx̃\widetilde{xy}=\tilde y\,\tilde x (c3_reverse_anti) — the one property that makes the next definition behave like a conjugation rather than an arbitrary sandwich. Call an element a rotor when it lies in the even subalgebra,

  Definition c3_even (x : C3 F) : Prop :=
    c3_1 x == 0 /\ c3_2 x == 0 /\ c3_3 x == 0 /\ c3_123 x == 0.

and define the sandwich:

  Definition c3_rotate (r x : C3 F) : C3 F := r * x * c3_reverse r.

Sandwiching a vector by an even rotor produces another vector — c3_rotate_vector proves this unconditionally, without requiring rr to be a unit. Read as an action on vectors, that sandwich is a rotation; what follows is what it does to rr itself.

Theorem. A rotor rr and its negation −r-r sandwich every multivector xx to the identical result.

Reversal only ever negates coefficients — it is linear — so reversing −r-r negates r̃\tilde r a second time on top of the first: −r̃=−r̃\widetilde{-r}=-\tilde r. Substituting that into the sandwich,

(−r)x(−r)̃=(−r)x(−r̃)=(−1)(−1)⋅rxr̃=rxr̃,(-r)\,x\,\widetilde{(-r)} \;=\; (-r)\,x\,(-\tilde r) \;=\; (-1)(-1)\cdot r\,x\,\tilde r \;=\; r\,x\,\tilde r,

the two minus signs produced by negating rr meet the two minus signs produced by negating r̃\tilde r and cancel outright. The Coq proof, verbatim:

  Theorem c3_rotate_neg : forall r x : C3 F, c3_rotate (- r) x == c3_rotate r x.
  Proof.
    intros r x. unfold c3_rotate.
    assert (Hrev : c3_reverse (- r) == - c3_reverse r).
    { apply c3_eq_intro. intros A.
      rewrite (c3_proj_neg (c3_reverse r) A), (c3_proj_reverse (- r) A),
        (c3_proj_reverse r A), (c3_proj_neg r A).
      exact (mul_neg_r (c3_rsign A) (c3_proj r A)). }
    rewrite Hrev.
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_l r x).
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_r (- (r * x)) (c3_reverse r)).
    rewrite (c3_neg_mul_l (r * x) (c3_reverse r)).
    exact (c3_neg_involutive (r * x * c3_reverse r)).
  Qed.

Hrev proves −r̃=−r̃\widetilde{-r}=-\tilde r coefficient by coefficient: c3_proj_neg and c3_proj_reverse unfold both sides at an arbitrary blade AA to the same product of signs, closed by one field fact, mul_neg_r. Rewriting by Hrev replaces −r̃\widetilde{-r} with −r̃-\tilde r in the goal, giving exactly the middle expression above. c3_neg_mul_l pulls the minus sign on −r-r out of the product (−r)x(-r)\,x; c3_neg_mul_r then pulls the remaining minus sign (on −r̃-\tilde r) out of the outer product, leaving −(−(rxr̃))-(-(r\,x\,\tilde r)); c3_neg_involutive cancels the double negation — the (−1)(−1)=1(-1)(-1)=1 step made explicit — and closes the goal.

Sandwiching by rr and by −r-r therefore act on every xx completely indistinguishably. Read as a statement about rotating three-dimensional vectors, this is the fact behind the rule stated without derivation in the opening section: the rotor generating a given spatial rotation is not unique, rr and −r-r both do it, so a continuous path of rotors from 11 back to a 360°360° rotation does not have to return to 11 — it can return to −1-1 instead, and only a second 360°360° turn, 720°720° total, is guaranteed to close the loop. c3_rotate_neg is that fact, proved for an arbitrary field and an arbitrary even rr, not asserted.

The Jacobi identity, checked one basis triple at a time

The bracket above is built directly from nine numbers, with no guarantee from that construction alone that it satisfies a Lie algebra’s defining laws. Bilinearity and antisymmetry follow from the table’s own antisymmetry under swapping its first two indices (bracket_add_l, bracket_add_r, bracket_antisym) for arbitrary elements, not only basis vectors. The Jacobi identity is the one law that does not fall out so directly; whether these particular nine numbers genuinely combine into a consistent Lie algebra has to be checked. Define the Jacobiator of three elements as

  Definition jacobiator (x y z : Fin.t 8 -> F) : Fin.t 8 -> F :=
    bracket x (bracket y z) + bracket y (bracket z x) + bracket z (bracket x y).

and, for basis generators, jacobi_basis_expand rewrites each double bracket using the structure constants, turning the Jacobiator’s value at output coordinate dd into a sum of three single sums, one per cyclic pair.

Theorem (jacobi_123). The Jacobiator of T1,T2,T3T_1,T_2,T_3 vanishes at every output coordinate.

On the blackboard: the only nonzero value of ff with first two indices 2,32,3 is f231=1f_{231}=1, so the first of the three sums collapses to its lone term, at b=1b=1: f231f11d=f11df_{231}\,f_{11d}=f_{11d}. The same collapse happens to the other two sums, at b=2b=2 and b=3b=3 respectively, giving f22df_{22d} and f33df_{33d}. But every structure constant with a repeated index is zero — the table’s antisymmetry under swapping its first two arguments forces faad=−faadf_{aad}=-f_{aad}, hence faad=0f_{aad}=0 — so all three terms are individually zero, and the Jacobiator vanishes at T1,T2,T3T_1,T_2,T_3 not by a cancellation among several nonzero numbers but because each term is zero on its own. The Coq proof, verbatim:

  Lemma jacobi_123 : jacobiator (e g1) (e g2) (e g3) == 0.
  Proof. su3_jacobi g1 g2 g3. Qed.

su3_jacobi case-splits on the output coordinate dd (fin8_rect, an eight-way case analysis standing in for forall d : Fin.t 8), rewrites by jacobi_basis_expand to produce the three-term sum above, unfolds the table (sc) and the numbering (gnum), and simplifies (cbn) — turning the abstract sum into the concrete numbers the table actually stores. su3_step then clears the resulting zero terms with the ring facts mul_0_l/mul_0_r and closes each of the eight resulting goals, one per output coordinate, by reflexivity.

The same check is repeated, jacobi_124 through jacobi_678, for all (83)=56\binom{8}{3}=56 strictly increasing basis triples — every one closed, none Admitted. Four of the 56 (mixing T4T_4 through T7T_7 with T8T_8) need one further fact, that (3/2)2(\sqrt3/2)^2 equals three copies of (1/2)2(1/2)^2, since 32=3\sqrt3^2=3; every other triple closes exactly like jacobi_123 does, on the table’s zeros alone.

What this does not explain

Geometric algebra does not explain the Pauli exclusion principle. The fact that two electrons cannot occupy the same quantum state — the antisymmetry of a fermion wavefunction under exchanging two particles — is a separate result, the spin-statistics theorem (Fierz 1939; Pauli, Phys. Rev. 58 (1940) 716), derived in relativistic quantum field theory from locality, Lorentz invariance, and a positive-definite energy spectrum. It has nothing to do with Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0)’s rotor structure. What this post’s algebra explains is spin’s geometry: the pseudoscalar’s role as a real, central square root of −1-1, and the double cover, rr and −r-r producing the identical rotation. It does not explain, and does not attempt to explain, why electrons exclude one another.

Using the two algebras

Both constructions are ordinary algebra, computable directly. A spin state built as ρR\sqrt\rho\,R is arithmetic in the eight real coordinates of Cl(3,0)\mathrm{Cl}(3,0) — no complex matrices, and every operation above (c3_rotate, c3_reverse, the double cover) computes the same way for any field, not only the reals. A color-charge computation is arithmetic in the eight real coordinates of 𝔰𝔲(3)\mathfrak{su}(3): given any two color states as vectors in Fin.t 8 -> F, bracket returns their commutator’s eight coordinates directly from the nine structure constants — a Lie bracket checked against its own defining laws, not assumed to satisfy them.
